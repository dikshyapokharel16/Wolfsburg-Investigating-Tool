# Intersection Design Reference

Comprehensive intersection design guidance covering geometry, signal timing, pedestrian crossings, bicycle treatments, roundabouts, accessibility requirements, and design vehicle considerations. Based on NACTO, AASHTO, FHWA MUTCD, ADA, and international best practices.

---

## Intersection Design Philosophy

Intersections are the most critical locations in any street network. They are where most crashes occur, where pedestrians are most vulnerable, and where the competing demands of all modes converge. The overarching design philosophy is:

1. **Minimize conflict points**: Reduce the number of locations where paths of different users cross
2. **Minimize crossing distance**: Shorter crossings mean less pedestrian exposure time
3. **Minimize vehicle speed**: Slower turning vehicles are less likely to cause fatal injuries
4. **Maximize visibility**: All users must see and be seen
5. **Maximize predictability**: Clear geometry, markings, and signals so all users understand right-of-way

## Intersection Types

### Signalized Intersections

Signalized intersections use traffic signals to assign right-of-way to conflicting movements in sequence. They are appropriate where traffic volumes, pedestrian volumes, or crash history warrant signal control.

**Signal Warrants** (simplified from MUTCD):
- Minimum vehicular volume: 300+ vehicles/hour on major street and 200+ on minor for 8+ hours
- Pedestrian volume: 100+ pedestrians/hour for 4+ hours crossing major street
- Crash experience: 5+ correctable crashes in 12 months
- School crossing: elementary school with inadequate gaps in traffic
- Coordinated signal system: needed for progression on arterial

**Geometry Standards**:
- Approach lane width: 3.0-3.3m
- Left-turn lane: 3.0m wide, 30-45m storage length (based on queue analysis)
- Right-turn lane: 2.7-3.0m wide, 30-45m taper and storage
- Stop bar setback: 1.2m minimum from crosswalk, 4.0m where bike box present
- Crosswalk location: set perpendicular to curb line, aligned with pedestrian desire line
- Curb return radius: 3.0-4.5m standard urban (see corner radius section below)
- Channelized right-turn islands: discouraged in urban areas; where present, yield-controlled with raised crosswalk

**Signal Timing for Pedestrians**:
- Walk phase (steady walk signal): 7 seconds minimum
- Pedestrian clearance interval: crossing distance / walking speed
  - Standard walking speed: 1.2 m/s
  - School zones or senior areas: 1.0 m/s
  - Conservative / accessible design: 0.9 m/s
- Clearance interval formula: (Crossing distance in meters) / (Walking speed in m/s) = seconds
- Example: 15m crossing / 1.2 m/s = 12.5 seconds clearance, round up to 13 seconds
- Total pedestrian phase: Walk (7s) + Clearance (13s) = 20 seconds minimum
- Maximum cycle length: 90-120 seconds urban (longer cycles increase pedestrian delay and non-compliance)
- Maximum pedestrian wait time: 60-90 seconds (consider pedestrian recall or shorter cycle)

**Leading Pedestrian Interval (LPI)**:
- Duration: 3-7 seconds
- Function: pedestrians get walk signal before parallel vehicle green, establishing presence in crosswalk
- Application: all signalized intersections with significant pedestrian volumes (NACTO recommends universally)
- Crash reduction: 13% reduction in pedestrian-vehicle crashes at signalized intersections (FHWA)

**Signal Phasing Concepts**:
- Protected left turns: separate left-turn phase with green arrow (safer but less capacity)
- Permitted left turns: left-turning vehicles yield to oncoming traffic (higher conflict)
- Protected-permitted: protected phase followed by permitted (compromise)
- Lagging left turns: left-turn phase after through phase (allows pedestrians to clear first)
- Exclusive pedestrian phase (scramble): all vehicles stopped, all pedestrian movements including diagonal permitted

**Level of Service (LOS) Basics**:
- LOS A: delay <10 seconds (free flow, ideal)
- LOS B: delay 10-20 seconds (stable flow)
- LOS C: delay 20-35 seconds (stable, acceptable urban)
- LOS D: delay 35-55 seconds (approaching unstable, acceptable in urban peak)
- LOS E: delay 55-80 seconds (unstable, at capacity)
- LOS F: delay >80 seconds (forced flow, over capacity)
- Target: LOS D or better for vehicles, LOS C or better for pedestrians (measure both)
- Note: NACTO and progressive agencies increasingly reject vehicle LOS as the sole measure, preferring multi-modal LOS and person-throughput metrics

### Roundabouts

Roundabouts replace signalized or stop-controlled intersections with a circular roadway around a central island. Vehicles yield on entry and circulate counterclockwise (in right-hand traffic countries). Roundabouts eliminate left-turn and head-on conflicts and reduce approach speeds, resulting in dramatically fewer severe crashes.

**Types and Sizing**:

| Type | Inscribed Circle Diameter | Entry Width | Circulating Width | Capacity (ADT) | Speed |
|------|--------------------------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Mini-roundabout | 13-25m | 4.0-5.0m (single lane) | 4.5-5.5m | <15,000 | 15-25 km/h |
| Single-lane | 25-30m | 4.0-5.5m | 5.0-6.0m | 15,000-25,000 | 25-30 km/h |
| Multi-lane (2 lanes) | 45-55m | 7.0-9.0m (two lanes) | 9.0-11.0m | 25,000-45,000 | 30-40 km/h |
| Multi-lane (3 lanes) | 55-70m | 10.0-13.0m (three lanes) | 13.0-15.0m | 40,000-60,000 | 30-40 km/h |

**Design Elements**:
- **Central island**: landscaped, not traversable (except truck apron on mini-roundabouts); minimum 4.0m diameter for mini, larger for standard
- **Truck apron**: mountable paved area around central island on mini-roundabouts (2.0-4.0m wide, 25-50mm raised, textured surface); allows trucks to track over while discouraging cars
- **Splitter islands**: raised islands on each approach separating entry and exit lanes; minimum 15m long, 1.8m wide at crosswalk, tapered to 0.6m at ends; provide pedestrian refuge and deflect entering vehicles
- **Entry deflection**: approaching vehicles must deflect around splitter island and central island; ensures speed reduction; minimum deflection angle 30 degrees
- **Approach curvature**: entry path radius 20-30m for single-lane, varies for multi-lane; tighter curves = lower speeds = safer

**Pedestrian Crossings at Roundabouts**:
- Located one car length (6.0m) back from yield line on each approach
- Set on the splitter island, which provides pedestrian refuge
- Single-lane roundabout: one-stage crossing (yielding vehicles on entry, then on exit)
- Multi-lane roundabout: two-stage crossing with refuge on splitter island
- Crosswalk width: 3.0m minimum at roundabouts
- Signalized crossings may be needed at multi-lane roundabouts with high pedestrian volumes

**Bicycle Treatment at Roundabouts**:
- Single-lane roundabout: cyclists may share the circulating lane (if speed <30 km/h) or use a separated cycle track around the perimeter
- Multi-lane roundabout: separated cycle track required (cyclists should never share multi-lane roundabout)
- Cycle track set back from circulatory roadway by 3.0-5.0m
- Crossings for cyclists adjacent to (but separate from) pedestrian crossings

**Safety Performance**:
- Single-lane roundabout vs. signalized intersection: 78% reduction in fatal/serious injury crashes
- Roundabout vs. stop-controlled intersection: 82% reduction in fatal/serious injury crashes
- Pedestrian crashes at roundabouts: 40% fewer than at signalized intersections (IIHS data)
- Speed at conflict points: 20-30 km/h (vs. 40-60 km/h at signalized intersections)

### Stop-Controlled Intersections

**All-Way Stop Warrants** (simplified from MUTCD):
- Total entering volume >500 vehicles/hour for 8+ hours
- Combined vehicular and pedestrian volume on minor street >200/hour for 8+ hours
- Crash history: 5+ crashes in 12 months susceptible to correction by all-way stop
- Interim measure: pending signal installation or roundabout construction

**Two-Way Stop (minor street stops)**:
- Standard for local-to-local and local-to-collector intersections
- Sight distance: approaching vehicle must be visible from stopped position at a distance corresponding to design speed of major road
  - 30 km/h: 45m sight distance
  - 40 km/h: 70m sight distance
  - 50 km/h: 100m sight distance
  - 60 km/h: 135m sight distance
- Stop bar: 1.2m from crosswalk (or 3.6m from edge of intersecting travel lane if no crosswalk)

**Geometry**:
- Keep intersections compact (reduce curb radii to minimum)
- Align crosswalks with pedestrian desire lines
- Consider mini-roundabout as superior alternative for all-way stops

### Raised Intersections

A raised intersection elevates the entire intersection area to sidewalk level. Vehicles ramp up on approach and ramp down on exit, naturally reducing speeds. The intersection surface is flush with the surrounding sidewalk, providing a universal accessibility crossing in all directions.

**Dimensional Standards**:
- Raise height: 75-150mm above approach roadway level (flush with adjacent sidewalk)
- Approach ramp slope: 1:15 to 1:25 (gentler = more comfortable, especially for buses)
- Ramp length: 1.5-3.0m depending on slope and height
- Platform (flat top): entire intersection area at raised level
- Surface: distinct from roadway (unit pavers, colored concrete, or textured surface)

**Applications**:
- School zones
- Neighborhood gateways
- Pedestrian priority areas
- Where two local streets intersect
- Streets adjacent to parks, plazas, or civic buildings

**Design Notes**:
- Drainage: gutter at base of approach ramp captures runoff before it reaches raised surface
- Fire/emergency: gentle ramps are negotiable by emergency vehicles at appropriate speed
- Buses: avoid on frequent bus routes unless ramp is very gentle (1:20 or flatter)
- Maintenance: snowplow-compatible if ramps are gradual and surface is smooth

---

### Shared Space Intersections

Shared space intersections remove conventional traffic control devices (signals, signs, lane markings) and rely on low speeds, eye contact, and social negotiation between road users.

**Applicability Criteria**:
- Total entering volume: <15,000 ADT (all approaches combined)
- Design speed: <30 km/h (preferably <20 km/h)
- Pedestrian volumes: moderate to high (the more pedestrians, the more effective the social negotiation)
- Context: urban, mixed-use, historic, or residential

**Design Elements**:
- Flush surface throughout (no curbs, no level changes)
- No traffic signals, no stop signs, no lane markings
- Subtle grade changes, surface texture, and furnishing to guide movement
- Generous pedestrian space surrounding the shared zone
- Bollards or planters at perimeter to prevent high-speed entry
- Accessible design: detectable edges (tactile paving, contrasting materials) for visually impaired users

**Performance**:
- Speeds: typically 15-20 km/h
- Crashes: reduced severity due to low speeds
- Capacity: self-regulating, lower peak throughput but more consistent flow
- Place quality: dramatically improved for pedestrians and social activity

## Pedestrian Crossing Design

### Crossing Types

**Marked Crosswalk (Standard)**
- Two parallel white lines, 150-300mm wide, 1.8-3.6m apart
- Application: every intersection in urban areas, mid-block where warranted
- Adequate for: <6,000 ADT, speeds <50 km/h, <3 lanes (with refuge for wider crossings)

**High-Visibility Continental Crosswalk**
- Longitudinal white bars (ladder pattern), 300-600mm wide, 300-600mm gaps
- Much more visible to approaching drivers than standard parallel lines
- Recommended as default for all urban crosswalks by NACTO and FHWA
- Required for: arterials, school zones, transit stops, mid-block crossings

**Raised Crosswalk (Speed Table Crossing)**
- Full crosswalk width raised 75-100mm above roadway, flush with sidewalk
- Approach ramps: 1:15 to 1:25 slope
- Platform length: 3.0-6.0m (at least full crosswalk width)
- Application: school zones, pedestrian priority streets, neighborhood intersections
- Speed reduction effect: 10-15 km/h reduction at crossing point

**Signalized Crosswalk**
- Full traffic signal with pedestrian signal heads (walk/don't walk)
- Push-button or automatic pedestrian detection
- Application: all signalized intersections, high-volume mid-block crossings
- Minimum walk phase: 7 seconds
- Clearance: crossing distance / 1.2 m/s (or 1.0 m/s in school/senior zones)

**HAWK / Pedestrian Hybrid Beacon (PHB)**
- Overhead beacon, dark until pedestrian activates
- Sequence: flashing yellow (warning) -> solid yellow (prepare to stop) -> solid red (stop for pedestrian) -> flashing red (proceed if clear)
- Application: mid-block crossings on arterials, high-speed roads where full signal is not warranted
- Crash reduction: 69% reduction in pedestrian crashes (FHWA study)
- Minimum spacing from nearest signal: 90m

**Pedestrian Scramble (Barnes Dance)**
- Exclusive all-walk phase where all vehicular traffic is stopped
- Pedestrians may cross in any direction, including diagonally
- Application: high-pedestrian-volume intersections, CBDs, transit stations
- Phase length: 15-25 seconds depending on diagonal crossing distance
- Diagonal crossing distance: approximately 1.4x the longer crosswalk
- Advantages: eliminates turning-vehicle conflicts with pedestrians
- Disadvantages: longer cycle length, increased vehicular delay

### Crossing Width Standards

| Context | Minimum Width | Standard Width | High-Volume Width |
|---------|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Residential | 1.8m | 2.4m | 3.0m |
| Collector / Commercial | 2.4m | 3.0m | 3.6m |
| Arterial | 3.0m | 3.6m | 4.5m |
| Transit station / Arena | 3.6m | 4.5m | 6.0m+ |
| School zone | 3.0m | 3.6m | 4.5m |

Rule: crosswalk width should be at least as wide as the approaching sidewalk.

### Pedestrian Refuge Islands

Refuge islands allow pedestrians to cross in two stages, waiting safely in the median for a gap or signal in the second half of the roadway.

**When Required**:
- Crossing distance >3 travel lanes (single stage)
- Total crossing distance >10m without signal
- Speeds >40 km/h
- NACTO recommends refuge at any crossing of 4+ lanes

**Dimensional Standards**:
- Width (perpendicular to crossing): 1.8m absolute minimum, 2.4m recommended, 3.0m preferred
- Width must accommodate: wheelchair (0.75m) + companion (0.75m) + clear space = 1.8m minimum
- Length (parallel to crossing): minimum equal to crosswalk width, preferably 6.0m+
- Nose: rounded or pointed with bollards/reflectors for visibility, angled to direct pedestrians to face oncoming traffic
- Cut-through: at-grade or ramped, 1.5m minimum clear width, detectable warning surfaces at both edges
- Offset: where refuge is long enough, offset the pedestrian path so pedestrians face oncoming traffic in the second stage (Z-crossing or staggered crossing)

### Curb Ramps (ADA Requirements)

Every pedestrian crossing must have curb ramps on both sides that comply with ADA standards:

- **Width**: 1.2m minimum (exclusive of flares), match crosswalk width where possible
- **Running slope**: 8.3% maximum (1:12), 6.0-7.0% recommended
- **Cross slope**: 2.0% maximum
- **Landing**: 1.2m x 1.2m minimum level landing at top (within sidewalk) and at bottom (within roadway, ideally behind gutter line)
- **Flares**: where ramp is adjacent to walkway, flared sides at 10% maximum slope
- **Detectable warning surface**: contrasting truncated dome surface, 600mm deep in direction of travel, full width of ramp, at the bottom where ramp meets roadway
- **Alignment**: ramp must be aligned with crosswalk and perpendicular to curb line; one ramp per crosswalk (not shared diagonal ramps)
- **Gutter slope**: 5% maximum at gutter pan where it meets ramp bottom

**Preferred Configuration**: directional curb ramps (one ramp per crosswalk) rather than diagonal ramps (single ramp at corner apex pointing into intersection). Directional ramps align pedestrians with the crosswalk and are unambiguous for visually impaired users.

## Corner Radius Guidelines

Corner radius directly controls the speed of right-turning vehicles and the pedestrian crossing distance. Smaller radii = slower turns = safer pedestrians = shorter crossings.

### Effective vs. Actual Radius

The **actual curb radius** is the physical radius of the curb arc at the corner. The **effective turning radius** is the actual path a vehicle traces, which is larger than the curb radius when the vehicle starts from a parking lane, bike lane, or wider lane. This means a tight physical curb radius (e.g., 3.0m) can accommodate large vehicles when parking lanes or bike lanes provide additional clearance.

**Example**: 3.0m physical curb radius + 2.4m parking lane on the departure street = approximately 7.5m effective turning radius, sufficient for a standard bus (SU-30 or BUS design vehicle).

### Recommended Curb Radii by Context

| Context | Physical Curb Radius | Effective Radius (with parking/bike buffer) | Design Vehicle |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------------------------------|----------------|
| Tight urban, no buses | 1.5-3.0m | 4.5-7.5m | P (passenger car) |
| Standard urban | 3.0-4.5m | 6.0-9.0m | SU-30 (single-unit truck) |
| Urban with buses | 4.5-6.0m | 7.5-10.5m | BUS, SU-40 |
| Urban with articulated buses | 6.0-7.5m | 9.0-12.0m | A-BUS (articulated) |
| Suburban | 6.0-9.0m | 9.0-12.0m | SU-40 |
| With frequent WB-50 trucks | 9.0-10.5m | 12.0-15.0m | WB-50 |
| With frequent WB-67 trucks | 10.5-12.0m | 15.0-18.0m | WB-67 |
| Industrial / freight | 12.0-15.0m | 15.0-21.0m | WB-67 + |

### Strategies for Tight Radii with Large Vehicles

1. **Recessed stop bar**: set stop bar on cross street back 2.4-4.5m from crosswalk to give turning vehicle room to encroach into oncoming lane of cross street (acceptable for low-frequency large vehicle turns)
2. **Mountable truck apron**: paved area beyond the curb radius, raised 25-50mm above roadway, textured surface; cars stay on the roadway but trucks can mount the apron for the wider turn
3. **Compound curves**: combination of tighter curve at the pedestrian crossing point and wider curve further from the corner
4. **Curb extension (bulb-out)**: narrows the street to reduce crossing distance; the effective radius is controlled by the travel lane edge, not the physical curb (allows very tight 1.5-3.0m curb radius)
5. **Right-turn slip lane removal**: eliminate channelized right-turn islands and replace with tight corner; reduces pedestrian exposure, slows turns, reclaims space for sidewalk or plaza

## Bicycle Intersection Treatments

### Bike Boxes (Advanced Stop Lines)

A colored pavement area at the head of a traffic lane at a signalized intersection, providing cyclists with a safe, visible space to wait ahead of motor vehicles during the red phase.

**Dimensions**:
- Depth: 3.0-5.0m (from stop bar to vehicle stop line)
- Width: full lane width or multiple lanes (typically extending across right-turn lane and adjacent through lane)
- Ingress lane: approach bike lane continues to the box; 1.5m minimum width
- Surface: colored (green) with white bicycle stencil

**Application**: signalized intersections where cyclists need to make left turns from a right-side bike lane, or where right-turn conflicts are common.

### Two-Stage Turn Queue Boxes

A designated area for cyclists to reposition for a left turn in two stages: (1) proceed straight through the intersection in the bike lane, (2) stop in a queue box on the far side facing the cross street, then proceed on the next green.

**Dimensions**:
- Queue box: 1.5m wide x 2.0m deep minimum
- Located in the protected area between the crosswalk and the travel lane on the far side of the intersection
- Surface: colored (green) with turn arrow stencil
- Signage: "TWO STAGE TURN" or equivalent

**Application**: any intersection where a protected bike lane on the right side needs to access a left-turn movement.

### Protected Intersections (Dutch-Style)

The protected intersection maintains physical separation between cyclists and motor vehicles through the entire intersection, not just along the block. Corner refuge islands, setback crossings, and separate signal phases create a high-comfort, high-safety cycling environment.

**Key Elements**:
- **Corner refuge islands**: triangular or curved islands at each corner, 3.0-5.0m from the curb line, separating the cycle track from the right-turning vehicle path
- **Forward stop bar**: cyclists stop ahead of vehicles, increasing visibility
- **Setback crossing**: cycle track crossing is set back 2.0-6.0m from the parallel vehicle lane, so right-turning vehicles negotiate the cycle crossing at a near-perpendicular angle (easier to see cyclist)
- **Separate signal phase** (optional): dedicated bicycle signal phase with green bike symbol
- **Bend-out**: the cycle track bends away from the roadway as it approaches the intersection, creating space for the corner island

**Dimensions**:
- Corner island: minimum 2.0m wide at narrowest point
- Cycle track through intersection: 1.8-2.5m one-way, 3.0m two-way
- Setback distance: 2.0-6.0m (measured from near edge of parallel vehicle travel lane to near edge of cycle crossing)

**Application**: best practice for any intersection where a protected bike lane crosses a street with >6,000 ADT or speeds >40 km/h.

### Mixing Zones

Where a right-turning motor vehicle lane and a bike lane must merge before an intersection, a mixing zone provides a defined area for this conflict.

**Design Principles**:
- Bike lane markings (solid line) transition to dashed line at the start of the mixing zone
- Green colored pavement through the mixing zone
- Length: 15-30m (enough for speed adjustment and merging)
- Signage: "YIELD TO BIKES" or equivalent for motor vehicles
- Note: mixing zones are a compromise; protected intersections are preferred wherever feasible

## ADA / Accessibility Requirements

All intersection designs must comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Accessibility Guidelines (ADAAG) and/or equivalent local accessibility standards. The following requirements apply universally.

### Curb Ramps
- Present at every pedestrian crossing on every corner
- Directional (one per crosswalk, aligned with crosswalk direction)
- Maximum running slope: 8.3% (1:12)
- Maximum cross slope: 2.0%
- Minimum width: 1.2m (exclusive of flares)
- Level landing at top: 1.2m x 1.2m minimum
- Detectable warning surface (truncated domes): 600mm deep, full width of ramp, contrasting color

### Crosswalks
- Maximum cross slope: 2.0%
- Maximum running slope: 5.0% (matching roadway grade where necessary)
- Minimum clear width: 1.5m (between marked lines)
- Smooth, firm, stable, slip-resistant surface
- Grade breaks at gutter: maximum 13.3% algebraic grade change (smooth transition preferred)

### Pedestrian Signals
- Accessible Pedestrian Signals (APS) required at all signalized crossings
- Audible walk indication: locator tone, walk indication tone, and/or speech message
- Vibrotactile walk indication: vibrating push button during walk phase
- Push button: 1.0-1.2m above grade, within 1.5m of curb, within 3.0m of crosswalk edge
- Push button should be on a pole at the edge of the crosswalk, oriented to face the direction of crossing
- Automatic pedestrian recall (no push button needed) recommended for all urban intersections

### Pedestrian Refuge Islands
- Cut-through: at-grade passage minimum 1.5m wide, 2.0m preferred
- Detectable warning surfaces at both edges of the cut-through (where pedestrian path meets roadway)
- Level surface (maximum 2% cross slope) within refuge
- If ramped (raised refuge): maximum 8.3% ramp slope, level landing 1.2m at top

### Crossing Time
- Walking speed for clearance calculation: 1.0 m/s where elderly or disabled populations are significant (standard is 1.2 m/s)
- Minimum walk phase: 7 seconds
- Ensure adequate total crossing time for slowest expected pedestrians

### Detectable Warning Surfaces (Truncated Domes)
- Required at: all curb ramps where they meet roadway, all pedestrian refuge island edges, all transit platform edges, all shared-use path roadway crossings
- Dome dimensions: 23mm diameter base, 13mm diameter top, 5mm height (per ADA)
- Spacing: 41-61mm center-to-center (inline pattern)
- Contrast: visually contrasting with adjacent surface (typically bright yellow on gray concrete)
- Material: cast-in-place, applied tile, or stamped into wet concrete; must be durable and slip-resistant

### General Intersection Accessibility
- No gratings, utility covers, or gaps in pedestrian path wider than 13mm (perpendicular to travel) or 6mm (parallel to travel)
- All control devices (push buttons, signal poles) accessible from a level area on the sidewalk
- Protruding objects: nothing may protrude more than 100mm into pedestrian path between 685mm and 2030mm above grade (cane-detectable zone)
- Changes in level: up to 6mm vertical, 6-13mm beveled at 1:2, >13mm treated as ramp

## Design Vehicle Reference

Intersections must accommodate the design vehicle -- the largest vehicle expected to regularly use the intersection. Choice of design vehicle directly determines curb radius, lane width, and intersection geometry.

| Design Vehicle | Width | Length | Turning Radius (outer) | Application |
|---------------|-------|--------|----------------------|-------------|
| Passenger car (P) | 2.1m | 5.8m | 7.3m | Residential, local streets |
| Single-unit truck (SU-30) | 2.6m | 9.1m | 12.8m | Standard urban |
| Single-unit truck (SU-40) | 2.6m | 12.2m | 15.2m | Collectors, commercial streets |
| City bus (BUS) | 2.6m | 12.2m | 12.8m | Any transit route |
| Articulated bus (A-BUS) | 2.6m | 18.3m | 13.7m | BRT, express routes |
| WB-50 semi-trailer | 2.6m | 16.7m | 13.7m | Collectors, minor arterials |
| WB-67 semi-trailer | 2.6m | 22.4m | 13.7m | Arterials, freight routes |
| Fire truck (aerial ladder) | 2.6m | 14.3m | 12.2m | All streets (emergency access) |

**Design Vehicle Selection Guidance**:
- Default urban: SU-30 or BUS (accommodate with tight curb radius + mountable truck apron for occasional larger vehicles)
- Arterials: WB-50 or WB-67
- Industrial/freight: WB-67
- Residential: P (passenger car) with occasional SU-30 (moving trucks, garbage trucks)
- NACTO recommendation: design for the most frequent large vehicle (typically bus or SU-30), accommodate the occasional largest vehicle (WB-67) through encroachment into opposing lane or mountable apron

## Sight Distance at Intersections

### Intersection Sight Distance (ISD)

Drivers approaching or stopped at an intersection must be able to see conflicting vehicles in time to avoid a collision.

**Sight Triangle** (clear of obstructions between 0.6m and 2.4m above grade):

| Major Road Speed | Sight Distance Along Major Road (from minor road stop bar) |
|-----------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| 30 km/h | 45m |
| 40 km/h | 70m |
| 50 km/h | 100m |
| 60 km/h | 135m |
| 70 km/h | 175m |
| 80 km/h | 215m |

**Obstructions to Remove from Sight Triangle**:
- Signs, fences, walls above 0.6m
- Parked vehicles within sight triangle (no parking zones at corners)
- Dense vegetation (hedges, shrubs above 0.6m)
- Utility boxes, newspaper racks, mailboxes
- Note: street trees with clear trunk to 2.0m generally do not obstruct sight triangles

**No-Parking Zone at Corners**:
- Minimum 6.0m from crosswalk (many jurisdictions use 6-10m)
- Provides sight distance for pedestrians crossing and for turning vehicles
- Also provides space for curb extensions (bulb-outs)

## Quick Reference: Intersection Design Checklist

Use this checklist when designing or reviewing any intersection:

### Geometry
- [ ] Curb radii minimized for context (see corner radius table)
- [ ] All travel lanes 3.0-3.3m (no wider)
- [ ] Turn lanes only where warranted by volume analysis
- [ ] Curb extensions (bulb-outs) at all corners where parking is present
- [ ] Sight triangles clear of obstructions

### Pedestrian
- [ ] Crosswalks on all legs of intersection (do not skip any leg)
- [ ] Crosswalk width matches or exceeds approaching sidewalk width
- [ ] High-visibility continental markings (not just parallel lines)
- [ ] Curb ramps at every corner, directional, ADA-compliant
- [ ] Detectable warning surfaces at all curb ramps
- [ ] Pedestrian signal heads on all signalized crossings
- [ ] Accessible pedestrian signals (audible/vibrotactile)
- [ ] Leading pedestrian interval (3-7 seconds)
- [ ] Pedestrian refuge islands where crossing >3 lanes or >10m
- [ ] Crossing time calculated at 1.2 m/s (1.0 m/s for school/senior zones)
- [ ] Maximum pedestrian wait time <90 seconds

### Bicycle
- [ ] Bike facility continuous through intersection (no gaps)
- [ ] Protected intersection elements where feasible (corner islands, setback crossings)
- [ ] Bike boxes or two-stage turn queues where needed
- [ ] Green colored pavement at conflict zones
- [ ] Separate signal phase for bicycles where warranted

### Transit
- [ ] Bus stop located to minimize delay (far-side preferred)
- [ ] Transit signal priority where applicable
- [ ] Level boarding platform at stops near intersection
- [ ] No right-turn-on-red where bus stop is near-side

### Safety
- [ ] Design speed through intersection <30 km/h
- [ ] No channelized right-turn islands (or redesigned as tight yield with raised crosswalk)
- [ ] No skewed intersections (realign to 90 degrees where possible)
- [ ] Lighting: 1.5x approach road illuminance
- [ ] No visual clutter (minimize signs, consolidate poles)
