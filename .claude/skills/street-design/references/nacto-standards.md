# NACTO Standards Reference

Comprehensive summary of the National Association of City Transportation Officials (NACTO) Urban Street Design Guide, Transit Street Design Guide, and Urban Bikeway Design Guide. This reference distills the key dimensional standards, design principles, and selection criteria used in contemporary urban street design practice.

---

## Core NACTO Principles

### 1. Design for the Target Speed, Not the Prevailing Speed
Streets should be designed so that the geometry, lane widths, and visual cues naturally limit drivers to the intended operating speed. Do not use observed 85th-percentile speed as the design speed; instead, set the target speed first and design the street to enforce it. Narrow lanes, tight radii, street trees, and on-street parking all reduce operating speeds.

### 2. Streets Are Public Spaces
Streets are not merely conduits for vehicle movement. They are the largest component of public space in any city, comprising 25-35% of urban land area. Street design must balance movement with placemaking, social interaction, commerce, recreation, and ecological function.

### 3. Measure Success by Throughput of People, Not Vehicles
A street's performance should be evaluated by the number of people it moves per hour across all modes (walking, cycling, transit, driving), not by vehicle level-of-service alone. A bus lane carrying 800 passengers/hour outperforms a general travel lane carrying 600-900 vehicles with 1.1 passengers each.

### 4. Context-Sensitive Design
Street design must respond to adjacent land use, activity, and community needs. The same ROW width may produce very different cross-sections in a commercial district versus a residential neighborhood. There is no one-size-fits-all street.

### 5. Safety Is the Primary Design Objective
Every design decision should reduce the likelihood and severity of crashes. This means slower vehicle speeds, shorter crossing distances, clear sight lines, and physical separation between modes where speed differentials exceed 15 km/h.

## NACTO Urban Street Types

### Boulevard

**Typical ROW**: 30-50m
**Lanes**: 2-3 per direction, divided by a landscaped or transit median
**Design Speed**: 40-50 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- Grand, tree-lined divided roadway
- Wide sidewalks (4.5-6.0m) with active frontage zone
- Landscaped median 3.6-6.0m wide, may accommodate center-running transit
- Protected bicycle facilities on both sides
- On-street parallel parking on both sides (or curbside flex zone)
- Double or triple row of street trees creating a canopy
- Major crossings signalized with pedestrian refuge in median
- Suitable for high-volume, multi-modal corridors with civic significance

**Recommended Elements**:
- Protected bike lanes (2.0-2.5m one-way)
- Dedicated bus lanes or center-running BRT/LRT
- Continuous tree canopy (target >60% coverage at maturity)
- Enhanced pedestrian lighting (15-20 lux on sidewalks)
- Formal street furniture (benches, bollards, wayfinding)
- Green infrastructure in median and furnishing zones

**Precedent Streets**: Champs-Elysees (Paris), Paseo de la Reforma (Mexico City), Commonwealth Avenue (Boston), Unter den Linden (Berlin), La Rambla (Barcelona)

---

### Avenue

**Typical ROW**: 25-35m
**Lanes**: 1-2 per direction, may or may not have median
**Design Speed**: 35-40 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- Tree-lined street connecting neighborhoods and districts
- Moderate traffic volumes balanced with pedestrian activity
- Sidewalks 3.6-4.8m with furnishing zone
- Optional median 1.8-3.6m (may be intermittent for turn pockets)
- Bike lanes (protected or buffered)
- On-street parking one or both sides
- Regular intersection spacing (100-200m in urban grid)
- Mixed retail and residential frontage

**Recommended Elements**:
- Buffered or protected bike lanes
- Transit stops with shelters every 300-400m
- Single row of large-canopy street trees at 7.5-9.0m spacing
- Curb extensions at intersections
- Raised crossings at key pedestrian desire lines

**Precedent Streets**: Park Avenue (New York), Kurfurstendamm (Berlin), St Kilda Road (Melbourne)

---

### Main Street / Commercial Street

**Typical ROW**: 20-27m
**Lanes**: 1 per direction, often with center turn lane or parking on both sides
**Design Speed**: 25-30 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- Continuous active retail frontage at ground level
- High pedestrian activity, heavy foot traffic
- Wide sidewalks with furnishing zone for outdoor dining, displays
- Frequent mid-block crossings and short block lengths
- On-street parallel parking providing curbside buffer and customer access
- May be candidates for pedestrianization or shared street treatment
- Lighting emphasizes pedestrian realm (warm, frequent, low-mount)
- Gateway treatments and identity signage

**Recommended Elements**:
- Sidewalks 3.6-4.8m minimum (wider preferred)
- Continuous retail frontage zone for awnings, signage, displays
- On-street parallel parking both sides (flex zones for loading AM, parking midday, dining PM)
- Narrow travel lanes (3.0m) to slow traffic
- Enhanced crosswalks (raised, patterned, or high-visibility) at every intersection
- Street trees at 6.0-7.5m spacing with large canopy species
- Pedestrian-scale lighting at 4.0-5.0m height, 3000K warm white
- Bicycle parking corrals (in former parking stalls)
- No right-turn-on-red

**Precedent Streets**: Broadway in Portland, High Street Oxford, Main Street (traditional American small town typology)

---

### Neighborhood Street

**Typical ROW**: 15-22m
**Lanes**: 1 per direction
**Design Speed**: 25-30 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- Residential character with strong tree canopy
- Low traffic volumes, primarily local access
- Parallel parking one or both sides
- Standard sidewalks 1.8-2.4m
- Children playing, dog walking, neighbor interaction
- Traffic calming through geometry (chicanes, neckdowns) rather than signs
- Quiet, green, walkable environment

**Recommended Elements**:
- Travel lanes 2.7-3.0m (narrower to slow speeds)
- Parallel parking one side minimum (alternating sides for chicane effect)
- Continuous tree canopy both sides at 7.5-9.0m spacing
- Curb extensions at intersections
- Stop signs or mini-roundabouts at cross-streets
- Shared lane for cycling (sharrows) or advisory bike lanes
- Pedestrian-scale lighting at 4.0-5.0m height

**Precedent Streets**: Typical residential streets in Portland, Amsterdam, Melbourne inner suburbs

---

### Yield Street

**Typical ROW**: 12-18m
**Lanes**: Single shared travel lane (bidirectional), one or both sides parking
**Design Speed**: 20-25 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- Too narrow for two cars to pass simultaneously
- Drivers must yield to oncoming traffic using gaps in parking
- Inherently slow-speed due to yielding behavior
- Common in older neighborhoods with narrow ROW
- Sidewalks on both sides (may be narrow, 1.5-1.8m)

**Recommended Elements**:
- Single travel lane 3.6-5.0m (two-way)
- Parallel parking one or both sides providing yielding bays
- Street trees in furnishing zone (may require structural soil beneath parking)
- Continuous sidewalks with curb ramps at all crossings
- Speed limit 20-25 km/h posted and enforced through design

---

### Shared Street / Woonerf

**Typical ROW**: 8-15m
**Lanes**: No defined lanes, single surface
**Design Speed**: 10-20 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- No curbs, flush surface throughout
- Pedestrians have legal and practical priority
- Vehicles permitted but must travel at walking speed
- Textured paving (unit pavers, cobblestone, or stamped concrete)
- Bollards, planters, and furniture define edges and slow vehicles
- Social space: play, gathering, markets, events
- Emergency vehicle access maintained
- Works best with very low ADT (<1,000) and short block lengths

**Recommended Elements**:
- Flush, level surface (no curbs, no grade changes except at drainage)
- High-quality unit paving (permeable where feasible)
- Movable and fixed planters defining vehicle path (sinuous, not straight)
- Bollards at entries (retractable for emergency access)
- Seating, play elements, public art integrated into design
- Drainage via gentle cross-slope to linear drain or permeable surface
- Trees in large above-grade planters or in-ground with structural cells

**Precedent Streets**: Dutch woonerven, Exhibition Road (London), Bell Street Park (Seattle), Argyle Street (Chicago)

---

### Residential Shared Street

**Typical ROW**: 8-12m
**Lanes**: No defined lanes
**Design Speed**: 10-15 km/h
**Key Characteristics**:
- Variation of shared street specifically for low-density residential contexts
- Driveway access to adjacent properties
- Very low traffic (<300 ADT)
- Primarily serves as neighborhood social space
- Parking integrated into landscape bays
- No through-traffic (dead-end or loop configuration common)

## Design Elements Reference

### Sidewalks

Sidewalks consist of distinct functional zones, each with specific dimensional requirements:

**Frontage Zone**
- Width: 0.3-0.9m (up to 2.4m where outdoor dining or retail display occurs)
- Function: transition from building face to public walkway, accommodates door swings, architectural features, window shopping
- Surface: may match building material or standard sidewalk
- Obstructions: none that reduce pedestrian clear zone

**Pedestrian Through Zone (Clear Zone)**
- Width: 1.8m absolute minimum (ADA), 2.4m residential recommended, 3.0-3.6m commercial recommended, 4.5m+ high-volume areas
- Function: unobstructed, continuous walking path
- Surface: smooth, firm, stable, slip-resistant; cast-in-place concrete preferred; maximum cross-slope 2%
- Obstructions: absolutely none permitted in this zone (no signs, poles, trees, furniture, merchandise, sandwich boards)

**Furnishing / Amenity Zone**
- Width: 0.6m minimum, 1.2-1.8m recommended
- Function: accommodates all non-walking elements -- trees, lights, signs, parking meters, hydrants, utility access, benches, trash cans, bike racks, newspaper boxes, transit shelters
- Surface: concrete, unit pavers, tree grates, planting, permeable paving
- Also serves as: buffer between pedestrians and moving vehicles, green infrastructure location, snow storage in winter climates

**Edge / Curb Zone**
- Width: 0.15-0.3m (curb itself)
- Function: vertical separation between sidewalk and roadway, drainage, vehicle deterrent
- Height: 150mm standard, 100mm at bus stops (for level boarding), flush at shared streets and accessible crossings
- Material: granite or concrete curb

### Street Trees

**Spacing**: 6.0-9.0m on center for continuous canopy (varies by species mature spread)
**Soil Volume**: 15-20 cubic meters per tree minimum for healthy growth to maturity
- Individual tree pits: minimum 1.2m x 1.2m opening, structural soil or suspended pavement system below
- Continuous tree trenches: 1.2-1.8m wide, connected below grade, shared soil volume among multiple trees (preferred method)
- Silva cells or equivalent modular structural soil systems allow full pavement loading above root zone

**Canopy Goals**: 40-60% canopy coverage at maturity for urban heat island mitigation and pedestrian comfort
**Clearance**: 2.4m minimum above sidewalk, 4.2m minimum above bike lanes, 4.5m minimum above roadway

**Species Selection Factors**:
- Native or well-adapted species preferred for ecosystem integration
- Salt tolerance for streets with winter maintenance
- Drought tolerance for urban conditions and climate resilience
- Non-invasive root systems to protect adjacent infrastructure
- Disease resistance (avoid monoculture, plant diverse species)
- Canopy shape: columnar for narrow streets, spreading for boulevards
- Deciduous for seasonal solar access (shade in summer, sun in winter)

### Transit Infrastructure

**Bus Stops**:
- Spacing: 200-400m in urban areas, 300-500m in suburban areas
- Length: 15m for one bus, 30m for two (articulated: 24m for one)
- Near-side vs. far-side: far-side preferred (reduces signal delay, improves sight lines)
- Bus bulbs (curb extensions at stops): eliminate merging delay, allow level boarding, add pedestrian space
- In-lane stops: bus stops in travel lane (no pull-out), keeps buses on schedule

**Shelters**:
- Minimum 1.5m x 3.0m footprint, clear of pedestrian through zone
- Seating, lighting, real-time arrival information, weather protection
- Transparent walls for security and visibility
- Accessible: level boarding platform, tactile guidance, audible announcements

**Transit Platforms (BRT/LRT)**:
- Island platform: 2.4-3.6m wide, center of median
- Side platform: 2.4-3.0m wide, at curbside
- Level boarding: platform height matches vehicle floor (300-380mm for BRT, varies for LRT)
- Length: 15-60m depending on vehicle consist

### Bicycle Facilities

**Selection Criteria by Context**:

| Motor Vehicle Speed | Motor Vehicle Volume | Recommended Facility |
|---------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| >50 km/h | Any | Protected bike lane or separate path |
| 40-50 km/h | >6,000 ADT | Protected bike lane |
| 40-50 km/h | <6,000 ADT | Buffered bike lane |
| 30-40 km/h | >3,000 ADT | Protected or buffered bike lane |
| 30-40 km/h | <3,000 ADT | Conventional bike lane |
| <30 km/h | <3,000 ADT | Shared lane (sharrow) or neighborhood greenway |
| <20 km/h | <1,000 ADT | Shared street, no specific facility needed |

**Protected Bike Lane (Cycle Track)**:
- One-way: 1.5m minimum, 2.0m recommended, 2.5m high-volume
- Two-way: 2.4m minimum, 3.0m recommended, 3.6m high-volume
- Separation: physical barrier (curb, planters, bollards, parked cars, grade separation)
- Buffer between bike lane and parking lane: 0.6m minimum (to avoid door zone)
- Buffer between bike lane and travel lane: 0.3m minimum painted, 0.6m preferred with vertical elements
- Surface: smooth asphalt or concrete, distinct from sidewalk and roadway
- Colored surface (green) at conflict zones (driveways, intersections, bus stops)
- Drainage: grade toward roadway or separate bike lane drainage

**Conventional Bike Lane**:
- Width: 1.5m minimum, 1.8m recommended (measured from curb face or edge of parking)
- Marked with solid white line and bike lane symbol
- Must be outside door zone of parked cars (0.9m from parked car face to bike lane)
- Not recommended where speeds >40 km/h or volumes >6,000 ADT

**Buffered Bike Lane**:
- Bike lane (1.5-1.8m) plus painted buffer (0.6-0.9m) on one or both sides
- Buffer marked with diagonal or chevron hatching
- Provides additional separation without physical barriers
- Interim treatment toward protected bike lane

**Shared Bus-Bike Lane**:
- Width: 3.6-4.5m (wide enough for bus to pass cyclist)
- Applicable where both modes share low-frequency corridor
- Bike symbols and "BUS BIKE ONLY" markings
- Not ideal; protected bike lane + dedicated bus lane preferred when ROW permits

### Curbside Management

The curb zone is a contested and valuable resource. NACTO recommends flexible curbside allocation:

**Flex Zone Concept**: Same curbside space serves different functions at different times of day:
- 6:00-10:00 AM: commercial loading zone
- 10:00 AM-3:00 PM: metered parking
- 3:00-7:00 PM: no parking, transit or bike lane peak extension
- 7:00-11:00 PM: restaurant/bar parking and rideshare pickup
- Overnight: unrestricted parking

**Curbside Uses** (in priority order for urban areas):
1. Transit stops (highest priority - most people per linear meter)
2. Pedestrian safety features (curb extensions, accessibility ramps)
3. Bicycle facilities (bike corrals, bike-share stations)
4. Loading and deliveries (time-restricted)
5. Rideshare pickup/drop-off
6. Metered short-term parking
7. Long-term or free parking (lowest priority)

### Roadway Configuration

**Lane Widths**:
- 2.7m: yield streets, very low speed environments, right-turn slip lanes
- 3.0m: standard urban lane, all street types, adequate for cars, SUVs, and most trucks
- 3.3m: bus routes, truck routes, outside lane on multi-lane roads
- 3.6m: maximum for any urban lane; wider lanes induce speeding and are never recommended
- NACTO explicitly states that 3.0m lanes are safe and appropriate for urban streets at speeds up to 50 km/h

**Medians**:
- Minimum width for pedestrian refuge: 1.8m (2.4m preferred, 3.0m with accessibility)
- Landscaped median minimum: 2.4m (for trees and planting)
- Transit median: 3.6-5.0m (includes platform)
- Left-turn pockets within median: 3.0-3.3m wide, 30-45m storage length
- Median noses: rounded or pointed, with bollards or reflectors for visibility
- Median breaks: at intersections and authorized U-turn locations only

**Turn Lanes**:
- Only where turn movement volumes warrant (>100 turning vehicles in peak hour)
- Width: 2.7-3.0m
- Storage length: 30-45m minimum, based on queue analysis
- Always combine with pedestrian refuge island in median
- Right-turn channelized islands: minimize, redesign as tight corners with yield control

### Stormwater Management

**NACTO Urban Street Stormwater Guide Integration**:

Green infrastructure should be integrated into every street redesign. Priority locations:
- Furnishing zones (bioswales, tree trenches, stormwater planters)
- Curb extensions (rain gardens in bulb-outs)
- Medians (bioswales, rain gardens, permeable surfaces)
- Parking lanes (permeable pavement)

**Design Standards**:
- Bioswale depth: 150-300mm below curb cut inlet
- Bioswale side slopes: 3:1 maximum
- Soil media: engineered mix (60% sand, 20% compost, 20% topsoil typical)
- Underdrain: perforated pipe at 450-600mm depth, connected to storm system as overflow
- Sizing: capture first 25mm of rainfall from contributing impervious area
- Ponding: maximum 150mm depth, 24-48 hour drawdown
- Maintenance: mulch replacement annually, plant replacement as needed, inlet cleaning quarterly
