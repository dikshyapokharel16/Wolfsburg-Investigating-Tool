# Street Typologies Reference

Comprehensive specifications for 12+ street typologies used in urban and suburban design. Each entry includes dimensional standards, cross-section description, design character, materials palette, applicable contexts, and global precedents.

---

## 1. Boulevard

### Overview
The boulevard is the grandest urban street type: a wide, tree-lined, divided thoroughfare designed for multi-modal movement at a civic scale. Boulevards are the structural spines of great cities, accommodating high traffic volumes while maintaining a strong pedestrian and landscape character. They are defined by their central median, generous sidewalks, and formal tree planting.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 35-50m | 40m |
| Travel lanes per direction | 2-3 | 2 |
| Lane width | 3.0-3.3m | 3.3m |
| Central median | 3.6-6.0m | 4.8m |
| Protected bike lane (each side) | 2.0-2.5m | 2.0m |
| Bike lane buffer | 0.6m | 0.6m |
| Parking lane (each side) | 2.4m | 2.4m |
| Furnishing zone (each side) | 1.2-1.8m | 1.5m |
| Pedestrian clear zone (each side) | 4.5-6.0m | 4.8m |
| Frontage zone (each side) | 0.6-0.9m | 0.6m |
| Design speed | 40-50 km/h | 40 km/h |
| ADT capacity | 15,000-40,000 | 25,000 |

### Cross-Section Description
From building face to centerline: frontage zone (0.6m), wide pedestrian clear zone (4.8m), furnishing zone with double tree row and pedestrian lighting (1.5m), protected one-way bike lane (2.0m) with physical buffer (0.6m), parallel parking lane (2.4m), two or three travel lanes (3.3m each), and a landscaped central median (4.8m) that may accommodate center-running BRT or LRT. The opposite side mirrors this arrangement.

### Design Character
- Formal, civic, monumental
- Strong spatial enclosure from building facades and mature tree canopy
- Generous sidewalk areas encourage strolling, outdoor dining, sidewalk cafes
- Median provides green spine, pedestrian refuge, and potential transit corridor
- Regular rhythm of trees, lights, and furniture creates visual order

### Materials Palette
- Sidewalks: cast-in-place concrete with scored joints, granite or bluestone unit pavers at special nodes
- Furnishing zone: tree grates (cast iron or steel), granite curbs, formal benches (cast iron and wood)
- Cycle track: smooth asphalt with green color at conflict zones
- Roadway: hot-mix asphalt, concrete at bus stops
- Median: planted (low shrubs, ground cover, seasonal flowers, large-canopy trees)
- Lighting: decorative poles 4.5-5.0m for pedestrians, 10-12m for roadway, coordinated design family

### Applicable Contexts
- City-wide corridors connecting major destinations
- Ceremonial or civic routes (government buildings, cultural institutions)
- Transit trunk routes (BRT, LRT)
- Commercial corridors with ground-floor retail
- Waterfront or park-edge promenades at expanded scale

### Precedent Streets
- **Champs-Elysees, Paris**: 70m ROW, the archetypal grand boulevard, eight lanes with wide sidewalks and double tree rows
- **Paseo de la Reforma, Mexico City**: 90m ROW, center-running Metrobus BRT, grand median with monuments
- **Commonwealth Avenue, Boston**: 73m ROW, center mall/median, four rows of trees, residential character
- **Unter den Linden, Berlin**: 60m ROW, historic linden tree-lined boulevard connecting Brandenburg Gate
- **Avenida 9 de Julio, Buenos Aires**: 140m ROW (extreme case), multiple medians, 20+ lanes

## 2. Avenue

### Overview
The avenue is a moderately scaled tree-lined street connecting neighborhoods and districts. More intimate than a boulevard but grander than a main street, avenues balance movement with residential and commercial frontage. They may or may not feature a median and typically carry moderate traffic with good transit service.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 25-35m | 30m |
| Travel lanes per direction | 1-2 | 2 |
| Lane width | 3.0-3.3m | 3.0m |
| Median (where present) | 1.8-3.6m | 2.4m |
| Bike facility (each side) | 1.8-2.5m | 2.0m (protected) |
| Bike buffer | 0.3-0.6m | 0.6m |
| Parking lane (each side) | 2.1-2.4m | 2.4m |
| Furnishing zone (each side) | 0.9-1.5m | 1.2m |
| Pedestrian clear zone (each side) | 3.0-4.8m | 3.6m |
| Frontage zone (each side) | 0.3-0.6m | 0.6m |
| Design speed | 35-40 km/h | 40 km/h |
| ADT capacity | 8,000-25,000 | 15,000 |

### Cross-Section Description
Building face to centerline: frontage zone (0.6m), pedestrian clear zone (3.6m), furnishing zone with single row of large-canopy trees (1.2m), protected bike lane (2.0m) with buffer (0.6m), parallel parking (2.4m), two travel lanes (3.0m each), optional median (2.4m). Mirrored on opposite side.

### Design Character
- Elegant, tree-canopied, urban but with residential grace
- Strong tree canopy creating a green corridor
- Active ground-floor uses (shops, cafes, services) mixed with residential upper floors
- Transit stops every 300-400m with shelters

### Materials Palette
- Sidewalks: concrete with unit paver banding at tree locations
- Furnishing zone: continuous tree trench with grates or low planting
- Roadway: asphalt
- Median: planted with ground cover and pedestrian refuge cuts at crossings
- Lighting: coordinated pedestrian and roadway poles

### Applicable Contexts
- Cross-town connectors
- Neighborhood commercial corridors
- Transit corridors with mixed-use zoning
- Connections between civic destinations (parks, schools, libraries)

### Precedent Streets
- **Park Avenue, New York**: planted median, residential upper floors, commercial at grade in midtown
- **Kurfurstendamm, Berlin**: wide sidewalks, retail frontage, tree-lined
- **St Kilda Road, Melbourne**: tree-lined with tram reservation in median

## 3. Main Street / High Street

### Overview
The main street is the commercial heart of a neighborhood or town. Designed around pedestrian activity and retail frontage, it features wide sidewalks, narrow travel lanes, on-street parking to buffer pedestrians and provide customer access, and a lively, fine-grained streetscape. Speed is deliberately low (25-30 km/h) and the street reads as a public room rather than a traffic corridor.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 20-27m | 24m |
| Travel lanes per direction | 1 | 1 |
| Lane width | 3.0m | 3.0m |
| Median | None (occasional left-turn pockets) | None |
| Bike facility | Shared lane or bike parking corrals | Sharrow or parking-protected lane |
| Parking (each side) | 2.1-2.4m (parallel) | 2.4m |
| Furnishing zone (each side) | 0.9-1.5m | 1.2m |
| Pedestrian clear zone (each side) | 3.0-4.8m | 3.6m |
| Frontage zone (each side) | 0.6-1.8m | 1.2m (outdoor dining) |
| Design speed | 25-30 km/h | 30 km/h |
| ADT capacity | 3,000-12,000 | 8,000 |

### Cross-Section Description
Building face to centerline: active frontage zone with outdoor dining (1.2m), wide pedestrian clear zone (3.6m), furnishing zone with trees and pedestrian lighting (1.2m), parallel parking (2.4m), single travel lane (3.0m), centerline. Mirror on opposite side. Total approximately 23.6m.

### Design Character
- Vibrant, pedestrian-dominant, commercially active
- Continuous shopfront display windows at ground level
- Awnings and canopies providing weather protection
- Outdoor dining and retail display in frontage zone
- Slow vehicle speeds create safe, walkable environment
- Frequent, short pedestrian crossings

### Materials Palette
- Sidewalks: high-quality concrete or unit pavers, coordinated with streetscape theme
- Frontage zone: match building materials or decorative pavers
- Furnishing zone: tree grates, decorative bollards, coordinated street furniture family
- Roadway: asphalt with special treatment at crossings (brick, pavers, or colored asphalt)
- Lighting: decorative pedestrian poles at 4.0-5.0m height, warm 3000K, closely spaced (15-20m)

### Applicable Contexts
- Neighborhood commercial districts
- Downtown main streets in towns and small cities
- Ground-floor retail corridors in mixed-use developments
- Historic town centers
- Transit-oriented development main streets

### Precedent Streets
- **Broadway, Portland OR**: narrow lanes, wide sidewalks, strong retail, bike-friendly
- **High Street, Oxford**: historic, pedestrian-heavy, restricted vehicle access
- **King Street, Alexandria VA**: brick sidewalks, colonial character, lively retail
- **Roncesvalles Avenue, Toronto**: main street with streetcar, wide sidewalks, local shops

## 4. Neighborhood Street

### Overview
The neighborhood street is the most common street type by length in any city. It provides access to homes, with low traffic volumes and speeds. The emphasis is on creating a quiet, green, safe, and pleasant living environment with generous tree canopy, sidewalks for walking, and design that naturally calms traffic.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 15-22m | 18m |
| Travel lanes | 1 per direction | 1 per direction |
| Lane width | 2.7-3.0m | 3.0m |
| Parking | Parallel, one or both sides | One side, 2.1m |
| Furnishing zone (each side) | 0.9-1.5m | 1.2m |
| Pedestrian clear zone (each side) | 1.5-2.4m | 1.8m |
| Frontage zone | 0.3m or none | 0.3m |
| Design speed | 25-30 km/h | 30 km/h |
| ADT | 500-3,000 | 1,500 |

### Cross-Section Description
Building face to centerline: minimal frontage zone (0.3m), pedestrian clear zone (1.8m), furnishing zone with trees (1.2m), parallel parking one side (2.1m), travel lane (3.0m), centerline. Opposite side mirrors without parking or with parking on alternating blocks. Total approximately 17.4m.

### Design Character
- Quiet, green, residential
- Strong tree canopy (>50% coverage at maturity)
- Children walking to school, residents walking dogs, neighbors conversing
- Low vehicle speeds naturally enforced by narrow geometry
- Driveways and garage access

### Materials Palette
- Sidewalks: standard concrete (broom finish)
- Furnishing zone: open tree pits with ground cover or grates, grass strips where wider
- Roadway: asphalt
- Lighting: residential-scale poles at 4.0-5.0m, 3000K warm white, 20-25m spacing
- No special paving or treatments needed; simplicity is appropriate

### Applicable Contexts
- Single-family and low-rise residential neighborhoods
- Low-traffic access streets off collectors
- School walking routes

### Precedent Streets
- Typical residential streets in Portland, Vancouver, Amsterdam side streets, Melbourne inner suburban streets

## 5. Shared Street / Woonerf

### Overview
The shared street eliminates the conventional separation between pedestrian and vehicle space. A single flush surface is shared by all users, with pedestrians having legal and practical priority. Vehicles are guests and must travel at walking pace. The design uses texture, level changes, plantings, and furniture to create a pedestrian-dominant environment where vehicles can access but not dominate.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 8-15m | 12m |
| Defined lanes | None | None |
| Drivable width | 3.0-5.0m (sinuous path) | 4.0m |
| Pedestrian areas | Remainder of ROW | 7-8m combined |
| Design speed | 10-20 km/h | 15 km/h |
| ADT | <1,000 | 300-500 |

### Cross-Section Description
No conventional cross-section. The entire ROW is a single flush surface with no curbs. Planters, bollards, seating, and changes in paving pattern define a sinuous vehicle path (3.0-5.0m wide) that weaves through the space. The remaining area (60-75% of ROW) serves pedestrians with seating, planting, play space, and social gathering areas. Trees in large above-grade planters or in-ground with flush grates.

### Design Character
- Village square extended into a linear format
- Living room of the neighborhood
- Playful, informal, textured
- Visual richness through varied materials and planting
- Social interaction encouraged by design

### Materials Palette
- Surface: unit pavers throughout (clay brick, natural stone, concrete pavers), no asphalt
- Vehicle path: subtly differentiated by paver color or pattern (not by level change)
- Drainage: flush linear drains or permeable paving
- Furniture: movable and fixed benches, planters (heavy, not easily displaced), play elements
- Bollards: retractable or removable at entry points for emergency/service access
- Lighting: low-level (3.0-4.0m) festoon or wall-mounted, warm and inviting

### Applicable Contexts
- Low-traffic residential streets
- Market streets and commercial lanes
- Hotel and hospitality districts
- Historic cores with narrow ROW
- Event and festival streets
- Waterfront promenades with vehicle access

### Precedent Streets
- **Dutch woonerven**: the original living street concept, widespread in the Netherlands since 1970s
- **Exhibition Road, London**: high-profile shared surface redesign in museum district
- **Bell Street Park, Seattle**: converted residential street to linear park with vehicle access
- **New Road, Brighton**: shared surface high street, dramatic pedestrian increase after redesign
- **Argyle Street, Chicago**: shared street with Asian cultural district identity

## 6. Alley / Lane

### Overview
Alleys and rear lanes provide secondary access for servicing, loading, garage entry, and utilities. They relieve primary streets of truck and service vehicle conflicts. Increasingly, alleys are being redesigned as green alleys (with permeable paving and stormwater management) or activated alleys (with murals, lighting, seating, and small-scale commercial uses).

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 4-8m | 6m |
| Travel width | 3.0-6.0m | 4.5m |
| Direction | One-way or two-way (with yielding) | One-way preferred |
| Sidewalks | None (shared surface) | None |
| Design speed | 10-15 km/h | 10 km/h |
| ADT | <200 | <100 |

### Cross-Section Description
Full-width paved surface from property line to property line. No curbs, no sidewalks. Drainage via center crown or to one side. Garage doors and service entrances open directly onto the alley. May include overhead utility lines.

### Design Character
- Utilitarian but increasingly activated as urban amenity
- Murals, string lights, and planters for activated alleys
- Green alleys use permeable paving, planting strips, and rain gardens
- Informal, organic, discovery-oriented character

### Materials Palette
- Standard: asphalt or concrete
- Green alley: permeable concrete pavers, central drainage strip with gravel or planting
- Activated alley: unit pavers, overhead string lighting, wall-mounted planters, murals

### Applicable Contexts
- Mid-block service access in all urban contexts
- Garage access for residential blocks
- Loading access for commercial buildings
- Emerging use: pedestrian shortcuts, small-scale retail, pop-up markets, outdoor galleries

### Precedent Streets
- **Chicago green alleys**: permeable paving, stormwater management
- **Melbourne laneways**: world-famous activated alleys with cafes, bars, street art
- **Freeway Park Alleys, Seattle**: activated mid-block connections

## 7. Passage / Pedestrian Street

### Overview
A fully pedestrianized street that carries no regular vehicle traffic. Emergency and service vehicle access is maintained but controlled (time-restricted, retractable bollards). The entire ROW is designed as a public space with high-quality paving, seating, planting, public art, and active retail/cafe frontage.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 4-20m | 10-12m |
| Vehicle access | Emergency/service only | Bollard-controlled |
| Clear width for emergency vehicles | 3.5m minimum | 3.5m |
| Design speed | 5-10 km/h (service vehicles) | Walking pace |
| Pedestrian capacity | Very high | Sized to context |

### Cross-Section Description
No conventional cross-section. The full ROW is a high-quality pedestrian surface. Active retail frontage on both sides with outdoor seating, display, and spill-out activity. Trees in large planting beds or above-grade planters. Central clear zone of 3.5m minimum maintained for emergency access. Movable furniture (tables, chairs, planters) fills the remaining space.

### Design Character
- Vibrant, bustling, human-scaled
- Dense retail and cafe activity
- Public art, performance space, market stalls
- Strong sense of enclosure from building facades
- High-quality materials and design detailing

### Materials Palette
- Surface: natural stone (granite, bluestone), high-quality unit pavers, or polished concrete
- Drainage: subtle cross-fall to linear drains, flush with surface
- Furniture: high-quality benches, movable cafe seating, bicycle parking
- Planting: trees in structural soil or raised beds, seasonal flower displays
- Lighting: building-mounted, pendant, or low-level (3.0-5.0m), warm and decorative

### Applicable Contexts
- Historic city centers
- Major retail corridors
- Cultural and entertainment districts
- Connections between transit stations and destinations
- University campuses

### Precedent Streets
- **Stroget, Copenhagen**: Europe's longest pedestrian street, converted from traffic in 1962
- **Lijnbaan, Rotterdam**: post-war pedestrian shopping street, one of the earliest purpose-built
- **Grafton Street, Dublin**: major retail pedestrian street
- **Third Street Promenade, Santa Monica**: three-block pedestrian retail district
- **Buchanan Street, Glasgow**: pedestrianized civic spine

## 8. Transit Mall

### Overview
A transit mall is a street dedicated to public transit vehicles (buses, trams, or light rail), pedestrians, and cyclists. Private motor vehicles are excluded or severely restricted. The design prioritizes transit speed and reliability, pedestrian comfort, and cycling connectivity while creating a high-quality public space.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 18-30m | 24m |
| Transit lanes | 2 (one per direction) | 3.3-3.6m each |
| Pedestrian zones | 4.5-8.0m each side | 6.0m each side |
| Bike facility | 2.0-3.0m each side | 2.0m protected |
| Median/platform | 2.4-4.0m | 3.0m center platform |
| Design speed (transit) | 20-30 km/h | 25 km/h |
| Design speed (private vehicles) | Not permitted | N/A |

### Cross-Section Description
Building face to centerline: wide pedestrian zone (6.0m) with frontage, clear zone, and furnishing subzones; protected bike lane (2.0m); transit lane (3.3m); center platform or median (3.0m). Mirrored. Alternatively, transit lanes center-running with side platforms.

### Design Character
- Clean, modern, efficient transit environment
- Strong pedestrian realm with generous sidewalks
- Transit vehicles are the only motorized presence
- Platform canopies, real-time information, wayfinding
- High-quality paving differentiating zones

### Materials Palette
- Pedestrian zones: unit pavers or high-quality concrete
- Transit lanes: concrete (for durability under bus loads) or embedded rail
- Platforms: anti-slip concrete or stone, tactile edge warnings
- Bike lanes: colored asphalt
- Planting: formal tree rows, low-maintenance ground cover

### Applicable Contexts
- Downtown cores with high transit ridership
- University campuses
- Corridors connecting transit stations to activity centers
- Streets where traffic demand is low but transit demand is high

### Precedent Streets
- **16th Street Mall, Denver**: free bus shuttle, pedestrian-oriented, 1.6km long
- **Bourke Street Mall, Melbourne**: tram and pedestrian only in city center
- **Nicollet Mall, Minneapolis**: bus and pedestrian, recently redesigned
- **Market Street, San Francisco**: transit, bike, and pedestrian; private cars restricted since 2020

## 9. Festival Street

### Overview
A festival street operates as a normal street most of the time but is designed to be easily closed for events, markets, parades, and community gatherings. Key design features include flush surfaces (no curb height difference), removable bollards at entry points, underground utility access for event power and water, and a robust paving surface that can support both vehicles and heavy pedestrian loads.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 15-25m | 20m |
| Normal operation | Standard local or collector street | 2 lanes + parking |
| Event mode | Full pedestrianization | Bollard-closed |
| Flush surface | Required | Entire ROW |
| Design speed (normal) | 25-30 km/h | 30 km/h |
| Design speed (event) | Walking pace | 5 km/h |

### Cross-Section Description
Normal mode: sidewalks (flush, no curb), furnishing zone with bollards and trees, parking, travel lanes (all at same level, differentiated by material and color). Event mode: bollards raised or placed at street entries, parking cleared, entire ROW becomes pedestrian space. Event infrastructure (power outlets, water connections, anchor points) embedded in pavement.

### Design Character
- Dual personality: orderly street by day, festive public space by event
- Robust, durable, attractive materials throughout
- Flexible: can accommodate farmers markets, art fairs, concerts, block parties
- Community-building infrastructure

### Materials Palette
- Surface: unit pavers throughout (flush, heavy-duty, vehicular-rated)
- Bollards: retractable (hydraulic or manual) or removable sleeve-type
- Drainage: linear slot drains flush with surface
- Event infrastructure: flush-mount power outlets, water spigots, tent anchor points embedded in pavement
- Lighting: festoon-capable overhead catenary wires or poles with hook attachments

### Applicable Contexts
- Neighborhoods with strong community event traditions
- Streets adjacent to civic buildings, cultural venues, or parks
- Main streets in small towns with weekly or seasonal markets
- New development streets where community activation is a goal

### Precedent Streets
- **Festival streets in Portland, OR**: multiple streets designated for closure and events
- **Open Streets / Ciclovia programs**: temporary closures adapted to permanent festival street design
- **Times Square, New York**: while not a festival street per se, demonstrates the power of repurposing vehicle space

## 10. Green Street

### Overview
A green street integrates stormwater management, urban ecology, and green infrastructure as primary design drivers. While all streets should incorporate green elements, a designated green street makes stormwater treatment the organizing principle, with bioswales, rain gardens, permeable paving, and extensive tree planting forming the visual and functional backbone of the street.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 15-25m | 20m |
| Travel lanes | 1-2 per direction | 1 per direction |
| Lane width | 3.0m | 3.0m |
| Bioswale/rain garden width | 1.2-2.4m | 1.8m |
| Permeable parking lanes | 2.1-2.4m | 2.4m |
| Sidewalk | 1.8-3.0m | 2.4m |
| Design speed | 25-30 km/h | 30 km/h |
| Impervious area reduction | 30-50% vs. conventional | 40% |

### Cross-Section Description
Building face to centerline: sidewalk (2.4m) on permeable base, bioswale in furnishing zone (1.8m wide, 150-300mm deep, planted with native grasses and sedges), permeable paver parking lane (2.4m), travel lane on conventional asphalt (3.0m), narrow painted median or flush center. Bioswales receive stormwater through curb cuts every 6-9m. Overflow connects to conventional storm drain via underdrain.

### Design Character
- Lush, green, naturalistic
- Visible stormwater management (flowing water during rain events is a design feature)
- Native and adaptive planting creating habitat corridors
- Educational signage explaining green infrastructure function
- Seasonal variation in plant character

### Materials Palette
- Sidewalks: permeable concrete or permeable pavers
- Bioswales: native plantings in engineered soil media, river rock at inlets and outlets
- Parking lanes: permeable interlocking concrete pavers
- Roadway: asphalt (may use porous asphalt where subgrade permits)
- Curb cuts: granite or concrete notches at 6-9m intervals directing runoff into bioswales
- Tree planting: silva cells or structural soil for large canopy trees integrated with bioswales

### Applicable Contexts
- Neighborhoods with stormwater management challenges
- Combined sewer overflow reduction areas
- Streets adjacent to sensitive waterways or wetlands
- New developments with aggressive sustainability targets
- Retrofit of existing streets during reconstruction

### Precedent Streets
- **SE 12th Avenue Green Street, Portland**: pioneering green street with curbside bioswales
- **High Point, Seattle**: neighborhood-wide green street stormwater system
- **Elmer Avenue, Los Angeles**: residential green street demonstration project
- **Queensway, London**: proposed green infrastructure corridor

## 11. Complete Street (Generic)

### Overview
The "complete street" is not a specific typology but a design philosophy: every street should be designed for safe, comfortable, and convenient travel by all users of all ages and abilities, including pedestrians, cyclists, transit riders, motorists, and freight operators. The specific cross-section varies by context, but no single mode dominates at the expense of others.

### Dimensional Standards
Variable by context. A complete street applies the principles of:
- Pedestrian safety and comfort as the first priority
- Bicycle accommodation appropriate to speed and volume context
- Transit priority where transit operates
- Motor vehicle service at the target speed (not the maximum throughput)
- Freight and service access where needed
- Universal accessibility throughout

### Design Principles
| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| All ages and abilities | Design for 8-80 year old users; if an 8-year-old can bike and an 80-year-old can cross, the street works |
| Context sensitivity | Match the cross-section to adjacent land use and network role |
| Target speed | Design geometry to enforce the desired speed, do not post a limit and hope |
| Mode balance | Allocate ROW proportional to person-throughput goals, not vehicle throughput |
| Green infrastructure | Integrate stormwater management into every project |
| Placemaking | Streets are public spaces; design them as such |
| Network thinking | Every street is part of a walking, cycling, transit, and driving network; ensure continuity |

### Applicable Contexts
- Every urban and suburban street (the goal is that all streets become "complete")
- Required by complete streets policies in many US and Canadian jurisdictions
- Especially critical for: school routes, transit corridors, commercial districts, and equity-priority areas

## 12. Rural Road

### Overview
Rural roads connect towns, serve agricultural land, and provide access to rural properties. They differ fundamentally from urban streets: no curbs, open drainage ditches, wide clear zones, higher speeds, and minimal pedestrian infrastructure. Where rural roads pass through villages or small settlements, a transition to a lower-speed, more urban character is essential (gateway treatments).

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 15-30m | 20m |
| Travel lanes | 1 per direction | 1 per direction |
| Lane width | 3.0-3.6m | 3.3m |
| Paved shoulder | 0.6-2.4m | 1.5m |
| Clear zone (from edge of travel) | 3.0-9.0m | Varies by speed and terrain |
| Drainage | Open ditch, 0.6-1.2m deep | V-ditch or trapezoidal |
| Design speed | 50-80 km/h | 60 km/h |
| ADT | 500-5,000 | 2,000 |

### Cross-Section Description
From centerline to ROW edge: travel lane (3.3m), paved shoulder (1.5m), gravel or grass shoulder to drainage ditch, open drainage ditch (1.0m wide, 0.6m deep), clear zone to ROW boundary. No curbs, no sidewalks (except through villages), no street lighting (except at intersections).

### Design Character
- Open, rural, agrarian
- Long sight lines, gentle curves
- Fence lines, hedgerows, or open fields at ROW edge
- Minimal infrastructure: no curbs, no storm sewers, no street furniture
- Character changes dramatically at village transitions (gateway treatments with speed reduction, sidewalks, lighting, and narrowing)

### Materials Palette
- Roadway: asphalt (chip seal on lower-volume roads)
- Shoulders: crushed gravel or stabilized earth
- Drainage: earthen ditches with grass lining
- Markings: center line and edge lines painted
- Transitions: village gateway with surface change, curbs, narrowing, lighting, and signage

### Applicable Contexts
- Roads between settlements
- Agricultural and resource land access
- Rural-urban fringe (with transition treatments)
- Scenic routes and touring roads

## 13. Promenade / Waterfront Street

### Overview
A specialized street type along waterfronts, parks, or scenic corridors that prioritizes the pedestrian and recreational experience. Features extra-wide promenades, separated multi-use paths, enhanced landscape, view preservation, and a clear hierarchy favoring walking and cycling over driving.

### Dimensional Standards
| Parameter | Range | Typical |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Right-of-Way | 25-45m | 35m |
| Promenade (water side) | 6.0-12.0m | 8.0m |
| Multi-use path | 3.0-4.5m | 3.6m |
| Travel lanes | 1-2 per direction | 1 per direction |
| Lane width | 3.0m | 3.0m |
| Landscape buffer to water | 3.0-6.0m | 4.5m |
| Building-side sidewalk | 3.0-4.5m | 3.6m |
| Design speed | 30-40 km/h | 30 km/h |

### Cross-Section Description
From water edge: landscape zone (4.5m), wide promenade with seating areas (8.0m), multi-use path for cycling and jogging (3.6m), furnishing zone with trees (1.5m), travel lanes (3.0m each direction), furnishing zone (1.2m), building-side pedestrian zone (3.6m), building face.

### Design Character
- Open, scenic, recreational
- Strong visual and physical connection to water or park
- Generous public seating and gathering spaces along promenade
- Separated cycling for recreational and commuter use
- View corridors preserved through strategic tree placement and building massing
- Event-capable design for waterfront festivals and markets

### Materials Palette
- Promenade: premium unit pavers or boardwalk timber
- Multi-use path: smooth asphalt or colored concrete
- Landscape: native coastal or riparian planting, salt-tolerant species
- Furniture: marine-grade materials, durable benches, railings where needed for safety
- Lighting: low-level promenade lighting (4.0m) plus feature lighting on water

### Applicable Contexts
- River, lake, and ocean waterfronts
- Park edges and greenway corridors
- Scenic routes with special landscape character
- Redeveloped industrial waterfront districts

### Precedent Streets
- **Embarcadero, San Francisco**: post-freeway removal waterfront boulevard
- **Battery Park City Esplanade, New York**: waterfront promenade with separated path
- **Corniche, Beirut**: Mediterranean waterfront promenade
- **Circular Quay, Sydney**: transit, pedestrian, and waterfront combined
