# Neighborhood Typologies Catalog

This reference provides a comprehensive catalog of ten neighborhood typologies
commonly used in masterplan design. Each typology is described with its full
specifications, design rules, key metrics, and precedent projects. Use this
catalog when assigning character to areas within a masterplan and when
selecting appropriate density, building types, and spatial patterns for each
zone.

---

## How to Use This Catalog

1. During Phase 3 (Structuring Framework), identify which typologies are
   appropriate for the project based on context, market, and aspirations.
2. During Phase 5 (Block and Parcel Layout), apply the design rules from the
   selected typology to size and configure blocks.
3. During Step 12 (Define Character Areas), reference the typology descriptions
   and precedents to articulate the intended character of each area.
4. Most masterplans combine 2-4 typologies across their Transect zones.
   Transitions between typologies should occur at natural boundaries (parks,
   primary streets, topographic changes).

## 1. Garden Suburb

### Description

A low-density residential neighborhood characterized by generous plots,
mature tree canopy, curvilinear streets, and a strong relationship between
buildings and landscape. The Garden Suburb derives from the Garden City
movement of the early twentieth century and remains one of the most
enduringly popular residential environments worldwide. Streets follow
topography rather than a rigid grid, creating a varied and picturesque
sequence of views. Houses are typically detached or semi-detached, set
back from the street behind front gardens, with substantial rear gardens.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 10-50 ha
- Population: 1,000-5,000 residents
- Dwellings: 200-1,200 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 15-30 DU/ha
- FAR: 0.3-0.8
- Building height: 1-3 stories (predominantly 2 stories)
- Site coverage: 25-40%
- Building types: detached houses, semi-detached houses, occasional
  short terraces (3-6 units), coach houses over garages

### Street Pattern

- Modified grid or curvilinear layout following topography
- Street hierarchy: collector streets (16-20 m ROW) feed into local streets
  (12-16 m ROW) and shared lanes (8-10 m ROW)
- Cul-de-sacs are acceptable in limited quantities where topography demands
  them, but they must connect to the pedestrian network via footpaths
- Tree-lined streets with grass verges (minimum 2 m verge width)
- On-street parking on one or both sides of collectors
- Target: 80% of homes within 400 m walk of a bus stop

### Use Mix

- 90-95% residential
- 3-5% local convenience (small shop, cafe, post office at the village center)
- 2-5% community/institutional (primary school, church, community hall)

### Public Space Strategy

- Village green (0.5-1.5 ha) at the center, surrounded by the community
  facilities and convenience retail
- Allotments or community gardens (0.2-0.5 ha per 1,000 residents)
- Pocket parks at street junctions (0.05-0.2 ha every 300-500 m)
- Generous street tree planting: target 40% canopy cover at maturity
- Rear garden provision: minimum 50% of plot area for gardens

### Precedent Projects

- **Letchworth Garden City** (UK, 1903): the original Garden City. 2,000 ha,
  33,000 residents. Designed by Raymond Unwin and Barry Parker. Generous
  plots, tree-lined avenues, central town square, surrounding greenbelt.
- **Hampstead Garden Suburb** (UK, 1907): 100 ha, designed by Raymond Unwin.
  Curvilinear streets, hedged front gardens, the Heath Extension, Central
  Square with church and institute.
- **Forest Hills Gardens** (New York, 1909): 70 ha, Olmsted Brothers landscape
  plan. Tudor-style houses, winding streets, Station Square civic center.

### Design Rules

1. Maximum block length: 200 m (pedestrian cut-throughs every 100 m maximum)
2. Front garden depth: minimum 5 m, maximum 10 m
3. Building spacing: minimum 3 m between detached houses
4. Rear garden depth: minimum 10 m
5. Street trees: one tree per 8-12 m of street frontage
6. Maximum cul-de-sac length: 80 m, must connect to pedestrian network
7. All houses must front a street or public open space (no rear-loaded lots
   on public frontages)

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 20-25 DU/ha |
| Green space per capita | 15-25 m2 |
| Tree canopy at maturity | 35-45% |
| Walk to village center | <600 m for 90% of homes |
| Parking provision | 1.5-2.0 spaces per dwelling (on-plot) |
| Private garden as % of plot | 40-60% |

## 2. Urban Village

### Description

A medium-density, walkable neighborhood organized around a mixed-use village
center. The Urban Village model draws on the traditional European market town
and the New Urbanism movement: compact, mixed-use, with a clear center,
defined edges, and a connected street network. Buildings are predominantly
attached (terraces/rowhouses and small apartment buildings), creating a
continuous street wall that defines public space. The village center is a
genuine mixed-use environment with shops, cafes, offices, and civic uses at
ground level and housing above.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 15-60 ha
- Population: 2,000-8,000 residents
- Dwellings: 600-3,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 40-60 DU/ha
- FAR: 0.8-2.0
- Building height: 2-5 stories (predominantly 3-4 stories at the center,
  2-3 at edges)
- Site coverage: 45-60%
- Building types: terraced houses (rowhouses), small apartment buildings
  (4-12 units), live-work units, mansion blocks, mews houses

### Street Pattern

- Connected grid or modified grid with hierarchy
- Primary street (high street / main street): 20-24 m ROW, mixed-use frontage,
  on-street parking, wide sidewalks (3-4 m), cycle facilities
- Secondary streets: 16-20 m ROW, residential frontage, street trees
- Lanes and mews: 6-10 m ROW, rear access, garage courts, mews houses
- No cul-de-sacs; all streets connect to at least two other streets
- Target: intersection density >100 per km2

### Use Mix

- 70-80% residential
- 10-15% commercial (retail, food and beverage, offices in the village center)
- 5-10% civic/institutional (primary school, community center, place of worship)
- 5-10% public open space (village green, pocket parks, play areas)

### Public Space Strategy

- Village green or central square (0.3-1.0 ha) at the heart, surrounded by
  mixed-use buildings, designed for markets, festivals, and daily gathering
- Neighborhood park (1-3 ha) within 500 m walk of all homes
- Play areas distributed so every home is within 200 m of a play space
- Allotments or community gardens (0.2-0.5 ha per 1,000 residents)
- Green edges: a landscape buffer or park at the transition to surrounding
  countryside or lower-density areas

### Precedent Projects

- **Poundbury** (Dorchester, UK, 1993-ongoing): 160 ha at full build-out.
  Masterplanned by Leon Krier for the Duchy of Cornwall. Mixed-use village
  center, 35% affordable housing, traditional architectural character, no
  visible parking on street frontages.
- **Seaside** (Florida, USA, 1981): 32 ha, designed by Andres Duany and
  Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk. The foundational New Urbanist project. Type-based
  code, central square, beach pavilion, walkable street network.
- **Jakriborg** (Sweden, 1999): 7 ha, 600 units. Dense, traditional town
  character, central square, mixed uses, pedestrian-priority streets.

### Design Rules

1. Maximum block perimeter: 500 m; maximum block length: 130 m
2. Build-to line required on primary and secondary streets (no front setback)
3. Ground-floor retail required on both sides of the high street for at least
   200 m in each direction from the village center
4. Minimum 85% of building frontage on primary streets must be active
   (entries, shop windows, porches -- no blank walls or garage doors)
5. All parking concealed: rear courtyards, underground, or within mews
   blocks (no surface parking lots visible from public space)
6. Minimum building height on the high street: 3 stories (to ensure adequate
   street enclosure)
7. Residential buildings must address the street with front doors and windows
   (no side-on or rear-facing conditions on public frontages)

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 45-55 DU/ha |
| FAR (village center) | 1.5-2.0 |
| Green space per capita | 10-15 m2 |
| Walk to village center | <400 m for 80% of homes |
| Active ground-floor frontage | >85% on primary streets |
| Parking provision | 1.0-1.5 spaces per dwelling (concealed) |
| Affordable housing | 20-35% of total units |

## 3. Compact Neighborhood

### Description

A medium-high density, transit-served neighborhood designed around principles
of sustainability, walkability, and community. The Compact Neighborhood is
the contemporary European model for urban extensions and new neighborhoods,
combining efficient land use with high quality of life. Buildings are
predominantly multi-story apartment buildings (walk-up and mid-rise)
organized in perimeter blocks that create sheltered courtyards. Streets are
designed for people first, with generous sidewalks, protected cycling
facilities, and limited car access.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 20-80 ha
- Population: 5,000-20,000 residents
- Dwellings: 2,000-8,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 60-100 DU/ha
- FAR: 1.5-3.0
- Building height: 3-7 stories (predominantly 4-6 stories)
- Site coverage: 50-65%
- Building types: perimeter block apartments, courtyard apartments, stacked
  townhouses, point towers (occasional, at key locations), live-work units

### Street Pattern

- Regular grid or semi-regular grid, optimized for solar orientation
- Boulevard / primary street: 24-30 m ROW, tram or BRT if applicable,
  protected cycle tracks, double rows of trees
- Urban streets: 18-22 m ROW, on-street parking one side, protected cycle
  track or shared lane, street trees
- Residential streets: 14-18 m ROW, shared surface or 30 km/h zone, no
  parking or limited parking
- Green streets: 12-16 m ROW, pedestrian and cycle priority, stormwater
  management integrated
- Target: intersection density >120 per km2

### Use Mix

- 55-70% residential
- 15-25% commercial (neighborhood center with supermarket, shops, cafes,
  small offices, co-working spaces)
- 8-12% civic/institutional (schools, health center, community center,
  library)
- 10-15% open space and green infrastructure

### Public Space Strategy

- Central park or linear park (2-5 ha) as the primary open space, designed
  for multiple activities: sports, play, passive recreation, events
- Neighborhood square or plaza (0.3-0.8 ha) at the commercial center
- Pocket parks and play spaces within every 200-300 m
- Courtyards within perimeter blocks: semi-private green space for residents
  (typically 30-40% of block area)
- Green corridors connecting parks and providing stormwater management

### Precedent Projects

- **Vauban, Freiburg** (Germany, 1998-2006): 38 ha, 5,500 residents. Car-free
  residential streets, tram connection, passive house standard, community
  co-housing groups, 100% renewable energy from district CHP.
- **Hammarby Sjostad** (Stockholm, Sweden, 2000-2017): 200 ha, 26,000
  residents. Integrated environmental management (SymbioCity model), tram
  and ferry connections, 80% non-car mode share, brownfield regeneration.
- **Kronsberg** (Hannover, Germany, 1998): 70 ha, 6,000 residents. Low-energy
  standard for all buildings, integrated rainwater management, community
  engagement model, affordable housing mix.

### Design Rules

1. Maximum block perimeter: 450 m; minimum block width (face to face): 50 m
   (to ensure functional courtyard)
2. Perimeter block buildings must be continuous on at least three sides
   (openings permitted for pedestrian access and views into courtyard)
3. Building depth: 12-16 m to allow natural light and cross-ventilation to
   all apartments
4. Courtyard minimum dimension: 20 m x 20 m (to ensure adequate daylight
   and usable outdoor space)
5. Ground floor height minimum: 3.5 m clear (to allow flexible use:
   residential, commercial, community)
6. Car parking: maximum 0.8 spaces per dwelling, provided in underground
   garages or peripheral parking structures (not on residential streets)
7. Cycle parking: minimum 2 spaces per dwelling, secure and covered,
   located at building entries
8. All south-facing facades within the block must receive at least 2 hours
   of direct sun at winter solstice

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 70-90 DU/ha |
| FAR (overall) | 1.8-2.5 |
| Green space per capita | 9-14 m2 |
| Non-car mode share | >60% |
| Walk to transit | <400 m for 95% of homes |
| Walk to school | <800 m for 95% of homes |
| Energy performance | Passive house or near-zero-energy |
| Courtyard as % of block | 30-45% |

## 4. Urban Center / Downtown

### Description

A high-density, mixed-use district functioning as the civic and commercial
heart of a community or city. The Urban Center combines retail, office,
residential, civic, and cultural uses in a compact, intensely walkable
environment. Buildings are mid-rise to high-rise, with continuous active
ground floors creating a rich street-level experience. Public spaces are
formal and civic in character: plazas, promenades, and tree-lined boulevards
rather than parks and gardens.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 10-40 ha
- Population: 5,000-20,000 residents (plus significant daytime employment
  and visitor populations)
- Dwellings: 2,000-10,000 units
- Employment: 3,000-30,000 jobs

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 100-200 DU/ha
- FAR: 2.5-6.0
- Building height: 4-15 stories (with occasional towers to 25+ stories at
  key locations)
- Site coverage: 60-80%
- Building types: mid-rise mixed-use (podium + tower), urban apartment
  buildings, office buildings, civic buildings, market halls, parking
  structures wrapped with active uses

### Street Pattern

- Fine-grain grid with short blocks (60-100 m face length)
- Main street / high street: 24-30 m ROW, wide sidewalks (4-6 m), street
  trees, on-street parking (metered), transit priority
- Cross streets: 18-22 m ROW, sidewalks (3-4 m), street trees
- Pedestrian lanes / arcades: 6-10 m wide, pedestrian-only or shared surface,
  connecting to internal courtyards and secondary entrances
- Vehicular access to parking structures via rear lanes or cross streets
  (not from main street)
- Target: intersection density >150 per km2

### Use Mix

- 30-45% residential (predominantly apartments)
- 25-40% commercial (office, retail, food and beverage, hotel)
- 8-15% civic/institutional (government, cultural, education, health)
- 5-10% public space (plazas, streetscapes, pocket parks)
- 5-10% parking and infrastructure (structured, below grade, or wrapped)

### Public Space Strategy

- Central civic plaza (0.3-1.0 ha): hard-surfaced, designed for civic events,
  markets, performances, and daily life. Bordered by the most important civic
  and commercial buildings
- Secondary plazas at key intersections (0.1-0.3 ha each): themed (e.g.,
  restaurant plaza, arts plaza, market plaza)
- Promenades and boulevards: wide sidewalks with double tree rows, outdoor
  dining, and retail display
- Green squares: at least one formal green square (0.2-0.5 ha) for respite
  from the urban intensity
- Rooftop gardens and terraces: encouraged on all buildings over 4 stories

### Precedent Projects

- **Portland Pearl District** (Oregon, USA, 1998-ongoing): 120 ha brownfield
  regeneration. Fine-grain blocks (60 x 60 m), mixed-use mid-rise, Jamison
  Square and Tanner Springs Park, 40% affordable housing, streetcar-served.
- **King's Cross** (London, UK, 2007-2025): 27 ha former railyard. Granary
  Square, Coal Drops Yard, 50 buildings, 20 new streets, 10 public spaces,
  mixed-use with offices, housing, retail, university, cultural venues.
- **Vauban Quarter, Vienna** (Austria): multiple new urban quarters including
  Aspern Seestadt (240 ha, 20,000 residents, 20,000 jobs, lake-centered,
  U-Bahn connected) demonstrating contemporary urban center design.

### Design Rules

1. Maximum block length: 100 m (pedestrian permeability is paramount)
2. Active ground floor required on all street-facing facades: minimum 60%
   transparent facade between 0.5 m and 2.5 m height
3. Ground floor height: minimum 4.0 m clear (to accommodate retail/commercial)
4. Building entrances every 8-12 m along primary streets (to create fine-grain
   rhythm and maximize footfall for retail)
5. Tower separation: minimum 24 m face-to-face to ensure daylight and privacy
6. Wind mitigation required for all buildings over 30 m height (podiums,
   canopies, setbacks)
7. Service access from rear lanes only; no loading docks on primary streets
8. Parking structures must be wrapped with active uses on all public-facing
   frontages

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 120-180 DU/ha |
| FAR (overall) | 3.0-5.0 |
| Employment density | 200-500 jobs/ha |
| Active ground-floor frontage | >90% on primary streets |
| Walk Score | >90 |
| Parking provision | 0.3-0.6 spaces per dwelling |
| Public space as % of area | 15-25% |
| Transit headways | <5 minutes peak |

## 5. Transit Corridor

### Description

A linear high-density district organized along a transit line (tram, light
rail, BRT, or metro). Development is concentrated within a 400-800 m walkshed
of each station, creating a "string of pearls" pattern. Each station area has
its own character and mix: some are primarily residential, others commercial
or institutional. The corridor as a whole provides a diversity of environments
linked by fast, frequent transit. Between stations, density drops to allow
for green space, lower-density residential, or preservation of existing
character.

### Typical Scale

- Corridor length: 3-15 km
- Corridor width: 400-1,200 m (200-600 m each side of the transit line)
- Population: 10,000-80,000 residents (across the entire corridor)
- Dwellings per station area: 500-3,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 80-150 DU/ha (within 400 m of stations)
- FAR: 2.0-5.0 (within 400 m of stations), stepping down to 0.5-1.5 beyond
- Building height: 4-15 stories at stations, 2-4 stories between stations
- Site coverage: 55-70% at stations, 40-55% between
- Building types: mixed-use mid-rise and towers at stations, townhouses and
  walk-up apartments between stations

### Street Pattern

- Boulevard as the transit spine: 30-40 m ROW, dedicated transit lanes,
  protected cycle tracks, double tree rows, wide sidewalks
- Perpendicular streets connecting the boulevard to surrounding neighborhoods
- Grid pattern within station areas; more relaxed pattern between stations
- Station plazas at each transit stop: 0.2-0.5 ha, providing interchange
  space (bus, bike, taxi, ride-share) and public gathering space

### Use Mix

- 45-60% residential
- 20-30% commercial (concentrated at station areas)
- 8-12% civic/institutional (distributed along corridor, major facilities
  at selected stations)
- 10-15% open space and green infrastructure

### Public Space Strategy

- Station plazas (0.2-0.5 ha each) at every transit stop
- Linear park or greenway parallel to the transit corridor (minimum 15 m wide)
- Neighborhood parks (0.5-2 ha) between station areas, where density is lower
- Pocket parks and play spaces within 200 m of all residences
- Green bridges or underpasses connecting neighborhoods across the transit
  corridor (if the corridor is a barrier)

### Precedent Projects

- **Curitiba** (Brazil, 1970s-ongoing): the pioneer of BRT-oriented
  development. Five structural axes radiating from center, high-density
  mixed-use towers flanking the BRT corridor, lower density behind.
- **Arlington Rosslyn-Ballston Corridor** (Virginia, USA, 1970s-ongoing):
  5 km Metro corridor, 5 stations, 40,000+ residents, 100,000+ jobs. Each
  station has distinct character. "Bull's-eye" zoning concentrates density
  at stations with rapid falloff.
- **Finger Plan, Copenhagen** (Denmark, 1947-ongoing): regional transit
  corridors (S-tog fingers) with dense development at stations and green
  wedges between. The model for half a century of sustainable metropolitan
  growth.

### Design Rules

1. Maximum density within 400 m of station; reduce density by at least 30%
   between 400-800 m; transition to surrounding character beyond 800 m
2. Mixed-use ground floors required within 200 m of each station
3. Transit boulevard must have continuous street wall (minimum 70% frontage
   occupancy) within station areas
4. Perpendicular streets must provide clear sightlines to the transit station
   from at least 200 m away (wayfinding)
5. No surface parking within 200 m of transit stations; structured parking
   only, wrapped with active uses
6. Cycle parking: minimum 50 secure spaces at each transit stop, plus covered
   bike parking at all buildings
7. Maximum block perimeter within station areas: 400 m
8. Ground floor transparency: minimum 60% on transit boulevard

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Density within 400 m of station | 100-150 DU/ha |
| FAR within 400 m of station | 2.5-4.0 |
| Transit mode share | >40% for commute trips |
| Walk to transit | <400 m for 90% of homes in station areas |
| Parking at stations | 0.3-0.5 spaces per dwelling |
| Daily transit ridership per station | 3,000-10,000 |
| Mixed-use ground floor | 100% within 200 m of station |

## 6. Waterfront District

### Description

A medium-high density district that leverages the amenity of a waterfront
(river, lake, harbor, or coast) to create a distinctive urban environment.
The defining feature is public access to the water's edge: a continuous
promenade or boardwalk, complemented by public spaces, cultural venues, and
active ground-floor uses. Buildings are oriented to maximize water views
while maintaining permeability from the hinterland to the waterfront.
Building heights typically step up from the water's edge to protect the
public waterfront from wind and shadow.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 15-100 ha
- Waterfront length: 500 m - 5 km
- Population: 5,000-25,000 residents
- Dwellings: 2,000-12,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 60-120 DU/ha
- FAR: 1.5-4.0
- Building height: 3-12 stories (stepping up from the water's edge: 3-5
  stories on the waterfront, 6-12 stories on the second and third rows)
- Site coverage: 50-65%
- Building types: waterfront apartment blocks, mixed-use buildings with
  ground-floor restaurants and retail, converted warehouses and industrial
  buildings (if brownfield), point towers at key locations

### Street Pattern

- Primary waterfront promenade: 15-30 m wide, public access, no vehicular
  traffic (or very limited), continuous for the full length of the waterfront
- Perpendicular streets connecting the waterfront to the hinterland: 18-24 m
  ROW, framing views to the water from inland
- Parallel streets behind the waterfront: 18-22 m ROW, providing vehicular
  access, servicing, and secondary frontage
- Maximum spacing between perpendicular streets: 100 m (to maintain visual
  and physical permeability to the water)

### Use Mix

- 50-65% residential
- 20-30% commercial (restaurants, cafes, retail on waterfront; offices inland)
- 5-10% civic/cultural (museum, gallery, event space, maritime heritage)
- 10-15% public space (promenade, plazas, waterfront parks, jetties)

### Public Space Strategy

- Continuous waterfront promenade (minimum 10 m wide, ideally 15-20 m)
- Waterfront plazas at key points: at the terminus of perpendicular streets,
  at ferry landings, at heritage buildings (0.2-0.5 ha each)
- Harbor or canal basins retained as public water features where applicable
- At least one major waterfront park (1-3 ha) with active and passive areas
- Water access: steps, ramps, or beaches that allow people to physically
  touch or enter the water
- Floating elements: pontoons, floating gardens, houseboats (where permitted)

### Precedent Projects

- **HafenCity, Hamburg** (Germany, 2001-2030): 157 ha, 15,000 residents,
  45,000 jobs. Europe's largest inner-city waterfront regeneration. Flood-
  resilient ground floors (warft system), Elbphilharmonie concert hall,
  diverse architectural character across 10+ sub-quarters.
- **Nordhavn, Copenhagen** (Denmark, 2009-ongoing): 100 ha, 40,000 residents
  and workers. Islet structure creating multiple waterfronts, harbor baths,
  cycling superhighway, metro extension, adaptive reuse of grain silos.
- **Darling Harbour, Sydney** (Australia, 1984-ongoing): 60 ha waterfront
  regeneration. Convention center, maritime museum, public parks, Barangaroo
  headland park, mixed-use towers, continuous waterfront promenade.

### Design Rules

1. The waterfront promenade must be 100% publicly accessible, uninterrupted
   for the full length of the development (no gated enclaves or private
   stretches)
2. All ground-floor uses facing the waterfront must be active (restaurants,
   cafes, shops, galleries, cultural venues -- no residential ground floors
   on the waterfront)
3. Maximum building height on the first row (waterfront-facing) is limited
   to ensure the promenade receives at least 3 hours of direct sunlight at
   equinox
4. Perpendicular streets must frame views to the water; no building may
   completely block a view corridor from the hinterland to the waterfront
5. Flood resilience: all habitable space above the 100-year flood level plus
   0.5 m freeboard plus climate change allowance (check local projections)
6. Wind mitigation is essential on exposed waterfronts: podium buildings,
   canopies, and wind screens at outdoor dining areas
7. Heritage industrial structures (warehouses, cranes, bollards, rail tracks)
   should be retained and adaptively reused where possible

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 80-110 DU/ha |
| FAR (overall) | 2.0-3.5 |
| Public waterfront length | 100% of total waterfront |
| Promenade width | 15-20 m minimum |
| Active ground floor on waterfront | 100% |
| View corridors to water | Every 80-100 m |
| Walk to waterfront | <300 m for 90% of residents |
| Flood resilience | 100-year + 0.5 m + climate change |

## 7. Campus District

### Description

An institutional or innovation-focused district organized around open spaces
and anchored by one or more major institutions (university, hospital, research
park, corporate campus). The Campus District combines the spatial qualities
of a campus (generous landscapes, buildings as pavilions in the landscape)
with urban density and mix (housing, retail, services). The challenge is to
create a walkable, mixed-use neighborhood rather than a mono-functional
campus that dies at 5 pm.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 20-100 ha
- Population: 2,000-10,000 residents (plus 5,000-30,000 daytime population
  of workers and students)
- Dwellings: 800-4,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 30-80 DU/ha
- FAR: 0.5-2.5 (varying by sub-zone: lower FAR for institutional buildings
  with generous setbacks; higher FAR for mixed-use perimeter)
- Building height: 2-8 stories (institutional buildings often lower and more
  horizontal; mixed-use edges taller)
- Site coverage: 30-55%
- Building types: institutional buildings (labs, lecture halls, offices),
  apartment buildings, student housing, hotel/conference facilities,
  incubator/co-working spaces, live-work units

### Street Pattern

- Informal grid or campus layout organized around quadrangles and greens
- Primary boulevard connecting the campus to the city (24-30 m ROW)
- Internal campus streets may be pedestrian-priority or car-free
- Clear separation between service/loading access and pedestrian networks
- Generous pedestrian pathways (4-6 m) connecting buildings and open spaces

### Use Mix

- 25-40% institutional (teaching, research, healthcare)
- 25-40% residential (faculty, staff, student, and market housing)
- 10-20% commercial (retail, dining, hotel, conference, co-working)
- 15-25% open space (quadrangles, sports fields, landscaped grounds)

### Public Space Strategy

- Central quad or green (1-3 ha) as the ceremonial heart of the campus
- Secondary courtyards and gardens between building clusters (0.2-0.5 ha each)
- Sports and recreation fields (2-5 ha), shared between institution and
  community
- Innovation commons: outdoor collaboration spaces with wifi, power, seating,
  and shelter
- Transition zone: a mixed-use "main street" at the campus edge serving
  both the institution and the surrounding neighborhood

### Precedent Projects

- **MIT Kendall Square** (Cambridge, MA, USA): 10+ ha innovation district
  adjacent to MIT. Originally mono-functional industrial/office; now
  transformed into a vibrant mixed-use neighborhood with housing, ground-
  floor retail, open spaces, and innovation economy employers.
- **Stanford Research Park** (Palo Alto, CA, USA, 1951): 283 ha, one of the
  first university research parks. Originally low-density suburban campus;
  currently densifying with mixed-use, transit, and housing.
- **Novartis Campus** (Basel, Switzerland, 2001): 20 ha corporate campus
  designed by Vittorio Magnago Lampugnani. Urban grid, buildings by Pritzker-
  winning architects, internal campus streets, public river promenade.

### Design Rules

1. Institutional buildings should face public streets and open spaces (not
   present blank service facades to the public realm)
2. Ground-floor activation is required on the "main street" edge zone
3. Campus open spaces should be publicly accessible (not gated) unless
   security requirements demand otherwise
4. Parking should be in structures at the campus periphery, not in surface
   lots within the campus core
5. Housing should be integrated, not segregated, into the campus fabric
6. A 24-hour presence (housing, hotel, evening dining) is essential to avoid
   the empty-campus-after-dark problem
7. Wayfinding and signage must be clear and consistent across the campus

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density (residential parcels) | 50-75 DU/ha |
| FAR (overall) | 1.0-2.0 |
| Employment density | 150-400 jobs/ha (on institutional parcels) |
| Open space as % of site area | 25-35% |
| Walk to transit | <600 m for all buildings |
| Parking provision | 0.5-1.0 spaces per worker; 0.5-0.8 per dwelling |
| Active ground floor on main street | >80% |
| 24-hour occupation | Housing + hotel within 200 m of center |

## 8. Regeneration District

### Description

A brownfield or post-industrial district transformed into a mixed-use urban
neighborhood. Regeneration Districts are among the most compelling urban
design challenges: they must remediate contamination, repurpose industrial
heritage, create connectivity where barriers existed, and establish a new
identity while honoring the history of the place. The industrial heritage
(buildings, structures, infrastructure, materials, and memories) becomes the
unique design resource that distinguishes the district from generic new
development.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 10-80 ha
- Population: 3,000-20,000 residents
- Dwellings: 1,500-10,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 60-120 DU/ha
- FAR: 1.5-4.0
- Building height: 3-12 stories (new buildings); retained industrial
  structures often 2-5 stories
- Site coverage: 50-70%
- Building types: adaptive reuse of industrial buildings (lofts, studios,
  markets, cultural venues), new-build apartment buildings, mixed-use
  buildings, live-work units, maker spaces

### Street Pattern

- New grid overlay on the industrial layout, retaining key heritage routes
  (rail sidings as linear parks, quayside roads as promenades)
- Primary street connecting to surrounding urban fabric (24-30 m ROW)
- Heritage streets: retained industrial roads, often with setts or cobbles,
  integrated into the pedestrian network
- Green routes on former rail corridors (minimum 10 m wide)

### Use Mix

- 45-60% residential (loft apartments in converted buildings, new-build
  apartments)
- 15-25% commercial (creative industries, co-working, studios, tech, retail)
- 10-15% cultural/community (galleries, performance spaces, museums,
  community centers, maker spaces)
- 10-15% public space (parks on remediated land, waterfront promenades,
  rail trail parks)

### Public Space Strategy

- Retention of key industrial structures as landmarks and anchors for public
  space (gasometers, cooling towers, cranes, silos)
- Central public space incorporating a heritage element (a retained warehouse
  courtyard, a former loading dock, a rail turntable)
- Linear park on former rail corridor (connecting to citywide green network)
- Waterfront promenade if applicable (former dockside or canal)
- Remediated land as community gardens and urban agriculture (interim or
  permanent)

### Precedent Projects

- **King's Cross, London** (UK, 2007-2025): 27 ha former rail lands. Retained
  Grade I and II listed buildings, Granary Square, Coal Drops Yard, Central
  Saint Martins university, canal-side public spaces, 50 new and converted
  buildings.
- **Zollverein, Essen** (Germany, 2001-ongoing): 100 ha former coal mine and
  coking plant, UNESCO World Heritage Site. Adaptive reuse of industrial
  buildings for design, culture, and events. Rem Koolhaas and SANAA buildings.
- **Distillery District, Toronto** (Canada, 2003): 5 ha former Gooderham and
  Worts distillery. 47 heritage buildings adapted for galleries, restaurants,
  studios, and residential. Pedestrian-only district.

### Design Rules

1. Retain and adaptively reuse all heritage-listed structures. Where possible,
   retain unlisted but characterful industrial buildings as well.
2. New buildings must respond to the scale, materials, and character of
   retained heritage structures (not mimic, but respect and complement)
3. Industrial artifacts (cranes, rail tracks, machinery, signage) should be
   integrated into the public realm as placemaking elements
4. Contamination remediation strategy must be completed before development;
   use clean soil capping, containment, or treatment as appropriate
5. Creative and maker industries should be accommodated with appropriate
   building specifications (high ceilings, large floor plates, loading access,
   sound insulation)
6. Interim uses are essential during the long regeneration process: markets,
   events, pop-up retail, urban agriculture, art installations
7. Infrastructure may need to be built over or through contaminated or
   previously developed ground; additional geotechnical investigation is
   always required

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 75-110 DU/ha |
| FAR (overall) | 2.0-3.5 |
| Heritage structures retained | 80-100% of listed, 50-80% of unlisted |
| Creative/cultural space | 10-20% of total GFA |
| Remediation completion | 100% before occupation |
| Public space as % of site | 15-25% |
| Affordable housing | 20-35% (or per local policy) |
| Walk to transit | <500 m for 90% of homes |

## 9. Eco-District

### Description

A sustainability-focused district designed to achieve measurable environmental
performance targets, typically net-zero operational carbon, significant
reductions in embodied carbon, water self-sufficiency, zero waste, and high
biodiversity. The Eco-District applies advanced sustainability
technologies and design strategies at the neighborhood scale, demonstrating
that high environmental performance is compatible with high quality of life
and financial viability.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 10-60 ha
- Population: 3,000-15,000 residents
- Dwellings: 1,200-6,000 units

### Density and Built Form

- Net residential density: 50-100 DU/ha
- FAR: 1.0-3.0
- Building height: 3-8 stories (mid-rise is optimal for energy performance:
  sufficient density for district energy, low enough for natural ventilation
  and daylight)
- Site coverage: 40-60% (lower coverage allows for green infrastructure)
- Building types: passive house apartments, mass timber mid-rise, courtyard
  blocks with integrated energy systems, solar-optimized terraced housing,
  co-housing clusters

### Street Pattern

- Solar-optimized grid: streets oriented east-west to maximize south-facing
  building frontage (northern hemisphere) or north-facing (southern hemisphere)
- Green streets with bioswales and permeable paving (16-22 m ROW)
- Car-free residential streets: 10-14 m ROW, pedestrian and cycle only
- Mobility hub at the district edge: car-share, bike-share, e-scooter, last-
  mile delivery consolidation
- Maximum one vehicular access point per block (service and emergency)

### Use Mix

- 55-70% residential (optimized for energy performance)
- 10-15% commercial (local retail, co-working, food production, maker spaces)
- 8-12% community/energy infrastructure (district energy center, waste
  processing, water recycling, community center, school)
- 15-25% green infrastructure (parks, productive landscapes, biodiversity
  areas, stormwater systems)

### Public Space Strategy

- Productive landscapes: community gardens, orchards, urban farming (0.5-2 ha)
- Nature-rich parks: designed for biodiversity as well as recreation, with
  native planting, wildlife habitats, insect hotels, bird boxes
- Water features integrated with stormwater management: rain gardens, swales,
  constructed wetlands, retention ponds
- Car-free play streets: residential streets designed as shared spaces for
  children's play, socializing, and community events
- District energy infrastructure as visible, didactic public feature (solar
  canopies, wind indicators, energy dashboards)

### Precedent Projects

- **Vauban, Freiburg** (Germany, 1998-2006): 38 ha, 5,500 residents. Passive
  house and plus-energy buildings, district CHP, car-free streets, community
  co-housing, 100% renewable energy, 76% non-car mode share.
- **BedZED** (London, UK, 2002): 1.7 ha, 100 homes. Beddington Zero Energy
  Development. Combined heat and power, super-insulation, south-facing
  sunspaces, green roofs, car club, water recycling.
- **Masdar City** (Abu Dhabi, UAE, 2008-ongoing): 600 ha (only partially
  built). Designed by Foster + Partners. Net-zero carbon and waste targets,
  personal rapid transit, solar-powered, compact urban form, shaded streets
  oriented to capture breezes.

### Design Rules

1. All buildings must achieve passive house standard or equivalent (heating
   demand <15 kWh/m2/year)
2. On-site renewable energy must meet at least 80% of operational energy
   demand (solar PV on all suitable roofs and facades, plus district energy)
3. Rainwater harvesting and greywater recycling required; target 50% reduction
   in mains water consumption
4. Permeable surface coverage: minimum 60% of site area (green roofs count)
5. Car ownership: maximum 0.5 spaces per dwelling; car-share provided at
   1 car per 10 dwellings minimum
6. Embodied carbon: all buildings to report whole-life carbon and target
   <500 kgCO2e/m2 (structure and envelope)
7. Biodiversity net gain: at least 20% increase over pre-development baseline
8. Construction waste: at least 95% diversion from landfill

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Net density | 65-90 DU/ha |
| FAR (overall) | 1.5-2.5 |
| Operational carbon | Net-zero (Scope 1 and 2) |
| Embodied carbon | <500 kgCO2e/m2 |
| Renewable energy on-site | >80% of demand |
| Water consumption reduction | >50% vs. conventional |
| Non-car mode share | >70% |
| Permeable surface | >60% |
| Biodiversity net gain | >20% |
| Green space per capita | 12-20 m2 |

## 10. Informal Settlement Upgrading

### Description

The incremental improvement of an existing informal settlement through
community-led planning, targeted infrastructure investment, and in-situ
upgrading rather than demolition and displacement. This typology applies to
the majority-world context where informality is the dominant mode of
urbanization. The design approach respects existing social structures, land
tenure arrangements, and livelihood patterns while improving safety, health,
connectivity, and environmental conditions. The goal is to formalize and
improve, not to erase and rebuild.

### Typical Scale

- Site area: 5-50 ha
- Population: 2,000-50,000 residents (informal settlements are often very
  dense)
- Dwellings: 500-15,000 units (highly variable)

### Density and Built Form

- Existing net density: highly variable, often 100-600 DU/ha (much of it in
  single-story structures with very small plot sizes)
- Target density after upgrading: maintain or moderately increase density
  (displacement minimized; densification occurs vertically as residents
  improve their houses from 1 to 2-3 stories)
- FAR: 0.5-2.5 (variable by zone within the settlement)
- Building height: 1-4 stories (incremental self-build)
- Building types: self-built houses (incrementally upgraded), core houses
  (providing structure and services that residents complete), small apartment
  buildings along main streets, community buildings

### Street Pattern

- Retain and formalize the existing organic street network where possible
- Widen the most critical paths to a minimum 6 m (to allow emergency vehicle
  access, utility installation, and daylight/ventilation to buildings)
- Create 1-2 primary through-routes connecting the settlement to the formal
  city (minimum 10-12 m ROW) for transit, emergency access, and commerce
- Formalize secondary paths at 3-4 m width for pedestrian circulation and
  small utility vehicles
- Avoid wholesale grid imposition that would require mass demolition; instead,
  surgical interventions to improve connectivity

### Use Mix

- 70-85% residential (self-built housing, often with home-based enterprises)
- 10-15% commercial (informal markets formalized, main-street retail, home-
  based businesses supported)
- 5-10% community infrastructure (school, health post, community center,
  water and sanitation points)

### Public Space Strategy

- Community gathering spaces at intersections of primary paths (0.05-0.2 ha):
  flexible, multi-purpose, community-managed
- Children's play areas: safe, fenced spaces within 200 m of all homes
  (particularly critical given the lack of private outdoor space)
- Market space: formalized open-air market with basic infrastructure (drainage,
  shelter, water, waste collection)
- Community garden / productive space where land permits
- Stormwater management: open channels formalized with stone or concrete lining
  along primary routes; community maintained

### Precedent Projects

- **Medellin Comunas** (Colombia, 2004-ongoing): integrated urban projects
  (PUI) combining public buildings (library parks, schools, cable cars) with
  street improvements, public spaces, and housing upgrading in hillside
  informal settlements. Transformed safety and social cohesion.
- **Kigali, Rwanda** (2008-ongoing): citywide upgrading program combining
  in-situ upgrading of unplanned settlements with new affordable housing.
  Community-based mapping, incremental infrastructure, green public spaces.
- **Orangi Pilot Project** (Karachi, Pakistan, 1980-ongoing): community-
  managed sanitation program covering 1 million+ residents. Residents self-
  finance and build lane-level sewers; government provides trunk
  infrastructure. Model for community-led infrastructure delivery.

### Design Rules

1. Minimize displacement: target less than 10% of existing households
   relocated (only where absolutely necessary for infrastructure)
2. Community participation in all design decisions (community mapping,
   participatory planning, resident approval for interventions)
3. Secure tenure: land title regularization or community land trust
   mechanisms before physical upgrading
4. Infrastructure priorities (in order): water supply, sanitation, drainage,
   electricity, paved access, solid waste management
5. All interventions must be incrementally buildable (no big-bang projects
   that require complete area clearance)
6. Home-based enterprises must be accommodated and supported (not zoned out)
7. Main-street mixed-use frontage should be formalized and supported with
   proper infrastructure (drainage, paving, lighting, waste collection)
8. Emergency access: at least one 6 m wide route must reach within 50 m of
   every dwelling

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Households displaced | <10% |
| Access to improved water | 100% within 200 m |
| Access to improved sanitation | 100% (individual or shared, not open) |
| Emergency vehicle access | Within 50 m of 100% of dwellings |
| Public space per capita | Minimum 3 m2 (recognizing density constraints) |
| Tenure security | 100% of households with documented tenure |
| Community facilities | Health post and school within 800 m |
| Paved primary routes | 100% |
| Stormwater drainage | 100% of primary routes with functional drainage |
| Community participation | Documented consent for all interventions |
