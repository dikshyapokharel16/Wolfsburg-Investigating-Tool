# Masterplan Design Process -- Detailed Methodology

This reference provides the complete phase-by-phase methodology for masterplan
design. Each phase is described with its purpose, inputs, process, outputs,
decision points, and common pitfalls. Supplementary sections cover design
charrette facilitation, vision-to-form translation, and concept diagram
conventions.

---

## Phase 1: Site Understanding (Deep Dive)

### Purpose

Establish a thorough, evidence-based understanding of the site and its context
before any design decisions are made. The site analysis is the foundation of
every subsequent phase; errors or omissions here propagate through the entire
masterplan.

### Comprehensive Site Analysis Checklist

**Physical Context**
- [ ] Site boundary surveyed and georeferenced
- [ ] Topographic survey or DEM obtained (minimum 1 m contours)
- [ ] Slope analysis completed (0-2%, 2-5%, 5-10%, 10-15%, >15%)
- [ ] Aspect map generated (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW)
- [ ] Hydrology mapped (watercourses, flood zones, drainage direction)
- [ ] Geotechnical data obtained (soil type, bearing capacity, contamination)
- [ ] Vegetation survey completed (canopy cover, significant trees, habitats)
- [ ] Existing structures documented (buildings, walls, infrastructure)

**Infrastructure**
- [ ] Water supply: nearest main, capacity, pressure
- [ ] Sanitary sewer: nearest main, depth, capacity, treatment plant headroom
- [ ] Stormwater: drainage system type (combined/separate), capacity
- [ ] Electrical: nearest substation, available capacity (kVA)
- [ ] Gas: nearest main, capacity
- [ ] Telecommunications: fiber, 5G coverage, data capacity
- [ ] District heating/cooling: availability and capacity
- [ ] Utility easements mapped

**Regulatory**
- [ ] Current zoning designation and permitted uses
- [ ] Density limits (FAR, DU/ha, height, coverage, setbacks)
- [ ] Parking requirements (minimums, maximums)
- [ ] Overlay districts (historic, TOD, environmental, design review)
- [ ] Comprehensive plan future land use designation
- [ ] Pending rezoning or plan amendments
- [ ] Building code requirements (fire, energy, accessibility)
- [ ] Environmental regulations (wetlands, endangered species, air quality)

**Environmental Conditions**
- [ ] Solar analysis at solstices and equinox (9:00, 12:00, 15:00)
- [ ] Wind rose data obtained (10+ year records preferred)
- [ ] Noise mapping from traffic, rail, air, industrial sources
- [ ] Air quality data (PM2.5, NO2, ozone)
- [ ] Flood risk assessment (100-year and 500-year events)
- [ ] Climate projections (temperature, precipitation, extreme events at 2050)

**Connections and Context**
- [ ] Surrounding street network mapped with classifications and ADT
- [ ] Transit routes, stops, frequencies documented
- [ ] Cycling infrastructure mapped within 2 km
- [ ] Pedestrian network and barriers identified
- [ ] Adjacent land uses documented
- [ ] Walk-shed analysis at 400 m and 800 m completed
- [ ] Key amenities within walk-shed mapped (schools, parks, shops, transit)

**Community and Market**
- [ ] Demographics within walk-shed documented
- [ ] Income profile and housing affordability data obtained
- [ ] Property market data (values, rents, absorption rates)
- [ ] Development pipeline within 2 km documented
- [ ] Stakeholder map created (landowners, community groups, agencies)

### Synthesis Methods

**Constraint-Opportunity Overlay**: Layer all constraint maps (flood, slope,
easements, setbacks, heritage, contamination) into a single composite. The
resulting map shows four categories:
1. **Unbuildable**: areas with absolute constraints (flood plain, steep slopes,
   protected habitats, easements). These become open space or infrastructure.
2. **Constrained**: areas with conditional constraints (moderate slopes,
   archaeological potential, noise zones). Buildable with mitigation.
3. **Unconstrained**: areas with no identified constraints. Primary buildable
   land.
4. **Opportunity zones**: areas where multiple positive factors converge
   (good access, views, solar exposure, proximity to amenities). These are
   prime locations for the center and highest-value development.

**SWOT to Design Brief**: Translate each SWOT item into a design directive:
- Strength: "The site has excellent transit access" --> "Reduce parking ratios
  to 0.5 spaces per dwelling and orient density toward the transit stop."
- Weakness: "Northern half of site is in the 100-year flood plain" --> "Designate
  the northern half as a linear park with integrated stormwater detention."
- Opportunity: "Adjacent university is expanding" --> "Include student housing
  and research/incubator space in the program mix."
- Threat: "Highway expansion may increase noise on the western edge" --> "Place
  commercial/parking structures as a noise buffer on the western edge; locate
  residential behind."

## Phase 2: Vision and Program Definition (Deep Dive)

### Vision Statement Crafting

A masterplan vision statement should be one paragraph (3-5 sentences) that
answers four questions:
1. **What is this place?** (a transit-oriented neighborhood, a waterfront
   district, an innovation campus, etc.)
2. **Who is it for?** (young families, knowledge workers, a diverse mixed-income
   community, etc.)
3. **What makes it special?** (its relationship to the river, its sustainable
   design, its cultural vibrancy, etc.)
4. **What will success look like in 20 years?** (a thriving walkable community
   of 10,000 residents with a vibrant high street, etc.)

**Example vision statement**: "Riverside Quarter will be a walkable, transit-
connected neighborhood of 8,000 residents on the banks of the River Avon.
Designed for a diverse community of all ages and incomes, the neighborhood will
be defined by its generous public waterfront promenade, its tree-lined streets,
and its commitment to net-zero carbon. By 2045, Riverside Quarter will be
recognized as one of the most desirable and sustainable neighborhoods in the
region, with a thriving village center, excellent schools, and direct access
to the city center by tram."

### Stakeholder Engagement Methods

**Pre-Design Phase (before charrette)**:
- One-on-one interviews with key stakeholders (landowners, council members,
  community leaders): 30-60 minutes each, semi-structured, focused on
  aspirations and concerns
- Online survey for broader community: 10-15 questions covering preferred uses,
  character, priorities, concerns. Target 200+ responses for statistical
  relevance
- Site walk with community: guided walk documenting local knowledge, stories,
  preferences, pain points

**During Design (charrette)**:
- See the Design Charrette Facilitation Guide below

**Post-Design (review)**:
- Public exhibition of draft masterplan (physical and online): 2-4 weeks
- Feedback forms (structured: rate satisfaction with each element 1-5, plus
  open comments)
- Public meeting for presentation and Q&A: maximum 2 hours, structured format
  with small-group discussions
- Youth engagement workshop: separate session with age-appropriate methods
  (model building, drawing, photo surveys)

### Program Brief Development

The program brief quantifies the masterplan content. Develop it through the
following sequence:

**Step 1: Determine target population**
- Input: site area (net developable hectares), context density (from parameter
  matrix), market demand, policy targets
- Formula: target population = net developable area (ha) x target density
  (DU/ha) x average household size
- Cross-check: compare with regional growth projections and market absorption
  rate. A 20 ha site at 60 DU/ha with 2.3 persons per household yields
  approximately 1,200 dwellings and 2,760 residents

**Step 2: Calculate residential GFA**
- Formula: residential GFA = number of dwellings x average dwelling size (m2)
- Typical average dwelling sizes: studio 35-45 m2, 1-bed 50-65 m2, 2-bed
  70-90 m2, 3-bed 90-120 m2, 4-bed 120-160 m2
- Apply a unit mix (e.g., 10% studio, 25% 1-bed, 35% 2-bed, 20% 3-bed,
  10% 4-bed) based on demographics and market analysis
- Add common areas: typically 15-20% of net residential area for corridors,
  lobbies, plant rooms

**Step 3: Calculate non-residential GFA**
- Commercial/retail: 2-5 m2 per resident for neighborhood-serving retail,
  higher for district or city-serving centers
- Office: variable based on market demand and employment targets. 20 m2 per
  worker is a standard planning assumption
- Community facilities: 1.5-3.0 m2 per resident (schools, health, community
  centers, libraries, sports)
- Apply the use mix targets from the parameter matrix (Section 3 of SKILL.md)

**Step 4: Calculate infrastructure and open space**
- Streets: 20-32% of gross site area (varies by context)
- Public open space: 10-20% of gross site area (varies by context, minimum
  9 m2 per person per WHO)
- Community facilities land: based on facility sizes and population triggers
- Utilities: trunk infrastructure corridors, substations, pumping stations

**Step 5: Verify feasibility**
- Total GFA should not exceed the zoning envelope (max FAR x net developable
  area)
- Infrastructure capacity must support the target population
- Market absorption rate should be consistent with phasing timeline (typically
  50-200 units per year for a single development)

### Establishing Precedents

Select 3-5 precedent projects that share key characteristics with the proposed
masterplan. For each precedent, document:
- Project name, location, architect/planner, year
- Site area, program, density, population
- Key design strategies that are relevant to the current project
- Lessons learned (what worked well, what did not)
- Quantitative metrics for benchmarking (density, FAR, green space per capita,
  parking ratio, use mix)

## Phase 3: Structuring Framework (Deep Dive)

### Overlay Mapping Technique

The structuring framework emerges from overlaying three systems:

1. **Movement overlay**: draw all primary and secondary routes as lines of
   varying weight (heavier = more important). Mark transit stops as nodes.
   Mark connections to surroundings as arrows.
2. **Green-blue overlay**: draw all parks, corridors, waterways, and ecological
   features as shaded areas and lines. Mark stormwater flow direction.
3. **Built form overlay**: mark centers as concentrated dots. Mark density
   gradient as concentric bands or hatching. Mark edges and transitions.

When the three overlays are composited, the spatial structure of the masterplan
becomes visible. The center is where movement, green space, and density converge.
The neighborhoods are defined by the spaces between primary routes and green
corridors. The character areas emerge from the intersection of density zones
and landscape features.

### Corridor Identification

Corridors are the linear elements that organize the masterplan. Three types:

1. **Movement corridors**: primary streets and transit routes. They carry
   people, goods, and utilities. Frontage along movement corridors is the
   most valuable and should be activated with commercial uses.
2. **Green corridors**: linear parks, greenways, stream corridors. They carry
   stormwater, ecology, and recreation. Frontage along green corridors is
   highly desirable for residential uses.
3. **Interface corridors**: edges where the site meets a different condition
   (highway, rail line, industrial area, existing neighborhood). These require
   careful treatment -- buffering from negative adjacencies, transitioning to
   existing character, creating gateways.

### Center Identification

Every masterplan needs at least one center. For larger sites, a hierarchy of
centers creates a rich urban experience.

**Primary center**: the heart of the masterplan. Located at the convergence of
primary routes, preferably at or near transit. Contains the main public space
(plaza or green), the highest density and tallest buildings, the civic anchor
(community center, library, market hall), and the greatest mix of uses.

**Secondary centers (neighborhood nodes)**: smaller focal points for residential
neighborhoods. Typically at the intersection of a primary and secondary route.
Contains a small public space (pocket park or small plaza), convenience retail
(cafe, corner store), and a community facility (playground, community room).

**Edge centers**: where the site meets an existing high street or commercial
area. Development here should reinforce and complement the existing center
rather than compete with it.

### Edge Treatment

The edges of a masterplan are as important as the center. Five edge conditions:

1. **Street edge**: the site boundary is a street shared with the existing
   neighborhood. Match the setback, height, and character of the existing
   street. Do not turn your back to the existing neighbors.
2. **Park edge**: the site boundary is a park or open space. Orient buildings
   to face the park with active ground floors. Maximize frontage.
3. **Barrier edge**: the site boundary is a highway, rail line, or industrial
   area. Buffer with commercial buildings, parking structures, or landscape
   berms. Place residential behind the buffer.
4. **Waterfront edge**: the site boundary is a river, lake, or sea. Create a
   generous public promenade (minimum 10 m wide). All waterfront must be
   publicly accessible. Avoid privatizing the water's edge.
5. **Transition edge**: the site boundary is a low-density residential area.
   Step down height and density to match the existing scale. Use 2-3 story
   buildings at the boundary, stepping up to taller buildings further into
   the site.

## Phases 4-7: Cross-Skill Integration

Phases 4 through 7 are primarily executed through specialized skills. The
masterplan designer's role in these phases is integration -- ensuring that
each specialized output reinforces the overall structuring framework.

### Phase 4: Street Network Design

Invoke `street-design` with the following inputs:
- Primary movement structure from Phase 3
- Transect zone map with target street types per zone
- Climate data (prevailing wind, solar orientation)
- Target block dimensions from the parameter matrix

Integration checkpoints:
- [ ] Street hierarchy is consistent with the movement framework
- [ ] Cross-sections accommodate all modes (vehicles, transit, cycling,
      pedestrians, trees, utilities)
- [ ] Street tree planting is continuous and achieves 30% canopy target
- [ ] Utility corridors fit within the right-of-way
- [ ] Emergency vehicle access is ensured to all blocks
- [ ] Street orientation supports solar access and wind management

### Phase 5: Block and Parcel Layout

Invoke `block-and-density` with the following inputs:
- Street network from Phase 4
- Transect zone and density targets for each block
- Building typology preferences from the neighborhood typology catalog

Integration checkpoints:
- [ ] Block dimensions accommodate the assigned typology
- [ ] Block interiors have adequate courtyard or garden space
- [ ] Parcel sizes allow independent development
- [ ] Building envelopes respect solar access rights
- [ ] Parking is resolved (on-street, underground, or in structures)

### Phase 6: Land Use and Program Distribution

Invoke `mixed-use-programming` with the following inputs:
- Block plan with typologies from Phase 5
- Program brief from Phase 2
- Use mix targets from the parameter matrix

Integration checkpoints:
- [ ] Total GFA by use matches the program brief (within 5%)
- [ ] Retail and commercial uses are concentrated at the center and along
      primary streets (not dispersed to the point of non-viability)
- [ ] Civic facilities are located per population trigger thresholds
- [ ] Ground-floor activation strategy is applied to all active frontage streets
- [ ] Employment land is accessible by transit

### Phase 7: Public Space Network

Invoke `public-space-design` with the following inputs:
- Green-blue framework from Phase 3
- Population distribution from Phases 5-6
- Climate data (sun, wind, precipitation)

Integration checkpoints:
- [ ] Every dwelling is within 500 m of a park (at least 0.5 ha)
- [ ] Every dwelling is within 200 m of a pocket park or playground
- [ ] Central public space is sized and proportioned appropriately
- [ ] Stormwater management is integrated into public spaces
- [ ] Green space per capita meets or exceeds 9 m2 (WHO standard)
- [ ] Tree canopy target of 30% is achievable with the proposed planting plan

## Phase 8: Phasing and Implementation (Deep Dive)

### Phasing Logic

The phasing plan determines the order in which the masterplan is built. The
optimal phasing strategy balances four objectives:

1. **Technical necessity**: infrastructure must precede development. Streets
   and utilities must be built before the buildings they serve.
2. **Market viability**: each phase must be commercially viable on its own.
   The first phase must create a desirable address that attracts buyers and
   tenants. Subsequent phases build on established demand.
3. **Community building**: each phase should include a mix of uses and public
   spaces that create a functioning community. Avoid building all the housing
   first and the amenities last.
4. **Risk management**: front-load the most certain demand (typically housing)
   and defer the most speculative uses (typically office or hotel) to later
   phases when the address is established.

### Infrastructure Sequencing

**Phase 0 (Enabling Works)**: 12-24 months before first occupancy
- Site remediation (if brownfield): decontamination, demolition, grading
- Trunk infrastructure: primary water main, sewer trunk, electrical substation,
  gas main extension, telecommunications conduit
- Primary roads: at least the first primary road connecting the site to the
  existing road network
- Stormwater: primary detention/retention facilities, outfall connections
- First park or open space: a completed, landscaped public space that signals
  the quality and intent of the development

**Phase 1 (Catalyst)**: Years 2-5
- Secondary infrastructure serving the first development parcels
- Complete the central public space
- First residential buildings (target 300-500 units for critical mass)
- First commercial/retail space (target 1,000-3,000 m2, pre-leased if possible)
- Temporary uses on remaining parcels (markets, community gardens, events)
- Public transport service (if applicable)

**Phase 2+ (Growth)**: Years 5-15
- Extend infrastructure to next development parcels
- Trigger-based community facilities (school at 3,000 residents, community
  center at 5,000 residents)
- Diversify housing types (if Phase 1 focused on apartments, Phase 2 adds
  townhouses or vice versa)
- Expand commercial and employment uses as the address matures

### Financial Modeling Basics

A masterplan financial model is not a detailed feasibility study but a high-
level cash flow model that tests whether the phasing strategy is viable.

**Revenue sources**:
- Land sales or ground leases to developers (primary revenue in most models)
- Developer contributions (impact fees, infrastructure levies, community
  benefit agreements)
- Tax increment financing (if the project generates new tax revenue in a
  designated TIF district)
- Grants and subsidies (infrastructure grants, affordable housing subsidies,
  environmental remediation grants)

**Cost categories**:
- Land acquisition (if not already owned)
- Site remediation and preparation
- Infrastructure (roads, utilities, stormwater, parks)
- Planning and design (typically 5-8% of infrastructure costs)
- Finance costs (interest during construction, typically 5-8% per annum)
- Management and administration (typically 3-5% of total costs)

**Viability test**: total revenue from land sales must exceed total costs
(including finance charges) by a sufficient margin (typically 15-20% profit
on cost for private developers, or break-even for public agencies).

### Governance Frameworks

**Public-led development**: a public agency (municipality, development
corporation, land authority) acquires the land, masterplans the site, installs
infrastructure, and sells development parcels to private builders. The public
agency controls quality through design codes and parcel sale conditions.
Examples: HafenCity Hamburg, King's Cross London (public land, private
development partner).

**Private-led development**: a private landowner or developer masterplans and
develops the site, working within the regulatory framework. The public sector
approves plans, issues permits, and may contribute infrastructure. Risk and
reward are primarily private. Examples: most suburban subdivisions, Battersea
Power Station London.

**Public-Private Partnership (PPP)**: a joint venture between a public agency
and a private developer. The public sector typically contributes land and
regulatory support; the private sector contributes capital and development
expertise. Governance is shared through a joint venture board. Examples:
Aspern Seestadt Vienna, Orestad Copenhagen.

**Community Land Trust (CLT)**: a non-profit community organization owns the
land in perpetuity, leasing it to homeowners, businesses, and community
organizations. Ensures long-term affordability and community control. Can
be combined with other delivery mechanisms. Examples: Champlain Housing
Trust (Burlington VT), London Community Land Trust.

### Delivery Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Scale | Risk Profile | Public Control | Pace |
|-----------|-------|-------------|----------------|------|
| Single master developer | Any | High (concentrated) | Low | Fast |
| Parceled to multiple developers | >10 ha | Distributed | Medium | Medium |
| Public development corporation | >20 ha | Public bears | High | Slow |
| PPP joint venture | >15 ha | Shared | Medium | Medium |
| Community land trust | Any | Community bears | Very high | Slow |
| Self-build / custom-build | <5 ha or parcels | Individual | Medium (via design code) | Variable |

## Design Charrette Facilitation Guide

A design charrette is an intensive, multi-day collaborative design workshop
that compresses months of work into days. It is the most effective method for
developing a masterplan vision and initial spatial framework with stakeholder
buy-in.

### Three-Day Charrette Structure

**Day 1: Listen and Learn**

| Time | Activity | Participants | Outputs |
|------|----------|-------------|---------|
| 09:00-10:00 | Welcome and briefing | All | Shared understanding of objectives |
| 10:00-12:00 | Site walk and observation | All | Site photos, notes, sketches |
| 12:00-13:00 | Lunch (working) | All | Informal discussion |
| 13:00-15:00 | Stakeholder presentations | Presenters + all | Key issues and aspirations documented |
| 15:00-17:00 | Small group workshops (5-8 per group): "What should this place be?" | All, facilitated | Flip charts with priorities, ideas, concerns |
| 17:00-18:00 | Report back and synthesis | All | Consolidated list of themes and priorities |

**Day 2: Design**

| Time | Activity | Participants | Outputs |
|------|----------|-------------|---------|
| 09:00-09:30 | Recap Day 1 findings | All | Shared starting point |
| 09:30-12:00 | Design session 1: spatial structure (movement, green-blue, built form frameworks drawn on trace over base map) | Design team + stakeholder representatives | 2-3 alternative spatial frameworks |
| 12:00-13:00 | Pin-up and critique of alternatives | All | Preferred direction identified |
| 13:00-15:00 | Design session 2: refine preferred direction, test density, locate uses | Design team + stakeholder representatives | Single refined framework plan |
| 15:00-17:00 | Design session 3: character areas, key spaces, cross-sections | Design team | Character area descriptions and key sections |
| 17:00-18:00 | Internal review | Design team only | List of unresolved issues |

**Day 3: Resolve and Present**

| Time | Activity | Participants | Outputs |
|------|----------|-------------|---------|
| 09:00-12:00 | Design resolution: complete masterplan drawing, calculate metrics, prepare presentation | Design team | Draft masterplan with metrics |
| 12:00-13:00 | Lunch and rehearsal | Design team | Presentation ready |
| 13:00-14:30 | Public presentation of draft masterplan | All + broader public | Presented plan |
| 14:30-15:30 | Q&A and feedback collection | All | Feedback forms collected |
| 15:30-16:00 | Wrap-up and next steps | All | Agreed actions and timeline |

### Participant Roles

- **Lead designer**: runs the design sessions, makes real-time design decisions,
  draws the plan. Must be experienced in urban design and able to work fast.
- **Facilitator**: manages the workshop process, keeps time, ensures all voices
  are heard. Should not be the lead designer (different skill sets).
- **Technical advisors**: transport engineer, landscape architect, environmental
  specialist, cost estimator. Available for quick consultations during design.
- **Stakeholder representatives**: 2-4 community members, council members,
  landowner representatives who participate in design sessions (not just
  presentations).
- **Recorder**: documents everything -- photographs, notes, audio recording
  (with consent). Produces the charrette record.

## Vision-to-Form Translation Methodology

Converting client aspirations and community input into spatial parameters is
one of the most critical skills in masterplan design. This methodology provides
a structured approach.

### Translation Table

| Aspiration | Spatial Parameter | Design Response |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| "Walkable" | Max block perimeter 400 m, max block length 120 m | Fine-grain street grid, active ground floors, no cul-de-sacs |
| "Green" | Min 30% open space, 30% canopy, 9+ m2 green/person | Park within 500 m of all homes, tree-lined streets, green roofs |
| "Vibrant" | Min 20% non-residential use, active ground floors on primary streets | Mixed-use center, ground-floor retail requirements, evening economy |
| "Family-friendly" | Min 30% 3+ bed units, playground per 500 residents | Larger dwellings, ground-floor family units with gardens, school site |
| "Affordable" | Min 20-30% affordable units, mixed tenure | Tenure-blind design, cross-subsidy from commercial, CLT parcels |
| "Sustainable" | Net-zero operational carbon, 50% mode shift from car | Passive house standard, district energy, transit investment, low parking |
| "Connected" | Walk Score >70, Transit Score >50 | Grid connectivity index >1.4, transit route through site, cycling network |
| "Safe" | Natural surveillance, clear sightlines, active edges | Perimeter blocks, windows on streets, no blank walls, good lighting |
| "Distinctive" | Named character areas, landmark buildings, local materials | Design code with character area specifications, artist commissions |
| "Resilient" | 100-year flood protection, urban heat island mitigation | Blue-green infrastructure, permeable surfaces, shade, passive survivability |

### Process

1. Collect aspirations from the client brief and stakeholder engagement
2. Identify the top 5-8 aspirations (those mentioned most frequently and
   most emphatically)
3. For each aspiration, identify the corresponding spatial parameters from
   the translation table (or create new ones)
4. Check for conflicts between parameters (e.g., "affordable" may conflict
   with "low density" if land costs are high)
5. Resolve conflicts through design (e.g., achieve affordability through
   efficient building types rather than reducing density)
6. Embed the resolved parameters into the design brief as quantified targets

## Concept Diagram Conventions

Concept diagrams communicate the spatial logic of the masterplan before detailed
plans are drawn. They are essential for stakeholder communication and design
team alignment. When describing a concept diagram in text (as this skill does),
use the following conventions.

### Diagram Elements

| Element | Representation | Meaning |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| Primary route | Bold line with arrow | Main movement corridor, direction of flow |
| Secondary route | Medium line | Supporting street |
| Pedestrian route | Dashed line | Walking path or pedestrianized street |
| Green corridor | Wavy line or shaded band | Linear open space, stream, greenway |
| Center node | Filled circle (large) | Primary center, highest density |
| Secondary node | Filled circle (small) | Neighborhood node, secondary center |
| Gateway | Triangle or chevron | Entry point to the development |
| Density gradient | Concentric arcs or hatching (dense to sparse) | Decreasing density from center |
| View corridor | Arrow with eye symbol | Important view to preserve or create |
| Sun path | Arc with compass bearing | Solar orientation for design response |
| Wind direction | Arrow with speed notation | Prevailing wind for shelter design |
| Barrier | Thick dashed line with X marks | Highway, rail, or other barrier to cross |
| Water body | Wavy outline, shaded | River, lake, wetland |

### How to Describe a Concept Diagram in Text

When generating a masterplan in text form, describe the concept diagram using
cardinal directions and relative positions. Example:

"The masterplan is organized around a central east-west boulevard connecting
the existing high street to the west with the riverfront park to the east.
The primary center is located at the midpoint of this boulevard, where it
intersects with a north-south green corridor following the existing stream.
Density is highest at this central intersection (T5, up to 8 stories) and
decreases outward: T4 zones to the north and south within 300 m of the
center, transitioning to T3 at the site edges where the development meets
the existing low-rise residential neighborhood. A secondary neighborhood
node is located at the northern end of the green corridor, where a small
plaza and convenience retail serve the northern residential area."

This level of spatial specificity allows the reader to construct a mental
image of the plan and provides enough precision for subsequent detailed design.
