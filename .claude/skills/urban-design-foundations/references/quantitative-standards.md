# Quantitative Standards - Complete Reference

This document contains all quantitative urban design standards organized by category. Each metric includes minimum, recommended, and maximum values with source citations and contextual notes. Use this as the definitive reference for all numerical urban design decisions.

---

## Density Metrics

### Persons per Hectare (Gross)

| Density Category | Persons/ha | Typical Context | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Very Low | <25 | Rural fringe, exurban | US rural subdivisions, Australian rural residential |
| Low | 25-50 | Suburban | US single-family suburbs, Australian detached housing |
| Medium-Low | 50-100 | Garden suburbs, townhouse areas | UK inter-war suburbs, US traditional neighborhoods |
| Medium | 100-150 | Low-rise urban, mixed housing | European small towns, US walkable neighborhoods |
| Medium-High | 150-250 | Mid-rise urban | Paris (200/ha), Barcelona Eixample (~350/ha gross) |
| High | 250-400 | Dense urban | Inner London boroughs, Singapore HDB towns |
| Very High | 400+ | Ultra-dense | Hong Kong New Territories (650+/ha), Dhaka, Mumbai |

**Note:** Gross density includes all land (streets, parks, infrastructure). Net density includes only the residential parcel land. Conversion: net density is typically 1.4-1.8x gross density depending on infrastructure provision and open space allocation.

### Dwellings per Hectare (Net Residential)

| Context | Minimum | Recommended | Maximum | Typical Building Types |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Rural residential | 1 | 2-5 | 10 | Detached houses on large lots |
| Suburban | 15 | 20-30 | 35 | Detached and semi-detached houses |
| Low-rise urban | 35 | 40-60 | 70 | Townhouses, rowhouses, low-rise apartments |
| Mid-rise urban | 60 | 80-120 | 150 | 3-6 story apartment buildings |
| Urban center | 100 | 120-200 | 250 | 5-8 story mixed-use buildings |
| Urban core | 150 | 200-400 | 500+ | High-rise residential towers |

**Source:** UK Planning Policy Guidance, European urban planning standards, Singapore URA guidelines

### Floor Area Ratio (FAR) / Floor Space Index (FSI) / Plot Ratio

| Context | Minimum | Recommended | Maximum | Global Precedents |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Rural / very low density | 0.05 | 0.1-0.2 | 0.3 | US rural zoning |
| Suburban residential | 0.1 | 0.3-0.5 | 0.8 | US R-1 zoning, Australian suburban |
| Low-rise neighborhood | 0.5 | 0.8-1.2 | 1.5 | European villages, Japanese low-rise residential |
| General urban residential | 1.0 | 1.5-2.0 | 2.5 | European mid-rise, US urban residential |
| Mixed-use neighborhood | 1.5 | 2.0-3.0 | 3.5 | Barcelona Eixample (2.7), Paris inner (3.0) |
| Urban center | 2.0 | 3.0-5.0 | 6.0 | Singapore downtown (3.5-5.0), London City fringe |
| Urban core / CBD | 4.0 | 6.0-10.0 | 15.0 | Manhattan residential (6-8), Tokyo CBD (8-13) |
| Ultra-dense core | 10.0 | 12.0-15.0 | 25.0+ | Hong Kong CBD (10-15), Manhattan commercial (15-25) |

**Contextual notes:**
- FAR alone does not determine urban quality; building form, coverage, height, and setback distribution matter equally
- Same FAR can produce very different urban environments: FAR 2.0 as 2-story 100% coverage vs. 4-story 50% coverage vs. 8-story 25% coverage
- Higher FAR requires proportionally more investment in public space, transit, and infrastructure

### Site Coverage / Building Footprint Ratio

| Context | Minimum | Recommended | Maximum |
|---|---|---|---|

| Low-rise urban | 30% | 40-50% | 60% |

### Gross-to-Net Density Conversion

| Infrastructure Provision Level | Conversion Factor (Net = Gross x Factor) |
|---|---|
| Minimal infrastructure (developing context) | 1.3-1.4 |
| Standard infrastructure (established city) | 1.4-1.6 |

## Building Height and Spatial Proportion

### Height-to-Width (H:W) Ratio and Spatial Quality

| H:W Ratio | Spatial Quality | Typical Context | Design Implications |
|---|---|---|---|
| <1:6 | No sense of enclosure | Highway, open field | Space feels like a void; buildings are backdrop, not enclosure |
| 1:4 | Minimum spatial definition | Wide suburban boulevard | Beginnings of spatial awareness; very open |
| 1:3 | Comfortable open feeling | Generous urban boulevard | Pleasant openness with spatial definition; suitable for wide streets |
| 1:2 | Balanced enclosure | Ideal for most urban streets | Strong sense of place; comfortable proportion; most recommended |
| 1:1 | Strong enclosure | European urban street | Clear room-like quality; intimate but not oppressive; excellent for retail streets |
| 1.5:1 | Intense enclosure | Historic European centers | Very enclosed; works in narrow streets with active ground floors |
| 2:1 | Deep enclosure | Medieval streets, some Asian cities | Dramatic; requires active ground floors and sky views at intervals |
| 3:1+ | Canyon effect | Manhattan cross-streets | Potentially oppressive; needs generous sidewalks, street trees, and sky exposure at intersections |

### Solar Access and Daylighting

| Metric | Value | Condition | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Minimum winter sun angle at ground | 25 degrees | For ground-floor sun access at 45-degree latitude | Solar geometry |
| Minimum hours of direct sun on ground | 2-3 hours | At winter solstice, for streets and plazas | European daylighting standards |
| Maximum shadow length (winter solstice, 45N) | 2.1x building height | At noon, December 21 | Solar geometry |
| Minimum spacing between buildings (south-facing, 45N) | 1.5-2.5x building height | For ground-floor winter sun | European housing standards |
| Average Daylight Factor (outdoor public space) | 2% minimum | For feeling of adequate daylight | BRE daylighting guidance |

### Building Height Principles

| Principle | Guideline | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Human scale residential | Maximum 4 stories (approximately 14m) | Alexander Pattern 21 |
| Human-ground connection | Maximum 5 stories (approximately 17m) - above this, faces on street become unreadable from windows | Gehl, Cities for People |
| Street-level perception | Ground floor + 2-3 upper floors perceived as the "street wall"; above this is "skyline" | Cullen, Sitte |
| Sitte plaza proportions | Plaza width = 1-2x principal building height | Sitte |
| Alexander square sizing | Small public squares should be 15-21m across | Alexander Pattern 61 |
| Tower separation | Minimum 25m between tower faces; 18-22m minimum for privacy | International housing standards |
| Podium-tower transition | Base/podium defines street wall (3-6 stories); tower sets back 3-6m above podium | Urban design best practice |
| Courtyard daylight | Minimum courtyard dimension of 21m for adequate daylight access | 45-degree daylighting rule |

## Noise Standards

| Context | Daytime Limit dB(A) | Nighttime Limit dB(A) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| WHO guideline (outdoor, residential facade) | 55 | 45 | WHO Environmental Noise Guidelines (2018) |
| WHO guideline (indoor, sleeping) | 35 | 30 | WHO Night Noise Guidelines |
| Comfortable outdoor conversation | <60 | - | Gehl (talking/listening criterion) |
| Healthy Streets (not too noisy) | <70 | - | TfL Healthy Streets |
| Park / quiet area | <50 | <40 | European Environmental Noise Directive |
| School grounds | <55 | - | WHO / educational standards |
| Hospital / care facility grounds | <50 | <40 | WHO healthcare facility guidelines |

**Design strategies for noise mitigation:**
- Distance: every doubling of distance reduces noise by 3-6 dB(A)
- Building barrier: a continuous building row can reduce noise by 10-15 dB(A) for areas behind
- Earth berm: 3-5 dB(A) per meter of effective height
- Dense vegetation: 3-5 dB(A) per 30m depth of mature planting (modest effect)
- Street trees: minimal acoustic benefit, but perceived noise reduction through visual screening
- Speed reduction: reducing vehicle speed from 50 to 30 km/h reduces noise by approximately 3-5 dB(A)
- Surface treatment: low-noise asphalt can reduce tire noise by 2-4 dB(A)
