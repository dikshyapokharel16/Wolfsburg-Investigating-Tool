# Global Frameworks - Complete Reference

This document provides comprehensive coverage of all major international urban design and planning frameworks. Each framework includes its core principles, quantitative targets, scoring methodologies where applicable, and guidance on how to apply it in practice.

## UN-HABITAT - 5 Principles of Sustainable Neighbourhood Design

UN-Habitat's framework establishes quantitative spatial standards for sustainable neighborhoods based on analysis of successful urban areas worldwide. These five principles are intended to be applied together as an integrated system.

### Principle 1: Adequate Street Space and Network
- Streets should occupy 30-35% of total land area in new developments
- Street density: minimum 18 km of street length per km2 of development
- This ensures connectivity, permeability, and sufficient space for all modes of transport
- Streets serve as public space, infrastructure corridors, and economic frontage - not just vehicle movement
- Inadequate street space (below 20%) correlates with poor accessibility, congestion, and informal settlement patterns
- Excessive street space (above 45%) wastes land that could support habitation and activity

### Principle 2: High Density
- Minimum target: 15,000 people per km2 (150 people per hectare) gross density
- This density is necessary to support efficient public transit, local services, and viable retail
- Below this threshold, transit becomes financially unsustainable and walking-distance services are insufficient
- This density is achievable with 3-5 story buildings at moderate coverage - it does not require towers
- Example: 150 people/ha at 3 persons/household = 50 DU/ha, achievable with 3-story rowhouses at 60% coverage

### Principle 3: Mixed Land Use
- 40-60% of total floor space should be allocated to economic activity (commercial, office, light industrial, institutional)
- Single-function residential areas should not dominate; every neighborhood needs employment and services
- Mixed use generates activity at all hours, supports walking trips for daily needs, and creates local employment
- Vertical mixing (ground-floor commercial, upper-floor residential) is the most efficient approach at the neighborhood scale
- Horizontal mixing (different use parcels within one block) is appropriate at the district scale

### Principle 4: Social Mix
- 20-50% of residential floor area should be designated for affordable/low-cost housing
- Social diversity prevents spatial segregation by income, which concentrates poverty, limits opportunity, and creates social tension
- Mixed-income neighborhoods have better schools, safer streets, more services, and greater social mobility
- Affordable housing should be distributed throughout the neighborhood, not concentrated in a single zone
- Tenure diversity: owner-occupied, rental, social housing, cooperative, and shared ownership all within one neighborhood

### Principle 5: Limited Land-Use Specialization
- No single land use function should occupy more than 50% of any neighborhood's total area
- This prevents the creation of monoculture zones that are dead during off-peak hours
- Applies to residential, commercial, industrial, and institutional uses alike
- Promotes the mixed-use, active-all-day neighborhoods that Jacobs identified as essential for urban vitality

### UN-Habitat Spatial Metrics Summary

| Metric | Target Value |
|---|---|
| Street land area | 30-35% of total |
| Street density | 18+ km per km2 |
| Block density | 80+ blocks per km2 |
| Average block area | 1.0-1.5 ha |
| Population density (gross) | 15,000+ people per km2 (150/ha) |
| Economic floor space | 40-60% of total floor space |
| Affordable housing | 20-50% of residential floor area |
| Maximum single-use share | 50% of neighborhood area |

### Application Guidance
- Use these metrics as baseline targets for any new neighborhood design, particularly in developing country contexts where UN-Habitat standards are most directly applicable
- The street space and block density metrics are useful universal benchmarks for evaluating masterplan connectivity
- Density targets should be calibrated to local context but the 150 people/ha threshold is a sound minimum for transit viability

## NEW URBAN AGENDA (Habitat III, 2016)

The New Urban Agenda was adopted at the United Nations Conference on Housing and Sustainable Urban Development (Habitat III) in Quito, Ecuador. It establishes a shared global vision for sustainable urbanization over the next 20 years.

### Core Commitments

**Compact City Model**
- Promote compact, well-connected, and integrated urban patterns as the foundation of sustainable development
- Reduce per capita energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions through compact form
- Prioritize infill development and urban regeneration over greenfield expansion
- Mixed-use zoning as the default, not the exception

**Right to the City**
- Inclusive, safe, resilient, and sustainable cities for all
- Universal access to public spaces, green areas, and cultural facilities
- Gender-responsive urban planning and design
- Accessible design for persons with disabilities and elderly populations
- Affordable housing as a fundamental right, not a market externality

**Urban Resilience**
- Climate adaptation integrated into all urban planning and design processes
- Disaster risk reduction through spatial planning (avoiding flood zones, landslide areas, seismic risk zones)
- Redundancy in infrastructure systems (multiple water sources, distributed energy, backup transit routes)
- Social resilience through community networks, diverse economies, and adaptive governance

**Heritage and Culture**
- Protect cultural and natural heritage while enabling development and adaptation
- Traditional urban patterns and building techniques as resources, not obstacles
- Cultural diversity expressed in the built environment
- Intangible heritage (festivals, markets, traditions) supported by spatial design

### Quantitative Targets
- At least 15-20% of urban land dedicated to public open space
- Minimum 30% of land for streets and infrastructure
- Universal access to safe, affordable public transport within 500m
- Zero net land take by 2030 (all growth through densification and infill)

## WHO HEALTHY CITIES

The World Health Organization's Healthy Cities framework establishes health-based standards for urban environments, grounded in epidemiological evidence linking urban design to physical health, mental health, and social wellbeing.

### Green Space Standards
- Minimum green space: 9 m2 per person (absolute minimum for health outcomes)
- Recommended green space: 50 m2 per person (ideal for comprehensive health benefits)
- Maximum distance to usable green space: 300m (approximately 5-minute walk)
- All residents should have access to a green space of at least 0.5 hectares within 300m of their home
- Green space quality matters as much as quantity: biodiversity, tree canopy, seating, accessibility, safety

### Noise Standards
- Daytime outdoor noise at residential facades: maximum 55 dB(A), recommended below 50 dB(A)
- Nighttime outdoor noise at residential facades: maximum 45 dB(A), recommended below 40 dB(A)
- Indoor noise for sleeping: maximum 35 dB(A), recommended below 30 dB(A)
- Road traffic noise above 53 dB(A) Lden is associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk
- Environmental noise is responsible for an estimated 12,000 premature deaths per year in Europe

### Air Quality Standards
- PM2.5: annual mean below 10 micrograms per m3 (2021 WHO guideline: 5 micrograms per m3)
- PM10: annual mean below 20 micrograms per m3 (2021 WHO guideline: 15 micrograms per m3)
- NO2: annual mean below 40 micrograms per m3 (2021 WHO guideline: 10 micrograms per m3)
- O3: peak season 8-hour mean below 100 micrograms per m3 (2021 WHO guideline: 60 micrograms per m3)
- Urban design strategies: reduce vehicle trips, increase distance between traffic and pedestrians, vegetation buffers, low-emission zones, car-free areas

### Physical Activity Standards
- Urban design should support 150 minutes per week of moderate physical activity (WHO recommendation)
- Design for active transportation: walking and cycling should be the easiest and most convenient modes for trips under 5 km
- Parks, sports facilities, and playgrounds within walking distance of all residences
- Active design: stairs visible and attractive, walking routes pleasant and direct, cycling infrastructure safe and continuous
- Sedentary behavior reduction: design that encourages standing, walking, and movement throughout the day

### Mental Health and Social Wellbeing
- Access to nature reduces stress, anxiety, and depression: every resident within 300m of green space
- Social spaces prevent isolation: community facilities, gathering places, third places (neither home nor work)
- Quiet areas for mental restoration: at least one quiet zone (below 50 dB(A)) within each neighborhood
- Sense of safety: well-lit, surveilled, active public spaces with clear sightlines
- Sense of belonging: personalization, community identity, cultural expression in the built environment

## C40 CITIES

C40 is a network of nearly 100 global cities committed to addressing climate change. Their urban design-relevant frameworks include the Green and Thriving Neighbourhoods initiative and sector-specific pathways.

### Green and Thriving Neighbourhoods
- Every resident should live within a 15-minute walk or cycle of essential services (aligned with 15-Minute City)
- Access to quality green space: all residents within 15 minutes of a park, garden, or nature area
- Universal access to frequent public transport within walking distance (400-800m)
- Safe cycling infrastructure connecting all neighborhoods
- Clean air meeting or exceeding WHO guidelines in all neighborhoods

### Climate Targets
- Net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050 at the latest
- 1.5-degree-aligned climate action plans for all member cities
- Halving city-wide emissions by 2030 compared to peak levels
- 100% renewable energy for city operations by 2030

### Urban Mobility Targets
- 80% or more of all trips by walking, cycling, and public transit (combined mode share)
- Zero-emission public transport fleets by 2035
- Car-free zones in city centers and around schools
- Congestion pricing and parking demand management to reduce private vehicle use
- Complete, connected cycling networks in all member cities by 2030

### Building and Energy Targets
- Net-zero operational carbon for all new buildings by 2030
- Embodied carbon targets for new construction materials
- Deep energy retrofits for existing building stock (targeting 40-60% energy reduction)
- District energy systems for new neighborhoods where feasible
- Heat pump deployment replacing fossil fuel heating

### Green Space and Biodiversity
- 30% tree canopy coverage target for all neighborhoods
- Urban biodiversity plans for every city
- Green corridors connecting urban parks to peri-urban and rural ecological networks
- Nature-based solutions for stormwater, heat island, and air quality management

## NACTO URBAN STREET DESIGN GUIDE (2013)

The National Association of City Transportation Officials (NACTO) Urban Street Design Guide is the primary reference for context-sensitive urban street design in North America, increasingly adopted globally.

### Street Types

**Boulevard**
- A long-distance, moderate to high-speed through-route with a planted central median
- Pedestrian-friendly with wide sidewalks, street trees, and protected bike facilities
- 35-50m ROW typical
- Median: 3.6-6.0m, landscaped, may include transit
- Travel lanes: 2-3 per direction, 3.0-3.3m each
- Parking: parallel or angled, buffered from travel lanes
- Bike: protected cycle tracks, separated from traffic and pedestrians

**Avenue**
- A moderate-speed through-street connecting neighborhoods and districts
- Balanced for all modes: significant pedestrian activity alongside moderate vehicle volumes
- 25-35m ROW typical
- Travel lanes: 1-2 per direction, 3.0-3.3m
- Bike: protected cycle tracks or buffered bike lanes
- Sidewalks: 3.6-6.0m including furnishing zone

**Main Street**
- The primary commercial/retail street in a neighborhood or district
- Pedestrian-first: slow vehicle speeds (30 km/h), wide sidewalks, outdoor dining
- 20-27m ROW typical
- Travel lanes: 1 per direction, 3.0-3.3m, yield conditions where possible
- Parking: parallel both sides (2.4m), high turnover
- Sidewalks: 3.6-6.0m with generous frontage zone for outdoor dining and display
- Ground-floor retail with 0m setback and maximum transparency

**Neighborhood Street**
- A residential street with low vehicle volumes and speeds
- Designed for living: children playing, neighbors talking, walking, gardening
- 15-20m ROW typical
- Travel lanes: 1-2, yield flow or shared space, 3.0m each
- Parking: parallel one side or alternating sides
- Sidewalks: 1.8-3.0m each side, tree-lined
- Speed: 20-30 km/h design speed

**Yield Street**
- A very narrow street where vehicles must yield to each other due to constrained width
- One travel lane (3.0-4.5m) shared by both directions with intermittent parking bays for passing
- 7-10m ROW
- Suitable for very low-volume residential streets
- Inherently slow: drivers cannot pass each other without slowing and negotiating

**Shared Street (Woonerf)**
- A street with no curbs, where the entire surface is shared by pedestrians, cyclists, and vehicles at walking speed
- 6-9m ROW typical
- No grade separation between pedestrian and vehicle areas
- Bollards, planters, and furnishing define zones of use
- Speed: 10-20 km/h, enforced by design
- Suitable for low-volume, pedestrian-dominated areas: historic centers, market streets, residential courts

### Key Design Principles
- Streets are public spaces, not just transportation corridors - design for human activity, not just vehicle throughput
- Design for the desired speed, not the prevailing speed - use physical design (narrow lanes, tight corners, vertical elements) to enforce target speeds
- Design for all users: every street must accommodate pedestrians and should accommodate cyclists; transit accommodation depends on route designations
- Context sensitivity: street design responds to adjacent land use, not just traffic engineering criteria
- Safety first: Vision Zero principles - design to eliminate fatal and serious injuries

### Performance Metrics
- Fatal and serious injuries per kilometer per year (target: zero)
- Mode share by trip purpose (target: walking + cycling + transit majority)
- PM2.5 levels at pedestrian breathing height
- Retail revenue per meter of commercial frontage (measure of economic street vitality)
- Tree canopy coverage as percentage of right-of-way
- Proportion of children and elderly observed using the street (indicator of safety and comfort)

## NACTO GLOBAL STREET DESIGN GUIDE (2016)

The Global Guide extends NACTO's approach to a worldwide context, with particular attention to developing cities, informal settlements, and diverse cultural contexts.

### 6 Guiding Principles

**1. Streets for Safety**
- Design for zero traffic deaths and serious injuries
- Speed management is the primary tool: 30 km/h on all streets with pedestrians and cyclists
- Physical design enforces speed: narrow lanes, tight curves, raised intersections, textured surfaces
- Separate conflicting modes where speed differentials exceed 20 km/h

**2. Streets for Life**
- Streets as the most used public spaces in any city
- Design for dwelling, not just moving: seating, shade, gathering, playing, resting
- Active ground floors with transparent facades, frequent entrances, and engaging displays
- Night-time activation through lighting, programming, and safety

**3. Streets for Climate**
- Streets as climate infrastructure: tree canopy for cooling, permeable surfaces for infiltration, bioswales for stormwater
- Heat island reduction: shade from trees and structures, light-colored surfaces, evaporative cooling
- Carbon reduction: prioritize modes with lowest emissions (walking, cycling, transit)
- Climate adaptation: flood-resilient design, wind-responsive configuration, drought-tolerant planting

**4. Streets for Economic Activity**
- Streets generate economic value: retail frontage, market space, vending opportunities, cafe zones
- Good street design increases property values, retail revenue, and tax base
- Street vending and informal commerce are legitimate urban activities deserving designed accommodation
- Loading, deliveries, and curbside management support economic function without dominating public space

**5. Streets for Ecosystems**
- Streets as ecological corridors: connected tree canopy, planted medians, bioretention, habitat patches
- Biodiversity in street design: native species, pollinator planting, bird-friendly design, soil health
- Urban forest: every street should have trees; target 30-40% canopy coverage of street ROW
- Water cycle: capture, treat, and infiltrate stormwater within the street ROW where feasible

**6. Streets for Equity**
- Streets must serve all people: children, elderly, disabled, low-income, women, minorities
- Universal design: accessible surfaces, curb ramps, audible signals, tactile guidance, seating at regular intervals
- Gender-responsive design: lighting, sightlines, active edges, proximity to transit, avoidance of entrapment spots
- Economic equity: affordable transit access, safe walking/cycling infrastructure in underserved neighborhoods

### Key Global Metrics
- Streets account for 25-35% of urban land area globally
- More than 1.35 million people die in road traffic crashes per year, predominantly pedestrians and cyclists in low-income countries
- 90% of traffic deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries
- Streets designed for 30 km/h reduce pedestrian fatality risk from 80%+ (at 50 km/h) to under 10%

## NACTO TRANSIT STREET DESIGN GUIDE (2016)

### Bus Stop Design

| Type | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Near-side stop | Before the intersection | Simple operations; allows right-turn conflicts; use with signal priority |
| Far-side stop | After the intersection | Preferred: reduces intersection delay; compatible with signal priority; better pedestrian access |
| Mid-block stop | Between intersections | Use at major destinations not near intersections; requires safe crossing |

| Configuration | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Curbside (inline) | Bus stops in travel lane or at curb pullout | Standard for local bus on neighborhood streets |
| Bus bulb (curb extension) | Sidewalk extends to meet the bus lane | Eliminates pull-out/merge delay; adds pedestrian space; best for frequent service |
| Floating island | Bus stops at floating platform between cycle track and travel lane | Resolves bus-bike conflict; bus stops in lane; passengers board from island |

### Bus Lane Design
- Dedicated bus lane width: 3.3-3.6m (3.5m standard)
- Offset bus lane: one lane from curb, with parking or bike lane between curb and bus lane
- Curbside bus lane: directly adjacent to curb; shared with right-turning vehicles at intersections
- Center-running bus lane: in the median; requires center platforms; eliminates right-turn conflicts and double-parking blockages; best for BRT
- Contraflow bus lane: bus traveling opposite to general traffic; effective on one-way street networks

### BRT Elements
- Center-running lanes with physical separation from general traffic
- Level boarding: platform height matches bus floor (approximately 350-400mm)
- Off-board fare collection: pre-payment at stations reduces dwell time
- Real-time information: arrival predictions at stations and on mobile devices
- Signal priority: active TSP (transit signal priority) at all signalized intersections
- Station spacing: 400-800m (closer in high-density areas, wider in lower-density)
- Passing lanes at stations: allow express services to overtake stopped buses

## NACTO URBAN BIKEWAY DESIGN GUIDE (2014)

### Facility Selection by Context

| Vehicle Speed / Volume | Recommended Facility |
|---|---|
| <30 km/h, <1,500 ADT | Shared lane / bicycle boulevard |
| 30-40 km/h, 1,500-6,000 ADT | Conventional or buffered bike lane |
| 40-50 km/h, 6,000-12,000 ADT | Protected bike lane (cycle track) |
| >50 km/h or >12,000 ADT | Grade-separated facility or parallel route |

**ADT = Average Daily Traffic (vehicles)**

### Protected Bike Lane Configurations
- **One-way separated:** bike lane on each side of street, 1.5-2.5m wide, physical separation (curb, delineators, planters, parked cars) from travel lanes; 0.6m buffer minimum
- **Two-way separated:** both directions of bike travel on one side of street, 2.4-3.6m total width; requires special intersection treatment; best for streets with destinations concentrated on one side
- **Raised:** bike lane at intermediate level between sidewalk and roadway (approximately 75-100mm above roadway); physical separation through grade change
- **Parking-protected:** line of parked cars between bike lane and travel lanes; bike lane between parking and curb; 0.9m buffer between parking and bike lane for door zone

### Intersection Treatments
- **Bike box:** colored pavement area at the head of a traffic lane at a signalized intersection; allows cyclists to position ahead of vehicles during red phase; improves visibility and reduces right-hook conflicts
- **Two-stage turn queue box:** designated area for cyclists making left turns in two stages (straight through, then turn); avoids merging across traffic lanes
- **Protected intersection:** corner refuge islands separate cyclists from turning vehicles; setback crossing aligns cyclist path with driver sight lines; the gold standard for intersection safety
- **Mixing zone:** short section where bikes and vehicles share space at low speed, used where full protection is not feasible; maximum 30 km/h vehicle speed
- **Bicycle signal phase:** dedicated green phase for cyclists, separating bike movements from conflicting vehicle movements in time

## ITDP TOD STANDARD 3.0

The Institute for Transportation and Development Policy's TOD Standard is the most rigorous framework for evaluating the quality of transit-oriented development. It scores development around transit stations on 8 principles totaling 100 points.

### Scoring Framework

**1. WALK (0-15 points)**
- Walkable block sizes: blocks <110m perimeter face length score highest
- Pedestrian-priority streets: proportion of streets within 500m that prioritize pedestrians
- Active frontages: ground-floor transparency and activity on pedestrian routes
- Accessible sidewalks: continuous, wide (>2m), obstacle-free, ADA-compliant
- Shaded paths: tree canopy or structures providing shade on >50% of walking routes
- Pedestrian crossings: safe, direct, frequent (every 80-100m on arterials)

**2. CYCLE (0-5 points)**
- Cycle network: connected, safe cycling infrastructure within 2km of station
- Cycle parking at station: secure, covered, sufficient quantity (5% of daily ridership)
- Cycle access from surrounding area: safe routes connecting neighborhoods to station

**3. CONNECT (0-15 points)**
- Small blocks: average block perimeter <400m within 500m of station
- Direct routes: actual walking distance to station no more than 1.3x straight-line distance (route directness ratio)
- Low-speed streets: >80% of streets within 500m have design speed <30 km/h
- Through-connections: no gated communities, dead ends, or barriers interrupting the pedestrian network

**4. TRANSIT (0-5 points)**
- Walk distance to station: <500m for all parcels within the scored area
- Transit quality: high frequency (headways <10 min peak, <15 min off-peak), long operating hours (18+ hours/day), reliable service
- Multi-modal integration: bus-rail connections, bike-transit connections at station

**5. MIX (0-15 points)**
- Complementary uses: residential, commercial, civic, institutional, recreational all present within 500m
- Housing diversity: range of unit sizes, types, and price points
- Affordable housing: inclusion of below-market-rate units
- Temporal diversity: activity at all times of day (measured by complementary peak periods of different uses)

**6. DENSIFY (0-15 points)**
- Residential density: >15,000 residents per km2 of developable area within 500m
- Employment density: significant job concentration within 500m (varies by city context)
- Density gradient: density highest near station, tapering outward
- Higher density bonuses for exceeding minimum thresholds

**7. COMPACT (0-10 points)**
- Urban footprint: development is contiguous, not leapfrog or disconnected
- Adjacent urbanized areas: station area connects to existing urban fabric, not surrounded by vacant land
- Infill development: scored area fills in gaps rather than extending the urban boundary

**8. SHIFT (0-20 points)**
- Parking management: maximum parking ratios (not minimums), paid parking, shared parking, time limits
- Off-street parking reduced: less than conventional suburban standards
- Traffic calming: physical speed reduction measures on all streets within 500m
- Car-free areas: pedestrian-only zones, limited vehicle access zones
- Mode share targets: measurable shift from private vehicle to walk/cycle/transit

### Scoring Thresholds

| Award Level | Score | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Gold Standard TOD | 85-100 | World-class transit-oriented development; model for replication |
| Silver Standard TOD | 70-84 | Excellent TOD with minor improvement opportunities |
| Bronze Standard TOD | 55-69 | Good TOD; meets most principles with room for improvement |
| Below Standard | <55 | Does not meet minimum TOD quality; significant gaps in one or more principles |

## TRANSPORT FOR LONDON - HEALTHY STREETS (2017)

Transport for London's Healthy Streets framework provides 10 evidence-based indicators for evaluating whether streets support health and wellbeing. It is used as a design assessment tool and a policy framework for all street investments in London.

### The 10 Indicators (Detailed)

**1. Pedestrians from All Walks of Life**
- A healthy street is one where everyone feels welcome: children, elderly, disabled, all ethnicities, all income levels, all genders
- Measured by: diversity of people observed using the street, time of day usage patterns, intercept survey data on perceived welcome
- Design implications: universal accessibility, multilingual signage, culturally sensitive design, safe at all hours, no hostile architecture targeting specific populations

**2. Easy to Cross**
- Pedestrians can cross the street directly on their desire line without excessive waiting or detour
- Measured by: maximum wait time at controlled crossings (<30 seconds target), number of uncontrolled crossing opportunities per 100m, pedestrian detour factor
- Design implications: frequent marked crossings (every 60-100m on busy streets), pedestrian-priority signals, raised crosswalks, median refuge islands, narrow roadway widths that reduce crossing distance and time

**3. Shade and Shelter**
- Protection from sun, rain, wind, and snow along walking routes and at waiting points
- Measured by: percentage of route length with shade/shelter, quality and continuity of protection
- Design implications: street trees with continuous canopy, building canopies and awnings, bus shelters with full weather protection, arcades and covered walkways, wind mitigation at tower bases

**4. Places to Stop and Rest**
- Benches, leaning rails, and other rest points available at regular intervals
- Measured by: rest point spacing (every 50-100m for elderly/disabled, every 200m for general population), variety of seating options, seating in sun and shade
- Design implications: benches with backs and armrests (for elderly), leaning rails, low walls at sitting height (450mm), secondary seating (steps, planter edges), resting opportunities along slopes

**5. Not Too Noisy**
- Ambient noise levels allow comfortable conversation and do not cause stress
- Measured by: dB(A) levels at ear height along the street, proportion of street length below 70 dB(A)
- Design implications: speed reduction (30 km/h), low-noise surfaces, vegetation buffers, building arrangement to avoid noise reflection and channeling, distance between pedestrians and traffic, setback of outdoor seating from roadway

**6. People Choose to Walk, Cycle, and Use Public Transport**
- Mode share reflects genuine choice, not compulsion or lack of alternatives
- Measured by: mode share counts, origin-destination surveys, census data
- Design implications: continuous, safe, comfortable infrastructure for walking, cycling, and transit; competitive travel times versus driving; convenient, affordable, reliable transit service

**7. People Feel Safe**
- Both actual and perceived safety from crime and traffic
- Measured by: crime statistics, injury statistics, perception surveys, observed behaviors (willingness to use space after dark, presence of unaccompanied women and children)
- Design implications: good lighting (minimum 10 lux on sidewalks, 20 lux at crossings), clear sightlines, active frontages with natural surveillance, other people present at all hours, no entrapment spots (blind alleys, hidden recesses, overgrown vegetation)

**8. Things to See and Do**
- Visual interest, activity, and engagement at eye level
- Measured by: number of active frontages per 100m, variety of uses, presence of public art, events, and programming
- Design implications: active ground floors (shops, cafes, studios, community uses), window displays, public art, street furniture, markets, performance spaces, seasonal installations, information displays

**9. People Feel Relaxed**
- A sense of calm and wellbeing, not stress or anxiety
- Measured by: observed behaviors (people lingering, reading, chatting vs. rushing, tense, avoiding), perception surveys
- Design implications: greenery and planting, water features, natural materials, calm traffic, clean well-maintained environment, absence of excessive commercial signage and advertising clutter, birdsong and natural sounds

**10. Clean Air**
- Air pollution levels below health-damaging thresholds
- Measured by: PM2.5, PM10, NO2, O3 levels at pedestrian breathing height (1.5m above ground)
- Design implications: reduce vehicle traffic (especially diesel), increase distance between traffic and pedestrians, vegetation for air filtration (noting limitations), low-emission zones, car-free zones, promote clean vehicles, monitor and report air quality publicly

### Assessment Method
- Streets are assessed on each indicator using a 0-10 scoring scale
- Scores are based on a combination of physical measurement, observational surveys, and user perception surveys
- Results presented as a radar/spider diagram showing strengths and weaknesses across all 10 indicators
- Used for before-and-after evaluation of street redesign projects

## PPS (PROJECT FOR PUBLIC SPACES) - PLACEMAKING

Project for Public Spaces has evaluated over 3,000 public spaces worldwide and distilled their findings into a framework for creating great places.

### 4 Qualities of Great Places

**1. Access and Linkages**
- The place is visible from a distance and from adjacent areas
- Easy to get to by foot, bike, and transit; well-connected to surrounding neighborhoods
- Edges of the space are permeable (many entry points, no barriers)
- Internally well-connected: clear paths linking sub-areas and activities
- ADA accessible: no barriers to people with disabilities
- Well-signed and wayfound: people can find it and navigate within it
- Evaluation questions: Can you see the space from a distance? Is there a direct path from the nearest transit stop? Are there enough entry points? Is the space ADA compliant?

**2. Comfort and Image**
- Safety: people feel safe from crime and traffic (both actual and perceived)
- Cleanliness: well-maintained, no litter, graffiti cleaned promptly, functioning infrastructure
- Seating: abundant, varied (formal benches, informal ledges and steps, moveable chairs), in sun and shade, facing activity
- Attractiveness: good design, quality materials, art, planting, water, well-proportioned spaces
- Good reputation: people speak positively about the place, it has a name that is known
- Comfortable microclimate: sheltered from wind, shaded from excessive sun, warm in winter
- Evaluation questions: Is there a good first impression? Is there enough seating? Are the surfaces clean and well-maintained? Would you bring a visitor here?

**3. Uses and Activities**
- Reasons to be there at all times of day and across seasons
- Vital and active: things are happening, people are engaged in activities
- Unique and special: the place has something you cannot find anywhere else
- Fun and interesting: playful, surprising, engaging elements
- Sense of ownership: community groups, businesses, and institutions are invested in the space
- Programmed events: regular markets, concerts, festivals, classes, performances
- Informal activities: children playing, people reading, dog walking, sketching, exercising
- Evaluation questions: Is there something to do here in the morning? At lunchtime? In the evening? On weekends? In winter? How many different activities can you count?

**4. Sociability**
- Diverse mix of people: all ages, genders, ethnicities, income levels present and comfortable
- Neighborly: people know each other, greet each other, stop to talk
- Interactive and welcoming: strangers feel comfortable, can easily join activities or conversations
- Sense of pride and ownership: local people feel this is "their" place and take responsibility for it
- Cooperative management: community organizations, businesses, and government collaborate on stewardship
- Evaluation questions: Do people come here in groups? Do people bring friends and family? Do strangers interact? Would you come here alone and feel comfortable?

### Power of 10+

PPS's signature framework for building great cities through nested scales of activity:

- Every city needs **10+ great destinations** (neighborhoods, districts, waterfronts) that draw people
- Every destination needs **10+ great places** (parks, plazas, markets, streets) that anchor it
- Every place needs **10+ things to do** (sit, eat, play, watch, learn, shop, perform, exercise, garden, celebrate) that keep people there

This creates a multiplicative effect: 10 destinations x 10 places x 10 activities = 1,000 reasons to be in the city. The Power of 10 is a practical tool for evaluating whether a city, neighborhood, or space has enough to sustain vitality.

### Placemaking Process
1. **Define the place and identify stakeholders:** Who uses this space? Who wants to? Who manages it? Who lives/works around it?
2. **Evaluate the space:** Apply the 4 Qualities framework; map uses, activities, and movement patterns
3. **Develop a vision:** What should this place be? What activities should it support? What quality of experience should it provide?
4. **Short-term experiments:** Tactical interventions to test ideas - temporary seating, popup events, paint, planters
5. **Long-term implementation:** Permanent design and programming based on tested and validated ideas
6. **Ongoing management:** Programming, maintenance, stewardship, adaptation, community governance

## ADDITIONAL GLOBAL FRAMEWORKS

### LEED-ND (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design for Neighborhood Development)
- US Green Building Council's certification for sustainable neighborhood-scale development
- 3 categories: Smart Location and Linkage (SLL), Neighborhood Pattern and Design (NPD), Green Infrastructure and Buildings (GIB)
- Key credits: walkable streets, compact development, connected and open community, mixed-use neighborhoods, transit facilities, housing types and affordability, reduced parking footprint, street network, access to civic and public spaces, access to recreation, tree-lined and shaded streetscapes, neighborhood schools, stormwater management, heat island reduction, solar orientation, on-site renewable energy, water efficiency, certified green buildings
- Certification levels: Certified (40-49), Silver (50-59), Gold (60-79), Platinum (80+)

### BREEAM Communities (Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Method)
- UK-based sustainability certification for community-scale development
- Categories: Governance (GO), Social and Economic Wellbeing (SE), Resources and Energy (RE), Land Use and Ecology (LE), Transport and Movement (TM)
- Assessment at masterplan/outline design stage
- Certification levels: Pass (25%), Good (40%), Very Good (55%), Excellent (70%), Outstanding (85%)

### Estidama Pearl Community Rating System
- Abu Dhabi's sustainability framework, specifically designed for hot-arid climate contexts
- 7 categories: Integrated Development Process, Natural Systems, Livable Communities, Precious Water, Resourceful Energy, Stewarding Materials, Innovating Practice
- Mandatory credits plus elective credits
- Rating: 1 Pearl (mandatory only) to 5 Pearls (all mandatory + high elective score)

### Green Star Communities (GBCA Australia)
- Australian sustainability certification for community-scale projects
- Categories: Governance, Liveability, Economic Prosperity, Environment, Innovation
- 6-star rating system: 4 Star (Best Practice), 5 Star (Australian Excellence), 6 Star (World Leadership)

### GSAS (Global Sustainability Assessment System)
- Qatar-based system, used across the Gulf region
- Categories: Urban Connectivity, Site, Energy, Water, Materials, Indoor Environment, Cultural and Economic Value, Management and Operations
- Certification levels: Class D (0.5-1.0) to Class A (2.5-3.0)

### Living Community Challenge (International Living Future Institute)
- The most rigorous sustainability standard: requires net-positive energy, net-positive water, zero waste, and regenerative impact
- 7 Petals: Place, Water, Energy, Health and Happiness, Materials, Equity, Beauty
- Performance-based: 12 months of actual operational data required
- Very few projects have achieved full certification; individual Petals can be certified separately
