# Detailed Land Use Reference Guide

This reference provides comprehensive space standards per use, parking and trip generation rates, revenue and operating cost assumptions, phasing logic for mixed-use development, and absorption rate benchmarks. Use this as a technical companion to the main mixed-use programming skill.

### 3.2 Shared Parking and Trip Reduction

In mixed-use developments, parking demand peaks at different times for different uses, allowing shared parking:
- Office peak: weekday 9 AM - 5 PM
- Residential peak: weekday 6 PM - 8 AM + weekends
- Retail peak: weekday 12 PM - 6 PM + Saturday
- Restaurant peak: weekday 6 PM - 10 PM + weekends
- Hotel: relatively flat (60 - 80% of rooms occupied consistently)

**Shared parking factor:** Reduce total parking by 15 - 30% in a well-mixed development (ULI Shared Parking methodology)

**Trip reduction credits (from baseline ITE rates):**
- Transit proximity (within 400m of rail): -10 to -30%
- Mixed-use internal capture: -5 to -15% (residents walk to on-site retail/services)
- Transportation demand management (TDM) program: -5 to -15%
- Bike infrastructure: -2 to -5%
- Car-share provision: -2 to -5%
- **Total possible reduction: -25 to -50%** in a transit-rich, well-mixed development

## 6. Phasing Logic for Mixed-Use Development

The order in which uses are delivered matters. Get the phasing wrong and the project stalls. Get it right and each phase supports the next.

### 6.1 Phasing Principles

1. **Infrastructure first:** Roads, utilities, drainage, and public open space must precede or coincide with the first vertical development. You cannot sell or lease space without functioning infrastructure.

2. **Residential creates population:** Residential is typically the first vertical use because it brings permanent residents who create demand for retail, services, and community facilities. Build residential first (or concurrently with the anchor).

3. **Anchor tenant before speculative space:** An anchor (a major employer, institution, transit station, supermarket, or cultural facility) creates critical mass and credibility. Secure the anchor early, even if it requires incentives.

4. **Retail follows rooftops:** Retail needs a population base to survive. Do not deliver retail space before there are enough residents, workers, or visitors to support it. Rule of thumb: first convenience retail when population reaches 3,000 - 5,000; first comparison retail at 15,000 - 25,000.

5. **Office follows connectivity:** Office tenants need transit access, good road connectivity, and amenity (F&B, fitness). Deliver office after transit and core amenity are operational.

6. **Civic follows population thresholds:** Community facilities (schools, clinics, libraries) are triggered by population milestones (see Section 5 of SKILL.md).

7. **Hotel follows destination status:** Hotels need a reason for visitors to come. Deliver hotel after the district has established a critical mass of attractions, employment, or events.

### 6.2 Typical Phasing Sequence

| Phase | Timeframe | Uses Delivered | Population/Employment Milestone |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Enabling Works** | Year 0 - 1 | Infrastructure, earthworks, public open space framework, roads | - |
| **Phase 1** | Year 1 - 3 | Residential (200 - 500 units), anchor retail (supermarket), temporary activation | 500 - 1,200 residents |
| **Phase 2** | Year 3 - 6 | Residential (300 - 800 units), convenience retail, primary school, first park, some office | 2,000 - 4,000 residents, 500 - 1,500 workers |
| **Phase 3** | Year 5 - 10 | Residential (ongoing), comparison retail, community center, health clinic, more office | 5,000 - 10,000 residents, 2,000 - 5,000 workers |
| **Phase 4** | Year 8 - 15 | Secondary school, cultural facilities, hotel, destination retail, innovation/tech | 10,000 - 20,000 residents, 5,000+ workers |
| **Phase 5** | Year 12 - 20+ | Remaining residential, premium office, major cultural/civic, transit upgrades | 15,000 - 30,000+ residents, 10,000+ workers |

### 6.3 Early Activation Strategies

New developments take years to reach critical mass. In the interim, use temporary activation to create life and build identity:
- Temporary markets and events on cleared land (weekend farmers market, food truck rally)
- Pop-up retail and F&B in shipping containers or temporary structures
- Community gardens on future development parcels
- Outdoor cinema, music events, sports on open land
- Art installations and murals on construction hoardings and blank walls
- Opening parks and public spaces as early as possible (even before all surrounding buildings are complete)

## 7. Absorption Rate Benchmarks

Absorption rate is the amount of new space the market can absorb (sell or lease) per year. This determines the build-out timeline and phasing.

### 7.1 Residential Absorption

| Market Type | Annual Absorption (units/year) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Slow market / small city | 30 - 80 units | Limited demand, long build-out |
| Mid-market / mid-size city | 80 - 200 units | Healthy demand, moderate pace |
| Strong market / large city | 200 - 500 units | High demand, rapid delivery possible |
| Boom market / global city | 500 - 2,000+ units | Multiple projects absorbing simultaneously |

**Rule of thumb:** A single development project should not assume more than 20 - 30% of total market absorption (to avoid oversaturation).

**Affordable housing:** Absorption is demand-limited - waiting lists often exceed supply. Affordable units absorb faster than market-rate in undersupplied markets.

### 7.2 Office Absorption

| Market Type | Annual Absorption (m2 NIA/year) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Slow market / secondary city | 2,000 - 8,000 m2 | Limited corporate demand |
| Mid-market | 8,000 - 20,000 m2 | Steady demand from SMEs and branches |
| Strong market / primary city | 20,000 - 60,000 m2 | Major corporates, tech, finance |
| Boom market / global hub | 60,000 - 200,000+ m2 | Major expansions, new HQs |

**Office pre-commitment:** Developers typically require 30 - 50% pre-lease before commencing speculative office construction (lender requirement). Government or institutional anchor tenants de-risk office delivery.

### 7.3 Retail Absorption

Retail absorption is driven by population growth and foot traffic, not by speculative supply.

| Trigger | Typical Retail Absorbed |
|---|---|
| First 3,000 - 5,000 residents | 500 - 1,500 m2 convenience retail (grocery-anchored) |
| 5,000 - 10,000 residents | 1,500 - 4,000 m2 (expanded convenience + first F&B cluster) |
| 10,000 - 20,000 residents | 4,000 - 10,000 m2 (comparison retail emerges, F&B district) |
| 20,000 - 40,000 residents | 10,000 - 25,000 m2 (full convenience + comparison + leisure) |
| 40,000+ residents | 25,000+ m2 (town center-scale retail, destination uses) |

**Critical rule:** Do not build retail space ahead of population. Empty retail is a project killer - it signals failure, deters investment, and creates dead frontage. Use temporary activation (pop-up, markets, containers) until population supports permanent retail.

### 7.4 Hotel Absorption

| Trigger | Typical Hotel Demand |
|---|---|
| District employment reaches 5,000+ | 50 - 100 rooms (business hotel) |
| Major cultural/event/tourist attraction | 100 - 300 rooms (mid-scale + boutique) |
| Airport / convention center / CBD adjacency | 200 - 500+ rooms (full service) |
| Destination resort / tourism district | Scale with visitor projections |

**Hotel feasibility indicators:**
- Existing hotel occupancy in the catchment > 70% suggests unmet demand
- Existing occupancy 50 - 70% suggests balanced market - caution
- Existing occupancy < 50% suggests oversupply - do not add rooms

## 8. Development Yield Calculator

Use these formulas for preliminary yield estimates during programming:

### 8.1 Residential Yield

```
Number of units = (Total residential GFA) / (Average unit GFA including common areas)

Where:
  Average unit GFA = Average NIA / NIA-to-GFA ratio
  Average NIA (typical mid-market) = 65 - 75 m2 (weighted average of mix)
  NIA-to-GFA ratio = 0.75 - 0.85

Example:
  Residential GFA: 50,000 m2
  Average NIA: 70 m2
  NIA-to-GFA: 0.80
  Average unit GFA: 70 / 0.80 = 87.5 m2
  Units: 50,000 / 87.5 = 571 units
  Population (at 2.1 persons/unit): 1,199 people
```

### 8.2 Office Yield

```
Number of employees = (Total office NIA) / (NIA per employee)

Where:
  Office NIA = Office GFA x NIA-to-GFA ratio (0.75 - 0.85)
  NIA per employee = 10 - 15 m2 (traditional), 8 - 12 m2 (open plan)

Example:
  Office GFA: 30,000 m2
  NIA = 30,000 x 0.80 = 24,000 m2
  Employees (at 12 m2 per): 2,000
```

### 8.3 Retail Yield

```
Number of retail units = (Total retail GFA) / (Average unit size)
Number of jobs = (Total retail GFA) / (25 - 35 m2 per employee)

Example:
  Retail GFA: 5,000 m2
  Average unit: 200 m2
  Units: 25
  Jobs: 5,000 / 30 = 167
```

### 8.4 Hotel Yield

```
Number of rooms = (Total hotel GFA) / (GFA per key)

Where:
  GFA per key = 45 - 80 m2 (depending on class)

Example:
  Hotel GFA: 12,000 m2
  GFA per key: 60 m2 (midscale)
  Rooms: 200
```

## 9. Feasibility Quick Check

Before committing to a program, run these quick checks:

### 9.1 Land Value Check
```
Residual Land Value = Total Revenue - Construction Cost - Developer Margin - Finance Cost
If Residual Land Value < Acquisition Cost --> Project is not feasible at this program
```

### 9.2 Revenue-to-Cost Ratio
- Target minimum: 1.15 - 1.25x (revenue = 1.15 - 1.25 x total development cost)
- Below 1.10x: too risky for most developers
- Above 1.30x: attractive; expect competition

### 9.3 Yield on Cost (for income-producing assets)
```
Yield on Cost = Annual Net Operating Income / Total Development Cost
```
- Target: 1 - 2% above the prevailing market cap rate
- Example: if office cap rate is 5%, yield on cost target = 6 - 7%

### 9.4 Infrastructure Cost Check
```
Infrastructure cost per dwelling (all-in: roads, water, sewer, power, telecoms, open space):
  Greenfield: $30,000 - 80,000 per dwelling equivalent
  Brownfield/infill: $15,000 - 50,000 per dwelling equivalent
  High-density urban: $10,000 - 30,000 per dwelling equivalent (shared over more units)
```

If infrastructure costs per unit are high relative to end values, consider increasing density (more units sharing the infrastructure cost) or staging infrastructure to match demand.
