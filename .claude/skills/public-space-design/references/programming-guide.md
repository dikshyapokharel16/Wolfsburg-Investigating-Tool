# Public Space Programming Guide

This reference provides detailed activity programming by user group, seasonal programming strategies, event infrastructure requirements, revenue-generating programs, and maintenance cost benchmarks. Use this when developing the program for any public space design.

---

## 1. Activity Programming by User Group

### 1.1 Infants and Toddlers (0 - 5 years)

**Primary needs:** Sensory stimulation, safe exploration, proximity to caregiver, shade, soft surfaces.

**Activities:**
- Sand play (digging, pouring, shaping): sand pit min 10 m2, depth 300mm, edged and drainable
- Water play (splashing, running through): shallow interactive fountain (5 - 15 mm depth), spray jets, hand-pumps
- Crawling and toddling: soft rubber mound (max 0.5m height), textured surfaces, ramps
- Swinging: bucket swings (2 minimum), low bay swing for caregiver + child
- Sliding: low slides (max 1.2m platform), wide for parent accompaniment
- Sensory exploration: musical instruments (chimes, drums), tactile panels, mirrors, planting they can touch
- Imaginative play: playhouses, tunnels (transparent or open-ended), vehicle-shaped rockers

**Space requirements:** 100 - 400 m2 enclosed area with 1.2m fence and self-closing gate. Soft safety surfacing throughout. Sun shade over 50% minimum.

**Adjacent amenities:** Parent seating with 100% sightlines, pram parking, changing table (in restroom within 100m), drinking water, handwash.

**Design considerations:**
- Fence is essential: toddlers will wander toward traffic and water
- Ground-level entry (no steps) for prams and wheelchairs
- No gaps wider than 89mm in fencing or equipment (head entrapment prevention)
- Muted natural colors preferred over garish primary colors
- Nature elements (logs, boulders, plants) more developmentally beneficial than plastic equipment

### 1.2 Children (6 - 12 years)

**Primary needs:** Physical challenge, social interaction, independence, imaginative play, connection to nature.

**Activities:**
- Climbing: climbing walls (2 - 4m), rope structures (net climbers, rope bridges), boulders, trees
- Swinging: belt swings (2 - 4), nest swing (group swing), rope swings
- Sliding: tall slides (2 - 4m platform), tunnel slides, embankment slides
- Spinning: merry-go-round, spinning disc, spinning cup
- Balancing: balance beams, stepping stones, slack line, log walk
- Jumping: trampolines (in-ground), jump platforms with soft landing
- Ball games: informal kick-about area (min 15m x 20m), basketball half-court (15m x 14m)
- Nature play: den building (loose sticks and logs), mud kitchen, stream play, tree climbing
- Creative play: chalk walls, sand sculpture, construction with loose parts
- Social spaces: treehouse, fort, elevated platform (for socializing away from adults)

**Space requirements:** 300 - 1,500 m2, loosely defined boundary (no fence required for this age). Varied surfacing: rubber under equipment, sand in play zones, grass for running, mulch in nature areas.

**Adjacent amenities:** Drinking water, restrooms within 200m, adult seating at perimeter (not hovering), bike parking.

**Design considerations:**
- Risk is essential for development: allow height (2 - 4m), speed, and physical challenge
- Avoid age-inappropriate restriction: this age group needs to test limits
- Nature play elements are more engaging and longer-lasting than manufactured equipment
- Provide spaces for hiding and privacy (children need spaces away from adult gaze)
- Loose parts (moveable elements) increase creative and social play by 3 - 5x

### 1.3 Teenagers (13 - 17 years)

**Primary needs:** Social gathering, physical activity, independence from adults, WiFi, identity expression, being seen and seeing peers.

**Activities:**
- Socializing: grouped seating (benches in clusters, amphitheater steps, hammocks), away from younger children and adults
- Ball sports: basketball half-court or full court (28m x 15m), futsal (40m x 20m), volleyball
- Skating: skate features (ledges, rails, quarter pipes, flat ground) - minimum 200 m2 skate zone
- Cycling/BMX: pump track (50 - 100m loop), jump lines
- Fitness: outdoor gym equipment, pull-up bars, parkour features
- Music/performance: informal performance area with seating, power outlets for amplifiers
- Digital: WiFi access, phone charging stations, covered seating for device use
- Creative expression: legal graffiti/mural walls, art installations by teens

**Space requirements:** 500 - 2,000 m2, visually connected to but physically separated from younger children's areas. Hard surfaces for skating and sports, lawn for hanging out.

**Adjacent amenities:** Food and drink within 100m (kiosk, vending), restrooms, lighting for evening use (this age group uses spaces later), WiFi.

**Design considerations:**
- Involve teens in design process (participatory workshops): they will use what they help create
- Do not over-program: teens need unstructured space to claim as their own
- Evening use is critical: this group stays latest, so lighting and safety are essential
- Noise tolerance: accept that teens are loud; locate away from residences where possible
- Visibility vs. privacy: teens want to be seen by peers but not monitored by adults

### 1.4 Young Adults (18 - 30 years)

**Primary needs:** Social gathering, exercise, relaxation, food and drink, digital connectivity, events.

**Activities:**
- Exercise: jogging/running loop (min 400m), outdoor gym, yoga/stretching lawn, group fitness space
- Social dining: picnic areas, BBQ grills, food truck zones, beer garden / cafe seating
- Relaxation: hammocks, loungers, lawn for sunbathing, reading nooks
- Sports: volleyball, badminton, frisbee, informal football
- Events: outdoor cinema, live music, markets, festivals
- Dating/socializing: attractive seating with views, ambient lighting, proximity to F&B
- Digital: WiFi, charging, outdoor co-working spaces with shade and tables

**Space requirements:** Varied - integrated into larger park or plaza design. This group uses all parts of a well-designed space.

**Design considerations:**
- Aesthetic quality matters most to this group: Instagram-worthy design increases use
- Evening activation: lighting, F&B, events are essential for this demographic
- Flexibility: moveable furniture, adaptable spaces, pop-up infrastructure
- This group drives food truck and market revenue

### 1.5 Families

**Primary needs:** Children's play adjacent to adult seating, picnicking, shade, restrooms, accessible paths for strollers.

**Activities:**
- Children's play with adjacent parent seating (sightlines essential)
- Picnicking: tables in shade with enough space for a family group (4 - 6 people, min 2.5m x 1.5m table)
- Walking and strolling: accessible paths, interesting things to see for children
- Nature exploration: pond-dipping, bug-hunting, bird-watching, foraging
- Events: children's performances, storytime, holiday events, seasonal markets
- Food: picnic facilities, BBQ grills, food kiosks, ice cream vendor
- Water play: interactive fountains, paddling, splash pad

**Space requirements:** Families are the primary user of neighborhood parks. They need the full range of facilities within a compact, legible layout.

**Design considerations:**
- Sightlines from parent seating to play is the single most important design move for families
- Restroom access within 200m is non-negotiable
- Pram and wheelchair access on all primary routes
- Shade is critical: families visit during the hottest part of the day (weekends, school holidays)
- Storage/lock-up for bikes and scooters near play areas

### 1.6 Elderly (65+ years)

**Primary needs:** Comfortable seating, level paths, shade, social contact, gentle exercise, safety, proximity to home.

**Activities:**
- Sitting and people-watching: benches with backs and armrests, in sun and shade, facing activity
- Walking: flat, well-surfaced paths with frequent benches (every 50m), handrails on slopes
- Gentle exercise: tai chi area (flat, shaded, 10m x 10m), petanque/bocce court (15m x 3m), gentle fitness equipment
- Social gathering: covered pavilion with table and chairs (for card games, chess, conversation)
- Gardening: accessible raised beds at 70 - 80 cm height
- Bird feeding, pond watching, garden enjoyment
- Reading, chess, checkers: table with seats, sheltered from wind

**Space requirements:** Integrated throughout the space, with particular attention to entrances, main paths, and the most sheltered and sunny locations.

**Design considerations:**
- Every bench needs a back and armrests (essential for getting up)
- Non-slip surfaces are critical: wet stone, loose gravel, and uneven pavers are hazardous
- Lighting must be even (no sudden light/dark transitions, which impair aged vision)
- Signage with large, high-contrast text
- The elderly visit most frequently but for shorter durations; proximity to home (200 - 400m) is essential
- Intergenerational proximity: elderly seating near (but not in) children's play areas is highly valued

### 1.7 People with Disabilities

**Primary needs:** Universal access, inclusive design, sensory richness, dignity and independence.

**Activities:**
- All activities available to all abilities: inclusive play equipment, accessible paths, reachable facilities
- Wheelchair-accessible routes: firm, level, wide (1.8m min), smooth transitions at level changes
- Sensory gardens: fragrant, tactile, and auditory planting for people with visual impairments
- Inclusive play: wheelchair-accessible swings, transfer platforms, ground-level activities, sensory panels
- Seating: wheelchair-friendly tables (knee clearance 680mm, surface height 750mm)
- Wayfinding: tactile ground indicators, braille signage, audio wayfinding, color contrast

**Design considerations:**
- Universal design is better than "accessible design": design for everyone from the start, not as an afterthought
- Accessible does not mean separate: avoid "disability-specific" zones; integrate accessibility throughout
- Test with disabled users during design development
- Maintain accessible routes: overgrown planting, displaced pavers, ponding water all create barriers
- Companion seating: always provide a clear space adjacent to benches for a wheelchair user to sit alongside a friend

## 2. Seasonal Programming Strategies

### 2.1 Summer Programming (May - September / Nov - March southern hemisphere)

**Weather-dependent activities:**
- Outdoor cinema screenings (Thursday - Saturday evenings, dusk onwards)
- Water play / splash pad operation (daily, 10 AM - 7 PM)
- Outdoor fitness classes (yoga, bootcamp, tai chi): early morning (6 - 7 AM) and evening (6 - 8 PM)
- Markets: weekend farmers market, evening street food market, monthly arts market
- Music: Friday/Saturday evening concerts (acoustic preferred for residential areas), Sunday jazz/classical
- Children's programming: weekday morning storytime, arts and crafts, nature activities
- Sports: casual leagues (volleyball, basketball, futsal) on weekday evenings

**Weather-independent activities:**
- Covered markets under permanent or temporary structures
- Pavilion-based activities (dance, fitness, workshops)
- Self-guided trails (art walk, nature trail) function rain or shine

### 2.2 Winter Programming (November - March / May - September southern hemisphere)

**Cold-weather activation strategies:**
- Holiday market: November - January, temporary stalls, food, crafts, music
- Ice skating: temporary rink (min 200 m2, 15m x 15m), November - February
- Winter lighting: festive lights, light art installation (attracts visitors in darkness)
- Hot food and drink: pop-up cafe, mulled wine stand, hot chocolate cart
- Fire pit / brazier gathering: where allowed by code, communal fire pit with seating (check local fire regulations)
- Winter sports viewing: outdoor screens for major events
- Indoor-outdoor programming: workshops, classes, exhibitions in adjacent community buildings that spill into the space

**Design for winter use:**
- Wind-sheltered sunny seating (sun traps with walls on 2 - 3 sides)
- Heated seating (radiant heaters under cover, heated bench seats)
- Well-drained surfaces (no ice pooling on paths)
- Deciduous trees that allow winter sun through

### 2.3 Shoulder Season Programming (Spring and Autumn)

- Seasonal planting festivals: community bulb planting (autumn), flower festivals (spring)
- Harvest events: community garden harvest, apple pressing, pumpkin carving
- Running / walking events: 5K fun runs, charity walks
- Outdoor art exhibitions: sculpture, photography, student work
- Clean-up days: community maintenance events (build ownership and stewardship)
- Educational: bird walks, tree identification, ecology workshops

## 3. Event Infrastructure Requirements

Design the space to accommodate events without excessive temporary infrastructure. Embed the following into the permanent design.

### 3.1 Power Supply
- Electrical kiosks with locking access panels at regular intervals (every 30 - 50m in event zones)
- Power capacity: minimum 32A three-phase at primary event locations; 16A single-phase at secondary
- Weatherproof outlets (IP66 rated) flush with ground or in bollard-mounted housings
- Separate event power from general lighting circuits

### 3.2 Water Supply
- Hose bibs / taps at every 50m in event zones (for food vendors, cleaning, irrigation)
- Drinking water fountains: permanent, at key locations
- Greywater drainage: floor drains or swales in food vendor zones

### 3.3 Level Surfaces
- Primary event area: max 2% slope in any direction (for stages, market stalls, seating)
- Min 500 m2 of level, hard-surfaced area for a small market (20 stalls)
- Min 1,500 m2 for a medium market (50 stalls) or outdoor cinema (500 seats)

### 3.4 Load-Bearing Surfaces
- Design for vehicle loads in event areas: delivery trucks (min 10 tonne axle load), cherry pickers, stage trucks
- Reinforced grass or permeable paving in areas that function as both park and event space
- Bollard-controlled vehicle access routes: 3.5m wide, turning radius 6m

### 3.5 Access and Circulation
- Emergency vehicle access to within 50m of all parts of the space (3.5m wide, 4.0m clear height)
- Crowd egress: minimum 2 exits from any event area, 1.0m width per 500 people
- Accessible viewing positions: level areas with sightlines to stage, near accessible routes
- Service/delivery access separate from public entrance (ideally from rear or service lane)

### 3.6 Stage and Performance
- Permanent stage pad: level, hard surface, 6m x 8m minimum for small performances, with power and lighting rigging points
- Alternatively: designated stage zone with ground anchors for temporary stage structure
- Audience area: 0.5 m2 per standing person, 0.75 m2 per seated person
- Sound orientation: direct performance sound away from noise-sensitive uses (residences, hospitals)

### 3.7 Vendor Infrastructure
- Market stall pitch: 3m x 3m per standard stall (with 1.5m aisle between rows)
- Power to each pitch: minimum 13A single phase
- Food vendor pitch: 5m x 5m with water, drainage, grease trap
- Food truck zone: 8m x 4m per truck, with kerb access, power, and drainage
- Waste management: bins at 15m intervals in market areas, trade waste collection point

## 4. Revenue-Generating Programs

Public spaces can generate revenue to offset maintenance costs. Design for these from the outset.

### 4.1 Markets
- Weekend farmers market: 20 - 50 stalls, pitch fee $50 - 200/stall/day, 1 - 2 days/week
- Monthly artisan/craft market: 30 - 80 stalls, pitch fee $75 - 250/stall/day
- Evening street food market: 10 - 30 stalls, pitch fee $100 - 300/stall/evening, 1 - 3 evenings/week
- Holiday market: 40 - 100 stalls, 4 - 8 week season, pitch fee $200 - 500/stall/day
- Estimated revenue: $50,000 - 300,000 / year for a well-managed market program

### 4.2 Food and Beverage
- Permanent cafe/kiosk concession: annual lease $20,000 - 100,000 (location-dependent)
- Food truck permits: $50 - 200/day/truck, 3 - 7 days/week
- Seasonal pop-up bar: $25,000 - 75,000 concession fee for a 4 - 6 month season
- Estimated revenue: $30,000 - 200,000 / year

### 4.3 Fitness and Wellness
- Licensed fitness classes (yoga, bootcamp, tai chi): $500 - 2,000/month license per operator
- Running events (5K, 10K): $2,000 - 10,000 venue hire per event
- Estimated revenue: $10,000 - 50,000 / year

### 4.4 Events and Venue Hire
- Outdoor cinema: ticket revenue or concession, $2,000 - 5,000/screening
- Concert/festival: $5,000 - 50,000 venue hire (large events)
- Private events (weddings, corporate): $2,000 - 20,000 per event
- Art installations (sponsored): $5,000 - 50,000 sponsorship per installation
- Estimated revenue: $20,000 - 200,000 / year

### 4.5 Seasonal Installations
- Ice rink: $50,000 - 200,000 net revenue for a 3-month season (ticket + skate hire + F&B)
- Urban beach: $30,000 - 100,000 net revenue for a 4-month season
- Holiday market: $50,000 - 300,000 net revenue for a 6-week season
- Estimated revenue: $50,000 - 500,000 / year for a major seasonal installation

### Total Revenue Potential
A well-programmed civic plaza or neighborhood park in a mid-to-large city can generate $100,000 - 500,000+ per year in programming revenue, offsetting 30 - 80% of annual maintenance costs.

## 5. Maintenance Cost Benchmarks

### 5.1 Cost Per Square Meter Per Year (by space type and quality level)

| Space Type | Basic ($/m2/yr) | Standard ($/m2/yr) | Premium ($/m2/yr) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Civic Plaza | 15 - 25 | 25 - 40 | 40 - 60 |
| Neighborhood Park | 5 - 12 | 12 - 20 | 20 - 35 |
| Pocket Park | 20 - 35 | 35 - 50 | 50 - 75 |
| Linear Park / Greenway | 3 - 8 | 8 - 15 | 15 - 25 |
| Waterfront Promenade | 12 - 20 | 20 - 35 | 35 - 50 |
| Playground | 20 - 35 | 35 - 50 | 50 - 70 |
| Community Garden | 3 - 6 | 6 - 10 | 10 - 15 |
| Streetscape | 5 - 12 | 12 - 20 | 20 - 30 |

**Quality level definitions:**
- **Basic:** Public space in a standard condition with routine maintenance only. Mowing, cleaning, basic repair. Acceptable but not attractive.
- **Standard:** Well-maintained public space with proactive care. Regular planting, furniture repair/replacement, seasonal cleanup, moderate programming. Attractive and comfortable.
- **Premium:** High-quality civic space with dedicated management. Daily cleaning, horticultural excellence, prompt repair, extensive programming, curated aesthetics. Beautiful and beloved.

### 5.2 Maintenance Task Frequency

| Task | Basic | Standard | Premium |
|---|---|---|---|
| Litter collection | 3x / week | Daily | 2x / day |
| Path sweeping | Weekly | 2x / week | Daily |
| Lawn mowing (growing season) | Bi-weekly | Weekly | 2x / week |
| Planting bed maintenance | Monthly | Bi-weekly | Weekly |
| Tree inspection | Annual | Semi-annual | Quarterly |
| Bench/furniture inspection | Quarterly | Monthly | Weekly |
| Play equipment inspection | Monthly | Weekly | Daily visual + monthly detailed |
| Lighting inspection | Quarterly | Monthly | Monthly |
| Deep clean (power wash, etc.) | Annual | Quarterly | Monthly |
| Irrigation system check | Monthly (season) | Bi-weekly | Weekly |
| Graffiti removal | Weekly | Within 48 hrs | Within 24 hrs |
| Paint/stain touch-up | Annual | Semi-annual | As needed (immediately) |
| Water feature maintenance | Weekly (season) | 3x / week | Daily |

### 5.4 Annual Maintenance Budget Formula

For preliminary budgeting, use this formula:

```
Annual Maintenance Budget = (Total Area in m2) x (Cost per m2 from table 5.1) x (Quality Factor)

Quality Factor:
  Basic = 1.0
  Standard = 1.5 - 2.0
  Premium = 2.5 - 3.5

Add sinking fund for lifecycle replacement:
  Annual Sinking Fund = (Total Replacement Cost of All Elements) / (Weighted Average Life in Years)
  Typical: 2 - 4% of original capital cost per year

Total Annual Budget = Maintenance Budget + Sinking Fund
```

**Example:**
A 1.0 ha (10,000 m2) neighborhood park maintained to Standard level:
- Maintenance: 10,000 m2 x $15/m2 = $150,000 / year
- Sinking fund (assuming $3M capital cost, 3%): $90,000 / year
- **Total: $240,000 / year**
- Revenue offset (if programmed): $50,000 - 100,000 / year
- **Net cost: $140,000 - 190,000 / year**
