# Public Space Typologies Reference

This reference provides comprehensive specifications for 12 public space typologies. Use this when determining dimensional parameters, material selections, programming, and design precedents for a specific space type.

---

## 1. Civic Plaza

**Definition:** A formal public gathering space at the heart of a civic or commercial district, primarily hard-surfaced, designed for events, ceremony, daily social life, and symbolic representation of community identity.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 0.5 - 4.0 ha (5,000 - 40,000 m2)
- Primary dimension: 30 - 100m across
- Aspect ratio: 1:1 to 1:2 (square to moderately rectangular)
- Enclosure ratio (building height : space width): 1:2 to 1:4

**Program Elements:**
- Central open area for events, markets, and gatherings (min 40% of total area)
- Formal seating (benches, built-in stone seats) and informal seating (steps, walls, edges)
- Water feature or fountain as focal point
- Public art / sculpture
- Flagpoles, monument, or civic symbol
- Cafe or kiosk with outdoor seating (at least 1 active F&B edge)
- Underground service access for event infrastructure (power, water, drainage)
- Stage or performance area (permanent or provisions for temporary)

**Design Principles:**
- Strong axial or geometric composition reflecting civic importance
- At least 2 active edges (ground-floor retail, cafe, civic building entrance)
- Level or gently sloping surface (max 2%) for gathering and event use
- Clear sightlines across the entire space
- Material quality should signal civic permanence (natural stone, high-quality concrete, bronze details)

**Materials Palette:**
- Primary: natural stone paving (granite, sandstone, basalt) or high-quality precast concrete
- Accents: bronze, stainless steel, polished stone
- Furniture: cast iron or stainless steel benches with timber seats; bespoke design preferred
- Lighting: architectural column fixtures (4 - 6m height) with accent/feature lighting on buildings and art

**Planting Strategy:**
- Formal tree planting: rows, grids, or bosques of single species (plane trees, oaks, lindens)
- Limited ornamental planting (geometric planters, seasonal bedding at edges)
- Canopy target: 20 - 30% of paved area (less than parks - civic character requires openness)

**Lighting Approach:**
- Even ambient lighting: 30 - 50 lux across primary space
- Feature lighting on civic buildings, art, water: 100 - 300 lux
- Festive lighting infrastructure (catenary wires, power outlets for seasonal lights)
- Color temperature: 3000K (warm, dignified)

**Furniture Specification:**
- Benches: 1 per 20 - 30m of edge, with backs and armrests
- Bollards: retractable for event vehicle access
- Bins: discreet, integrated into furniture line, every 30m
- Drinking fountains: minimum 2, universally accessible

**Maintenance Level:** High - daily cleaning, weekly furniture inspection, monthly stone/paving check, quarterly feature maintenance. Budget: $25 - 50 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Piazza del Campo, Siena - the canonical civic plaza; shell-shaped, sloping to the center, fully enclosed, endlessly used
2. Federation Square, Melbourne - contemporary civic plaza with active cultural edges, varied surfaces, event infrastructure
3. Pioneer Courthouse Square, Portland - "Portland's living room"; stepped seating, event stage, cafe, transit integration
4. Plaza de la Constitucion, San Sebastian - formal tree grid, cafe edges, cultural events, civic symmetry
5. Aker Brygge Waterfront Plaza, Oslo - high-quality materials, active F&B edges, waterfront views, evening activation

**Key Metrics:**
- Seating capacity (daily use): 50 - 200 seats per 1,000 m2
- Event capacity (standing): 2 - 4 persons per m2 of open area
- Active edge percentage: minimum 50% of perimeter
- Annual event days target: 50 - 150 days

## 2. Neighborhood Park

**Definition:** A multi-purpose green space serving a residential neighborhood, combining passive recreation (sitting, walking, picnicking) with active uses (play, sports, exercise), designed for daily use by a diverse population.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 0.5 - 5.0 ha (5,000 - 50,000 m2)
- Primary dimension: 70 - 250m
- Aspect ratio: varied (irregular shapes often work well)
- Service radius: 500m (7-minute walk)

**Program Elements:**
- Open lawn for unprogrammed use (min 20% of total area)
- Children's play area (0 - 5 and 6 - 12 age zones)
- Walking/jogging loop (min 400m circuit)
- Seating areas in sun and shade
- Picnic area with tables and shade
- Sports/exercise area (multi-use court, fitness equipment, or open field)
- Dog off-leash area (if serving dog-owning community, min 500 m2, fenced)
- Community pavilion or shelter (30 - 80 m2, covered, with power and water)
- Public restroom (within the park or within 200m)
- Stormwater management features (rain garden, bioswale, detention basin)

**Design Principles:**
- Multiple entry points aligned with surrounding streets and paths (minimum 3, ideally one per block face)
- Internal path network connecting all program areas with universal access
- Clear sightlines from paths and seating to play areas
- Balance between structured programming and open, flexible lawn
- Accommodate both weekday (individual/small group) and weekend (family/community) use patterns

**Materials Palette:**
- Paths: asphalt or stabilized aggregate for main routes; decomposed granite or woodchip for secondary
- Gathering areas: unit pavers, stone, or stamped concrete
- Play surfaces: rubber safety surfacing (poured-in-place or tiles), sand, engineered wood fiber
- Furniture: durable timber and steel benches; timber picnic tables; powder-coated steel fixtures

**Planting Strategy:**
- Mixed native and adapted species for biodiversity and low maintenance
- Canopy trees for shade: 60 - 80% cover at maturity over lawn and seating areas
- Understory planting along edges for habitat and visual enclosure
- Seasonal interest: spring flowering trees, summer perennials, autumn color, winter evergreen structure
- Stormwater planting: native grasses, sedges, and wetland species in bioswales and rain gardens

**Lighting Approach:**
- Path lighting: 10 - 20 lux, 3.5 - 4.5m pole height, 25 - 30m spacing
- Gathering/seating areas: 20 - 30 lux
- Play areas: lit only if intended for evening use (20 - 30 lux)
- Minimize light spill to neighboring residences
- Color temperature: 3000K

**Furniture Specification:**
- Benches: every 50m along paths, with backs and armrests on at least 50%
- Picnic tables: 1 per 2,000 m2 of park, wheelchair-accessible end
- Bins: every 50m along paths, dog waste bins near off-leash area
- Drinking fountains: 1 per 1 ha, with dog bowl attachment

**Maintenance Level:** Medium - mowing weekly (growing season), path sweeping weekly, play equipment inspection monthly, planting beds maintained seasonally. Budget: $10 - 25 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Superkilen, Copenhagen - three zones (red, black, green) serving a diverse immigrant community with global play and gathering elements
2. Parc de la Villette Pocket Parks, Paris - small thematic gardens within a larger framework, each with distinct character
3. Yanghui Waterfront Park, Shanghai - terraced neighborhood park with wetland, play, and gathering integrated naturally
4. Klyde Warren Park, Dallas - deck park over highway with lawn, play, food trucks, and daily programming
5. Victoria Park, London - historic neighborhood park with lake, sports, play, and cafe, loved for generations

**Key Metrics:**
- Visits per hectare per day (good park): 200 - 600
- Canopy cover at maturity: 60 - 80%
- Active recreation area: 20 - 30% of total park
- Passive / open lawn: 20 - 40% of total park
- Paths and hard surface: 15 - 25% of total park

## 3. Pocket Park

**Definition:** A small, intimate public space in a dense urban area, providing respite, seating, greenery, and a moment of pause in the daily routine. Often created from a single building lot or leftover space.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 0.01 - 0.5 ha (100 - 5,000 m2)
- Primary dimension: 10 - 30m
- Aspect ratio: 1:1 to 1:3 (often constrained by lot geometry)
- Service radius: 200m (3-minute walk)

**Program Elements:**
- Seating (primary function): benches, chairs, walls, steps
- Shade trees or canopy structure (min 1 tree per 50 m2)
- Ornamental planting for beauty and sensory experience
- Small water feature (optional but highly effective)
- Lighting for evening use and safety
- WiFi and phone charging (increasingly expected)
- Public art or mural (activates walls)

**Design Principles:**
- Simplicity: one clear idea, not too many competing elements
- Enclosure: use walls, planting, and level changes to create a sense of room
- Visibility from the street: the entrance must be visible and welcoming; avoid gates or barriers
- Microclimate: in small spaces, sun/shade/wind conditions are critical - model carefully
- Acoustic separation: use water, planting, or level changes to create a quiet refuge from street noise

**Materials Palette:**
- High-quality throughout (small area means materials budget per m2 can be higher)
- Natural stone, quality timber, weathering steel, polished concrete
- Avoid standard municipal catalogue furniture - bespoke or high-design pieces appropriate

**Planting Strategy:**
- Dense, layered planting creating lush green "room"
- Vertical greening on walls (climbing plants, green walls)
- Large-canopy trees (even 1 - 2 significant trees transform a pocket park)
- Fragrant species along seating (lavender, jasmine, herbs)
- Seasonal containers and planters for changing displays

**Lighting Approach:**
- Intimate scale: wall-mounted or low-level fixtures (1 - 2.5m)
- Feature lighting on planting, art, water
- 20 - 40 lux for comfortable evening use
- Color temperature: 2700K (warm, intimate)

**Furniture Specification:**
- Seating: maximum variety in minimum space - bench, chair, ledge, step, wall
- Moveable chairs strongly preferred (allow users to arrange)
- Table for eating/working: at least 1 - 2 cafe tables
- No bollards or barriers unless essential for vehicle protection

**Maintenance Level:** High - daily cleaning (small space = high use density = fast wear). Budget: $30 - 60 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Paley Park, New York - the original pocket park; waterfall wall, moveable chairs, honey locust canopy, cafe
2. Greenacre Park, New York - terraced, waterfall, lush planting, intimate and beloved
3. The Goods Line, Sydney - linear pocket spaces within a former rail corridor
4. Pocket parks of Barcelona (Superblocks program) - reclaimed from street intersections, simple and effective
5. Yanaka Ginza pocket gardens, Tokyo - tiny gardens in a dense neighborhood, each with unique character

**Key Metrics:**
- Seating capacity: 1 seat per 2 - 5 m2 of total area
- Green cover: 40 - 70% (including vertical)
- Daily visitors: 50 - 300 (high for the size)
- Dwell time: 10 - 30 minutes average

## 4. Linear Park / Greenway

**Definition:** A continuous green corridor following a natural or infrastructural alignment (river, rail, canal, ridge) providing connected walking, cycling, recreation, and ecological habitat.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Width: 10 - 30m (minimum 10m for meaningful park character)
- Length: 500m to 50+ km
- Aspect ratio: > 3:1 (often much greater)

**Program Elements:**
- Shared-use path: 3.0 - 4.0m wide (pedestrian + cyclist) or separated paths (2.0m ped + 3.0m cycle)
- Seating at regular intervals: every 100m along the route
- Rest stops / nodes every 300 - 500m with expanded seating, shade, water, info
- Exercise stations every 300m (optional but popular)
- Play nodes every 500 - 800m (nature play preferred in greenway context)
- Ecological habitat zones (native planting, wildlife corridors, pollinator meadows)
- Stormwater management (bioswales, rain gardens, riparian buffers)
- Wayfinding signage at every intersection and every 200m

**Design Principles:**
- Continuity: the path must be uninterrupted; every street crossing is a design challenge requiring clear priority, signalization, or grade separation
- Variety: vary the character along the route - open meadow, dense woodland, urban edge, water edge, play node, gathering space
- Access: frequent entry points from the surrounding neighborhood (every 200 - 300m)
- Dual character: the greenway is both a movement corridor (getting somewhere) and a destination (spending time)

**Materials Palette:**
- Path: asphalt (cycling), stabilized aggregate or resin-bound (walking), boardwalk (wetland)
- Bridges: timber, steel, or concrete; design as landmarks
- Furniture: robust timber and steel; bench style consistent along the route for identity
- Signage: unified wayfinding system with distance markers

**Planting Strategy:**
- Native and ecological planting predominant
- Riparian buffer (if along water): min 10m of native riparian planting
- Meadow zones: managed for biodiversity (seasonal mowing)
- Canopy trees for shade along path: 50 - 70% cover at maturity
- Edge planting for habitat connectivity

**Lighting Approach:**
- Paths: 10 - 15 lux along urban sections; unlit in ecological/rural sections (preserve darkness for wildlife)
- Nodes/gathering areas: 20 - 30 lux
- Bollard or low-level lighting preferred (3 - 4m poles) to reduce light pollution
- Solar-powered where off-grid

**Furniture Specification:**
- Benches: every 100m, alternating sides of path, in shade and sun
- Drinking fountains: every 500m
- Bins: every 200m, with recycling
- Bike repair stations: every 1 - 2 km
- Information boards: at every entry point

**Maintenance Level:** Low to medium - path sweeping monthly, mowing as scheduled (varies by zone), furniture inspection quarterly. Budget: $5 - 15 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. High Line, New York - elevated linear park on former rail; world-famous for planting design, art, and urban integration
2. Cheonggyecheon Stream, Seoul - restored urban stream with linear park; 5.8 km, transformative for the city center
3. The Promenade Plantee, Paris - the original elevated rail park, predecessor to the High Line
4. Buffalo Bayou Park, Houston - 65 ha river park with continuous trails, nature play, gardens, event spaces
5. Corniche, Beirut - waterfront promenade serving as the city's living room, 4.8 km of continuous public access

**Key Metrics:**
- Daily users per km: 500 - 5,000 (depending on urban context)
- Entry points per km: minimum 3, preferred 5+
- Ecological habitat area: 30 - 50% of total corridor
- Average speed: 5 km/h walking, 15 km/h cycling

## 5. Waterfront Promenade

**Definition:** A public walkway along a water body (river, lake, harbor, ocean) providing continuous public access, views, recreation, and activation at the water's edge.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Width: 8 - 20m (minimum 8m for two-way movement + seating + edge)
- Length: 200m to 20+ km
- Zones: water edge (1 - 2m), promenade (3 - 6m), activity zone (3 - 6m), building edge (2 - 4m)

**Program Elements:**
- Continuous promenade with universal access
- Seating oriented to water views: every 20 - 30m
- Water access points: steps, ramps, or platforms to the water's edge
- F&B kiosks or restaurants at regular intervals (every 300 - 500m)
- Fishing piers or viewing platforms projecting over water
- Public art and interpretive elements
- Boat moorings or water taxi stops where applicable
- Event areas (seasonal markets, festivals, concerts)

**Design Principles:**
- Continuous public access is non-negotiable - the water's edge belongs to the public
- Layer the experience: water edge for contemplation, promenade for walking, activity zone for sitting and socializing, building edge for commerce and shelter
- Frame water views from every seating position
- Protect against flooding: design for resilience (elevated sections, flood walls, temporary barriers)
- Connect the promenade to the hinterland: perpendicular streets and paths every 100 - 200m

**Materials Palette:**
- Promenade: natural stone, timber boardwalk, or high-quality concrete; non-slip finish essential
- Edge: stone or concrete coping with rounded profile; steel or timber railings where required (90 cm min height near water)
- Furniture: marine-grade materials (stainless steel, hardwood, aluminum)

**Planting Strategy:**
- Wind-tolerant species (salt-tolerant if coastal)
- Trees spaced to preserve views: 12 - 15m on center, high-crowned species
- Low planting at water edge (do not block views from promenade)
- Green pockets and garden rooms inserted where width allows

**Lighting Approach:**
- Promenade: 15 - 25 lux, low poles (3 - 4m) or bollards to preserve night sky and water views
- Feature lighting on bridges, piers, art, key buildings
- Water edge: subtle in-ground or bollard lighting
- Avoid light spill onto water (ecological impact)

**Furniture Specification:**
- Benches: every 20 - 30m, oriented to water, with backs
- Railings: designed for leaning and lingering (wide top rail at 1.0 - 1.1m)
- Bins: marine-grade, every 30m
- Bollards: for vehicle exclusion, marine-grade, retractable at service points

**Maintenance Level:** High - exposure to weather and water increases wear; daily cleaning, weekly inspection of edges and railings, monthly check of water access features. Budget: $20 - 40 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Copenhagen Harbor Promenade - continuous access, harbor baths, diverse activities, exemplary public life
2. Chicago Riverwalk - tiered levels to water, diverse room types (marina, cove, river theater), F&B activation
3. Bordeaux Quays, France - 4.7 km waterfront transformation with water mirror, promenade, gardens, tram
4. Wellington Waterfront, New Zealand - diverse precincts, public art, diving platform, lagoon, market
5. Yokohama Osanbashi Pier, Japan - sculptural pier with continuous public surface, views to harbor and Mt. Fuji

**Key Metrics:**
- Public access: 100% of water's edge
- Active frontage: minimum 40% of building edge
- Seating density: 1 seat per 3 - 5 linear meters of promenade
- F&B nodes: every 300 - 500m

## 6. Playground

**Definition:** A dedicated space for children's play, designed by age group with appropriate equipment, surfacing, enclosure, and parent/caregiver amenities.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Toddler play (0 - 5): 100 - 400 m2 (fenced, enclosed)
- Junior play (6 - 12): 300 - 1,500 m2 (open or loosely defined)
- Combined: 400 - 2,000 m2
- Adventure play / nature play: 500 - 5,000 m2
- Service radius: 300 - 500m (5 - 7 minute walk)

**Program Elements:**
- Age-separated zones with clear boundaries (fence, hedge, level change)
- Swings, slides, climbing structures, spinning elements, sensory features
- Sand play and water play (especially for younger children)
- Nature play elements: logs, boulders, sand, water, mounds, tunnels
- Inclusive/accessible play equipment (at least 30% of elements)
- Safety surfacing throughout (rubber, sand, engineered wood fiber)
- Parent/caregiver seating with sightlines to all equipment
- Shade over at least 50% of play area (canopy trees or shade structures)
- Drinking water and handwash station
- Restroom within 100m

**Design Principles:**
- Risk, not hazard: allow age-appropriate challenge and physical risk (height, speed, roughness) while eliminating hazards (entrapment, sharp edges, poor surfacing)
- Nature play: incorporate natural materials and elements wherever possible; nature play promotes creativity, resilience, and sensory development
- Inclusive design: children of all abilities must be able to play together, not in separate "accessible" zones
- Social play: design for group interaction (see-saws, group swings, collaborative climbing) not just individual equipment
- Sensory richness: texture, sound, color, movement, smell - avoid monochrome plastic equipment on rubber surfacing

**Materials Palette:**
- Equipment: robinia timber, stainless steel, high-density polyethylene, rope (marine grade)
- Surfacing: poured-in-place rubber (colored), sand, engineered wood fiber, artificial turf (for mounds)
- Edging: timber, stone, weathering steel
- Avoid: excessive plastic, primary-color schemes (prefer natural and muted tones)

**Planting Strategy:**
- Shade trees: deciduous for summer shade, winter sun; place to shade equipment and seating
- Sensory planting: herbs (rosemary, lavender), grasses (for rustling sound), flowering shrubs
- Buffer planting around perimeter for wind protection and visual enclosure
- Avoid toxic or thorny species within play areas

**Lighting Approach:**
- Generally not lit for evening use (children's play is daytime)
- If lit: 20 - 30 lux, even distribution, no dark corners; 3000K
- Security lighting after hours: minimal, motion-activated

**Furniture Specification:**
- Parent benches: every 15 - 20m of perimeter, with backs and armrests, positioned for full sightlines
- Picnic tables: 1 per 200 m2 of play area, wheelchair-accessible
- Bins: at every entrance/exit
- Bike racks: adjacent to entrance
- Pram parking: designated area near entrance with sightlines

**Maintenance Level:** High - daily surface inspection, weekly equipment check, monthly comprehensive inspection (per EN 1176 / ASTM F1487), annual professional audit. Budget: $30 - 60 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Tumbling Bay Playground, Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park, London - nature play, sand, water, timber, boulders; exemplary
2. The Imagination Playground, New York - loose parts play; children build their own structures from foam blocks
3. Toshimaen Play Park, Tokyo - adventure play with fire, tools, and supervised risk; radical and effective
4. Belleville Park Playground, Paris - sloped site with integrated climbing, sliding, and planting
5. Darling Quarter Playground, Sydney - 4,000 m2, water play, climbing towers, inclusive design, nature play

**Key Metrics:**
- Play area per child in catchment (under 12): 2.4 m2 minimum
- Shade cover: 50%+ over play areas
- Inclusive elements: 30%+ of total play features
- Parent seating: within 15m of every play element, 100% sightline coverage

## 7. Community Garden

**Definition:** A shared gardening space where individuals or families cultivate allocated plots, with communal facilities for socializing, learning, and food production.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 0.1 - 0.5 ha (1,000 - 5,000 m2)
- Individual plot size: 10 - 20 m2 (raised beds 1.2m x 2.4m typical)
- Communal area: 15 - 25% of total garden
- Service radius: 500m (7-minute walk)

**Program Elements:**
- Individual plots (raised beds or in-ground): 20 - 60 plots typical
- Communal herb/fruit garden
- Tool shed / storage (15 - 30 m2, secure, weatherproof)
- Communal gathering area with table and seating (covered preferred)
- Water supply: communal taps every 20 - 30m
- Composting area (screened from main gathering area)
- Noticeboard and signage
- Accessible raised beds (min 2, at 70 - 80 cm height for wheelchair access)
- Children's garden area (optional but community-building)

**Design Principles:**
- Security: fenced perimeter with lockable gate (community gardens suffer from theft/vandalism)
- Sunlight: orient long axis east-west for maximum sun exposure; avoid shading from trees or buildings on south side
- Water access: no plot should be more than 15m from a water tap
- Social space: the communal area is as important as the plots - design for lingering and conversation
- Soil quality: import clean topsoil for raised beds; test existing soil for contamination (especially on brownfield sites)

**Materials Palette:**
- Raised beds: untreated hardwood timber, galvanized steel, stone, or concrete block (avoid treated timber - leaching)
- Paths: compacted aggregate (3m wide for main, 1.5m for secondary), woodchip, or grass
- Structures: simple timber frame shed, pergola, or open shelter
- Fencing: 1.8m chain-link or timber paling (security)

**Planting Strategy:**
- Individual plots: user-determined (food production, flowers, herbs)
- Communal: fruit trees (espaliered or standard), berry bushes, herb spiral
- Perimeter: hedge planting for wind protection, habitat, and beauty
- Wildflower/pollinator strip to attract beneficial insects

**Lighting Approach:**
- Generally not lit (gardens close at dusk)
- If lit: minimal security lighting on shed, gate, and communal area
- Solar-powered preferred

**Furniture Specification:**
- Communal table and benches: 1 set per 20 plots
- Tool hooks and storage racks in shed
- Compost bins: 3-bin system (fresh, active, finished)
- Noticeboard: weather-protected, at entrance

**Maintenance Level:** Low (community-maintained) - individual plot holders maintain their plots; communal areas maintained by garden committee; annual infrastructure check by owner. Budget: $5 - 10 / m2 / year (plus volunteer labor).

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Prinzessinnengarten, Berlin - mobile community garden on former wasteland; social enterprise model
2. Dalston Eastern Curve Garden, London - community garden in a former rail cutting; cafe, events, growing
3. Eagle Street Rooftop Farm, Brooklyn - rooftop community food production; intensive urban farming
4. Incredible Edible Todmorden, UK - community food growing in all public spaces; transformative civic project
5. Les Jardins d'Eole, Paris - community garden integrated into a public park; social inclusion focus

**Key Metrics:**
- Plots per hectare: 50 - 200 (depending on layout and communal space)
- Waiting list: a healthy garden typically has a waiting list of 20 - 50% of current members
- Food production: 2 - 4 kg / m2 / year for an active plot
- Community events: monthly gathering minimum

## 8. Streetscape / Green Street

**Definition:** A redesigned street that prioritizes pedestrians, cycling, greenery, and stormwater management within the existing right-of-way (ROW), transforming the street from a traffic conduit into a public space.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Total ROW: 15 - 30m (typical urban street)
- Pedestrian zone: 2.0 - 6.0m per side
- Tree/furniture zone: 1.5 - 3.0m
- Bioswale zone: 1.5 - 2.5m (where applicable)
- Cycle lane: 1.8 - 2.5m (one-way protected)
- Carriageway: reduced to minimum for function (typically 6.0 - 7.0m for two lanes)

**Program Elements:**
- Widened sidewalks with quality paving
- Street trees at 8 - 12m spacing
- Bioswales or rain gardens for stormwater capture
- Seating (benches every 50m, informal seating on planters/walls)
- Cafe seating zones (where adjacent retail/F&B exists)
- Cycle infrastructure (protected lane, bike parking)
- Lighting (pedestrian-scale, 3 - 5m)
- Signage and wayfinding
- Public art integration (paving patterns, murals, sculptures)

**Design Principles:**
- Reallocate road space from vehicles to people: reduce lane widths, remove unnecessary lanes, convert parking to parklets
- Green infrastructure: every streetscape redesign should capture and treat stormwater through bioswales, tree pits, or permeable paving
- Continuous canopy: tree spacing and species selection to achieve 40 - 60% canopy cover at maturity
- Active ground floors: streetscape design must support and encourage ground-floor retail and F&B use
- Complete streets: accommodate all modes safely (pedestrian, cyclist, transit, vehicle, goods)

**Materials Palette:**
- Sidewalk: concrete pavers, natural stone, or quality cast-in-place concrete with exposed aggregate
- Bioswale: stone/concrete curb with native planting, overflow drain
- Cycle lane: colored asphalt (green preferred) or concrete with clear markings
- Furniture: coordinated family of elements (bench, bollard, bin, light, bike rack) in consistent material palette
- Tree grates/guards: cast iron or steel (removable as tree grows)

**Planting Strategy:**
- Street trees: large-canopy species tolerant of urban conditions (drought, salt, compaction, pollution)
- Bioswale planting: native grasses, sedges, and rain garden species; tolerant of wet and dry cycles
- Understory: low maintenance groundcover, ornamental grasses, seasonal bulbs in tree pits
- Green walls on blank facades where possible

**Lighting Approach:**
- Pedestrian scale: 3 - 5m poles, 15 - 25 lux on sidewalks
- Under-canopy lighting on tree trunks or branches (uplighting) for evening ambiance
- Feature lighting on key buildings, public art
- Color temperature: 3000K

**Furniture Specification:**
- Benches: every 50m, set back from path edge by 0.5m
- Bike racks: every 30 - 50m (or per demand study)
- Bins: every 50m, paired (general waste + recycling)
- Bollards: at intersections and pedestrian zones, retractable for access
- Parklets: convert 2 parking spaces into a seating/planting platform

**Maintenance Level:** Medium - city services for cleaning and tree care; bioswale maintenance seasonal; furniture quarterly check. Budget: $10 - 20 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Barcelona Superblocks - reclaimed intersections as public space, reduced through-traffic, greening and seating
2. SW 12th Avenue Green Street, Portland - pioneering bioswale integration in a standard streetscape
3. Exhibition Road, London - shared surface, no curbs, granite paving, pedestrian priority in museum district
4. Stroget, Copenhagen - pedestrianized shopping street; the world's longest car-free street (1.1 km); seating and activity throughout
5. Passeig de Sant Joan, Barcelona - redesigned boulevard with bioswales, play areas, wide promenades, and cycle paths

**Key Metrics:**
- Pedestrian space: minimum 50% of ROW in commercial areas, 40% in residential
- Canopy cover at maturity: 40 - 60%
- Stormwater captured: 80 - 100% of first 25mm rainfall event through green infrastructure
- Seating density: 1 seat per 15 - 20 linear meters of street

## 9. Urban Beach / Seasonal Space

**Definition:** A temporary or permanent public space designed for seasonal activation, often featuring imported sand, water play, pop-up structures, and a relaxed, holiday atmosphere in an urban setting.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 0.1 - 2.0 ha
- Typical: 1,000 - 10,000 m2
- Operating season: 3 - 6 months (summer)

**Program Elements:**
- Sand area with loungers and umbrellas
- Water play (fountains, misting, shallow pools)
- Bar/cafe with outdoor seating
- Music/performance area
- Sports (beach volleyball, petanque)
- Children's play zone
- Hammocks, swing seats, informal seating

**Design Principles:**
- Festive, joyful atmosphere: color, music, food, activity
- Low-cost, temporary materials: the ephemeral quality is part of the charm
- Activate underused spaces: parking lots, vacant lots, post-industrial sites, bridges, rooftops
- Accessible infrastructure: power, water, drainage, restrooms must be planned even for temporary installations

**Materials Palette:**
- Sand: clean, washed play sand (300 - 500mm depth)
- Structures: scaffolding, shipping containers, timber frame, fabric canopies
- Furniture: deck chairs, loungers, pallets, cable reels, beanbags
- Surfacing: decking over temporary subbase

**Planting Strategy:**
- Containerized plants: palms, grasses, flowering annuals in large planters
- Temporary turf or artificial grass for lawn areas
- No permanent planting (site must be restored)

**Lighting Approach:**
- Festive: string lights, colored LED, lanterns
- Functional: 15 - 25 lux for safety
- Feature lighting on bar, stage, art

**Furniture Specification:**
- Loungers: 1 per 5 m2 of sand area
- Shade: umbrellas or canopies over 50% of seated area
- Tables: bar-height and café-height mix

**Maintenance Level:** High during operating season - daily cleaning, lifeguard for water play, security. Budget varies; typically operated as commercial concession.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Paris Plages, Paris - annual summer beach along the Seine; the original urban beach
2. Strandbar Mitte, Berlin - permanent urban beach bar along the Spree
3. Sugar Beach, Toronto - permanent urban beach with pink umbrellas, sand, Muskoka chairs
4. Southbank Centre Beach, London - seasonal installation with sand, food, and free events
5. Copenhagen Harbor Baths - permanent urban swimming and beach integrated into the harbor

**Key Metrics:**
- Daily visitors (peak season): 500 - 5,000
- Operating season: 90 - 180 days
- Revenue per m2: $50 - 200 / season (if commercial concession)

## 10. Civic / Cultural Forecourt

**Definition:** The public space immediately in front of a civic or cultural institution (museum, library, theater, city hall), serving as a threshold between the public realm and the institution.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 200 - 5,000 m2
- Depth from building: 10 - 40m
- Width: proportional to building facade

**Program Elements:**
- Grand entrance approach (axial or asymmetric depending on building character)
- Queuing space (for ticketed venues)
- Outdoor exhibition / display area
- Cafe seating (operated by institution or independent)
- Public art related to institution's mission
- Event space for openings, performances, markets
- Drop-off zone (accessible vehicles, taxis, buses)
- Bike parking

**Design Principles:**
- The forecourt extends the institution's identity into the public realm
- Material and design quality should match the institution
- Function as a public space even when the institution is closed
- Balance civic grandeur with everyday usability
- Provide covered waiting area for queuing

**Materials Palette:**
- Match or complement the building's material palette
- Typically: natural stone, high-quality concrete, bronze or steel details
- Bespoke furniture reflecting institutional identity

**Planting Strategy:**
- Formal planting complementing architecture
- Feature trees as focal points
- Seasonal planting in containers for visual interest at entrance
- Minimal planting if forecourt is primarily event/gathering space

**Lighting Approach:**
- Architectural lighting on building facade: 100 - 300 lux
- Forecourt ambient: 30 - 50 lux
- Feature lighting on art, signage, landscape features
- Evening activation: the lit building draws people to the forecourt

**Furniture Specification:**
- Bespoke benches aligned with architectural language
- Bollards for vehicle protection (may be anti-ram rated for high-profile institutions)
- Information/wayfinding signage

**Maintenance Level:** High - maintained by institution to match interior quality standards. Budget: $25 - 50 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Tate Modern Turbine Hall / Forecourt, London - ramp approach, river edge, and forecourt as continuous public space
2. Lincoln Center Plaza, New York - iconic fountain, stepped seating, activated by Met Opera, ballet, concerts
3. Federation Square, Melbourne - forecourt of cultural institutions; irregular geometry, media screens, event space
4. National Gallery of Victoria Forecourt, Melbourne - water wall, moat, and symmetrical approach
5. Louvre Pyramid and Cour Napoleon, Paris - the most visited civic forecourt in the world; sunken entry, reflecting pools

**Key Metrics:**
- Active hours: should function as public space beyond institution hours
- Event capacity: sized for opening nights, markets, festivals
- Accessibility: 100% universal access from street to building entrance

## 11. Urban Plaza / Transit Plaza

**Definition:** A public space integrated with a transit stop or station, serving the dual function of public gathering and passenger access, waiting, and interchange.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 500 - 5,000 m2
- Width: 15 - 50m
- Depth: 15 - 40m
- Must accommodate transit infrastructure (bus stops, tram stops, station entrances) without sacrificing public space quality

**Program Elements:**
- Transit waiting area: sheltered, lit, with real-time information
- Public gathering area: separate from transit flows
- Retail kiosks, newsstand, cafe (activating the space and serving transit users)
- Bike parking and bike share station
- Wayfinding and area information
- Seating: waiting (formal, oriented to transit) and lingering (informal, oriented to activity and sun)
- Clock tower or prominent landmark (helps wayfinding)
- Green infrastructure where space allows

**Design Principles:**
- Separate flow from stay: transit passengers move through; space users want to linger - design distinct zones
- Multi-modal: accommodate bus, tram, metro, bike, pedestrian, taxi/rideshare
- Legibility: the space must be immediately understandable - where is my bus? Where is the exit? Where can I sit?
- Safety: excellent lighting and sightlines (this is often a night-time space)
- Activation: retail and F&B transform a transit stop from a functional node into a place

**Materials Palette:**
- Durable, anti-graffiti, easy-clean surfaces (transit plazas experience heavy use and wear)
- Natural stone or high-quality concrete for paving
- Stainless steel and glass for shelters and furniture
- LED screens and digital signage

**Planting Strategy:**
- Street trees where space allows (min 15 m3 soil volume)
- Green walls or vertical planting to soften infrastructure
- Low planting in beds at the edges (not in the flow of pedestrian traffic)
- Rain gardens to capture runoff from hard surfaces

**Lighting Approach:**
- High: 30 - 50 lux across the entire space (safety critical for transit environments)
- Shelter interiors: 50 - 100 lux
- Feature lighting on art, landmark element, building facades
- 24-hour operation: lighting must support all-night use

**Furniture Specification:**
- Transit seating: individual seats with armrests (prevents lying down - context-specific decision)
- Lingering seating: benches and informal seating away from transit zone
- Lean rails: for short waits, 0.9 - 1.1m height
- Information kiosk and map

**Maintenance Level:** Very high - daily cleaning (multiple times), 24-hour lighting, real-time maintenance of transit infrastructure. Budget: $40 - 80 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. Superkilen / Norrebro Station, Copenhagen - transit plaza integrated with the neighborhood park
2. Pershing Square Transit Plaza, Los Angeles - redesigned to integrate metro access with public space
3. Exchange Square, Manchester - tram stop framed by an active civic plaza with seating, food, and events
4. Kanazawa Station Plaza, Japan - transit plaza with iconic gate structure, water feature, and glass dome
5. Britomart Transport Centre, Auckland - heritage building restored as transit hub with activated public forecourt

**Key Metrics:**
- Pedestrian throughput: designed for peak flows (transit arrival/departure surges)
- Dwell time (non-transit users): 10 - 30 minutes target
- Shelter coverage: 100% of transit waiting areas
- Lighting: 30+ lux everywhere, 24/7

## 12. Rooftop Garden / Elevated Public Space

**Definition:** A public or semi-public space on a rooftop, podium, or elevated deck, providing green space, views, and recreation where ground-level space is limited.

**Dimensional Ranges:**
- Area: 200 - 10,000 m2
- Depth: varies with building footprint
- Access: elevator, stair, escalator, or ramp from street level

**Program Elements:**
- Seating with views (primary attraction)
- Planting (intensive or extensive green roof)
- Walking/strolling paths
- Cafe or bar
- Event space (small gatherings, yoga, film screenings)
- Play area (if serving residential population)
- Urban farming / food production
- Environmental education

**Design Principles:**
- Wind is the primary challenge: use walls, screens, planting, and furniture to create sheltered zones; conduct wind analysis
- Weight: structural loading limits determine soil depth, planting, and features (coordinate with structural engineer)
- Access: if truly public, must be easy to find, free to enter, and accessible by elevator from street
- Irrigation and drainage: design lightweight growing media, efficient irrigation, and reliable waterproofing/drainage
- Views: frame and celebrate panoramic views; orient seating and paths to the best vistas

**Materials Palette:**
- Lightweight: timber decking, aluminum furniture, lightweight planters, composite materials
- Waterproofing protection: root-resistant membrane, drainage mat, filter fabric
- Growing media: lightweight engineered soil (800 - 1200 kg/m3 saturated vs 1800 kg/m3 for standard soil)

**Planting Strategy:**
- Extensive (low maintenance): sedums, grasses, succulents (100 - 150 mm soil depth, 80 - 150 kg/m2)
- Intensive (garden): perennials, shrubs, small trees (300 - 1000+ mm soil depth, 300 - 1000+ kg/m2)
- Wind-tolerant and drought-tolerant species
- Food production: raised beds with 300+ mm depth for vegetables and herbs
- Biodiversity: wildflower meadow, insect hotels, bird boxes

**Lighting Approach:**
- Intimate: low-level, warm (2700K), integrated into furniture, planters, and railings
- Avoid uplighting that creates light pollution for surrounding residences
- Feature lighting on planting and views
- Solar-powered where possible

**Furniture Specification:**
- Lightweight, windproof (anchored or weighted)
- Moveable chairs and tables for flexibility
- Lounge seating (daybeds, hammocks, recliners) for a relaxed rooftop character
- Windscreen panels (glass, perforated metal) integrated with furniture

**Maintenance Level:** High - irrigation system maintenance, planting care, waterproofing inspection, wind-damage repair. Budget: $30 - 60 / m2 / year.

**Precedent Examples:**
1. ACROS Fukuoka, Japan - terraced green roof rising 15 stories, fully public, integrated with a park at the base
2. Lurie Garden, Millennium Park, Chicago - intensive rooftop garden over a parking garage; perennial meadow and hedgerow
3. Marina Bay Sands SkyPark, Singapore - 1.2 ha rooftop park 200m above ground; gardens, pool, restaurant
4. Caixa Forum Vertical Garden, Madrid - vertical green wall on the adjacent building creates a green room in the public space
5. Crossrail Place Roof Garden, Canary Wharf, London - enclosed rooftop garden with tropical and native planting above the station

**Key Metrics:**
- Structural loading: design load including saturated soil, water features, crowd loading, snow
- Waterproofing warranty: min 20 years
- Irrigation: drip irrigation with moisture sensors for efficiency
- Public access hours: align with building operating hours; maximize access
