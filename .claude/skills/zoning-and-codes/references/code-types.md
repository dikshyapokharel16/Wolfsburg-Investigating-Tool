# Zoning Code Types: Deep Reference

Comprehensive reference for all major zoning system types with real-world examples, model code excerpts, and typical zoning district specifications with dimensional standards. This document supplements the SKILL.md with detailed technical content for code drafting and analysis.

---

## 1. Euclidean Zoning (Use-Based)

### Origin and Legal Basis

Euclidean zoning derives its name from *Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co.* (1926), the U.S. Supreme Court case that upheld the constitutionality of use-based zoning. The Euclid, Ohio ordinance divided land into use districts (residential, commercial, industrial) with dimensional standards (height, area, setback) for each. This case established that zoning is a valid exercise of police power provided it bears a substantial relation to public health, safety, morals, and general welfare.

### Structure of a Euclidean Zoning Code

A typical Euclidean code contains the following components:

1. **Zoning Map**: divides the jurisdiction into districts
2. **Use Tables**: matrix of permitted (P), conditional/special (C/S), and prohibited (--) uses by district
3. **Dimensional Standards Tables**: height, setback, lot area, lot width, lot coverage, FAR by district
4. **Supplemental Regulations**: parking, signage, landscaping, lighting, accessory structures
5. **Administrative Provisions**: permits, variances, conditional uses, amendments, nonconformities, appeals
6. **Definitions**: legal definitions of all terms used in the code

### Strengths and Limitations of Euclidean Zoning

**Strengths**:
- Legally well-established (nearly 100 years of case law)
- Simple for administrators, applicants, and communities to understand
- Provides certainty about what can be built on a given parcel
- Effective at separating genuinely incompatible uses (heavy industry from schools)
- Widely understood by the real estate development industry

**Limitations**:
- Enforces separation of uses, requiring automobile trips between home, work, shopping, and recreation
- Does not regulate building form, resulting in unpredictable physical outcomes
- Does not address public realm quality, streetscape, or pedestrian experience
- Minimum parking requirements generate excess impervious surface and reduce density
- Lot-by-lot regulation ignores the cumulative effect of development on neighborhood character
- Variance and conditional use processes add uncertainty and political influence to development approvals

## 2. Performance Zoning

### Concept and Application

Performance zoning regulates land use based on the measurable impacts of development rather than the type of use. Any use is permitted in any zone provided it meets the performance standards for that zone. Performance standards typically address:

- **Traffic generation**: maximum vehicle trips per hour and per day (measured against ITE Trip Generation Manual or local data)
- **Noise**: maximum decibel levels at property boundaries (daytime and nighttime, measured in dBA)
- **Shadow/daylight**: maximum shadow impact on adjacent properties and public spaces (measured by shadow study at winter solstice and equinoxes)
- **Stormwater**: maximum impervious coverage or maximum runoff volume/rate (pre-development hydrology as baseline)
- **Air quality**: maximum emissions of particulates, VOCs, and criteria pollutants
- **Vibration**: maximum vibration levels at property boundaries
- **Lighting/glare**: maximum illumination at property boundaries (typically 0.5-1.0 foot-candle at residential boundary)
- **Odor**: no detectable odor at property boundary
- **Hazardous materials**: quantities and types regulated by separate environmental codes

### Performance Standard Example Matrix

| Standard | Zone A (Residential Character) | Zone B (Mixed Character) | Zone C (Employment Character) |
|----------|-------------------------------|-------------------------|------------------------------|
| Max traffic generation | 50 trips/day per acre | 200 trips/day per acre | 500 trips/day per acre |
| Max noise at boundary (day/night) | 55/45 dBA | 65/55 dBA | 75/65 dBA |
| Max impervious coverage | 40% | 60% | 80% |
| Max shadow on adjacent residential | 2 hours on Dec 21 | 4 hours on Dec 21 | No standard |
| Max lighting at boundary | 0.5 fc | 1.0 fc | 2.0 fc |
| Building height | 12m | 20m | 30m |
| Setback from residential zone | 10m + 1m per 3m height above 10m | 15m + 1m per 3m height above 10m | 20m + 1m per 3m height above 10m |

### Real-World Applications

- **Bucks County, Pennsylvania**: one of the earliest adopters of performance zoning in the U.S. (1970s), used performance criteria for residential and non-residential development in rural and suburban areas
- **Fort Collins, Colorado**: uses performance-based standards for noise, traffic, and visual impact as part of its development review process
- **Japan**: the Building Standards Act uses a form of performance zoning for daylight access (shadow regulations), with specific angular planes and shadow duration standards that vary by zone
- **Singapore**: performance-based approach to planning with detailed traffic impact assessment and environmental impact thresholds driving approval

### Advantages and Challenges

**Advantages**:
- Focuses on real impacts rather than arbitrary use categories
- Encourages creative design solutions
- Can accommodate new and unforeseen uses without code amendments
- More fair to property owners (any use permitted if impacts are acceptable)

**Challenges**:
- Requires technical expertise for review (traffic engineers, acousticians, environmental scientists)
- Unpredictable physical outcomes (the form of development is not regulated)
- Monitoring and enforcement are complex and resource-intensive
- Community members may find it difficult to understand what could be built nearby
- Pre-development impact modeling may not match post-development reality

## 3. Form-Based Codes

### Origin and Evolution

Form-based codes (FBCs) emerged from the New Urbanism movement in the 1980s and 1990s, pioneered by Andres Duany and Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk (DPZ) with their work on Seaside, Florida (1981) and subsequently the SmartCode. The Form-Based Codes Institute (FBCI) defines an FBC as "a land development regulation that fosters predictable built results and a high-quality public realm by using physical form (rather than separation of uses) as the organizing principle for the code."

### Model Code: Miami 21 Structure

Miami 21 (adopted 2009, updated regularly) is the first major city in the United States to adopt a comprehensive form-based code. Its structure:

  4.3  Civic/Institutional Uses

  4.5  Accessory Uses

ARTICLE 5: SPECIFIC TO ZONES
  5.1  T3 Zones
  5.2  T4 Zones
  5.3  T5 Zones

  5.7  CI (Civic Institutional)

Miami 21 Key Innovation: The code uses a "FLR" (Floor Lot Ratio, equivalent to FAR) bonus system where additional density is earned by providing public benefits (affordable housing, public open space, LEED certification, transit contributions, civic uses). Base FLR is lower; maximum FLR requires multiple public benefit contributions.

### Model Code: Denver Form-Based Code Structure

Denver adopted form-based zone districts as part of its comprehensive zoning code update (2010). Unlike Miami 21, Denver's FBC applies to specific zone districts rather than city-wide. Structure for a typical form-based zone district:

```
ZONE DISTRICT: [e.g., C-MX-8 (Urban Center, Mixed Use, 8 stories)]

SECTION 1: DISTRICT INTENT
  Purpose statement describing desired character

SECTION 2: BUILDING FORM STANDARDS
  2.1  Maximum Height: 8 stories / 110 feet
  2.2  Primary Street Setback: 0-10 ft build-to range
  2.3  Side Interior Setback: 0 ft (5 ft adjacent to protected district)
  2.4  Rear Setback: 10 ft (0 ft to alley)
  2.5  Build-To Alternative Compliance
  2.6  Upper Story Setback: above 5th story, 10 ft from primary street
  2.7  Lot Coverage: no maximum
  2.8  Transparency: ground floor 60% primary street, 40% side street
  2.9  Pedestrian Access: primary entrance on primary street

SECTION 3: USES
  3.1  Primary Uses: permitted by right
  3.2  Accessory Uses
  3.3  Temporary Uses
  3.4  Use Limitations (size caps, operational standards)

SECTION 4: PARKING AND LOADING
  4.1  Vehicle Parking: none required (or reduced minimum)
  4.2  Bicycle Parking: 1 per 4 DU, 1 per 5,000 sq ft commercial
  4.3  Loading: per use and size thresholds
  4.4  Parking Structure Design Standards

SECTION 5: DESIGN STANDARDS
  5.1  Street Level Activation
  5.2  Facade Variation
  5.3  Building Materials
  5.4  Roof Form and Screening
```

Denver Key Innovation: The zone district naming convention encodes key information. "C-MX-8" means Context (C = Urban Center), Use (MX = Mixed Use), and Height (8 = 8 stories). This makes the code navigable and self-documenting.

## 4. Transect-Based Zoning (SmartCode)

The SmartCode is the most comprehensive transect-based model code. It is an open-source document (free to download and calibrate) maintained by the Center for Applied Transect Studies (CATS). See the separate **transect-guide.md** reference for the complete T1-T6 specifications, frontage type catalog, building disposition types, and special district overlays.

### SmartCode Calibration Process

The SmartCode is a model (template) code that must be calibrated (customized) to local conditions before adoption. Calibration involves:

1. **Mapping sectors**: identify Growth Sector (areas intended for development or redevelopment), Preserved Sector (areas to remain undeveloped), and Restricted Growth Sector (environmentally or culturally sensitive areas with limited development capacity)
2. **Assigning community unit types**: Traditional Neighborhood Development (TND), Transit-Oriented Development (TOD), Regional Center Development (RCD), Conservation/Limited Development (CLD)
3. **Calibrating dimensional standards**: adjust height, setback, coverage, FAR, and density numbers to match local market conditions, climate, and community goals
4. **Selecting frontage and building types**: choose from the SmartCode menu or add locally appropriate types
5. **Specifying thoroughfare types**: calibrate cross-sections to local ROW widths, traffic volumes, and modal priorities
6. **Adding local definitions and administrative procedures**: integrate with existing municipal processes, legal framework, and institutional capacity

### Transect Ecology Principles

The transect approach treats human settlement as an ecology with six essential habitat types (T1-T6). Key principles:

- **Immersive quality**: each zone should be internally consistent in character. Suburban elements (wide setbacks, driveways, parking lots) should not appear in urban zones (T5, T6), and urban elements (zero setbacks, flat roofs, shopfronts) should not appear in suburban zones (T3)
- **Gradual transition**: zones should transition gradually. A T3 zone should be adjacent to T4, not jump to T6. The transect is a gradient, not a collection of isolated zones
- **Mixed-use by right**: uses are permitted based on their compatibility with the physical character of the zone, not segregated by category. A small office or shop in a T4 residential zone is appropriate because it fits the physical form
- **Complete neighborhoods**: every neighborhood should contain a range of transect zones from at least T3 to T5, with a center (T5), a general area (T4), and an edge (T3). This ensures walkable access to daily needs

## 5. Overlay Zones and Special Districts

### Purpose

Overlay zones add additional standards to the underlying base zoning to address specific conditions or objectives that cross zone boundaries. They "overlay" (supplement, modify, or supersede) the base zone standards.

### Common Overlay Types

**Historic Preservation Overlay**:
- Additional review for demolition, exterior alterations, new construction
- Standards for materials, proportions, window patterns, cornice lines, setback continuity
- Certificate of Appropriateness required from Historic Preservation Commission
- Typically references Secretary of Interior's Standards for Treatment of Historic Properties

**Floodplain Overlay**:
- Restrictions on development in FEMA-designated flood zones
- Minimum finished floor elevation (base flood elevation + freeboard, typically 0.3-1.0m)
- Prohibition on habitable space below flood elevation
- Requirement for flood-resistant materials and construction techniques
- Compensatory storage requirements

**Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) Overlay**:
- Applied within 400-800m walking distance of transit stations
- Increased density and FAR bonuses (typically 1.5-3x base zone)
- Reduced or eliminated parking minimums
- Pedestrian connectivity requirements (max block perimeter 400-500m)
- Required ground-floor retail on station-adjacent blocks
- Design standards for pedestrian-oriented development

**Environmental/Conservation Overlay**:
- Restrictions on development in sensitive areas (wetlands, steep slopes, riparian corridors, endangered species habitat)
- Additional setbacks from water bodies, wetlands, bluff edges
- Impervious surface limits more restrictive than base zone
- Native vegetation preservation and restoration requirements
- Stormwater management performance standards exceeding base requirements

**Design Review Overlay**:
- Requires design review by a Design Review Board or staff for all development within the overlay
- Design standards or guidelines for building form, materials, color, landscaping, signage
- Typically applied to gateways, corridors, civic precincts, or areas of special visual significance
- Review criteria may be mandatory (standards) or advisory (guidelines)

**Affordable Housing Overlay**:
- Density bonuses for provision of affordable dwelling units
- Reduced parking requirements for affordable housing
- By-right approval for projects meeting affordability thresholds
- Inclusionary zoning requirements (percentage of units at below-market rate)
- Fee-in-lieu option for projects that cannot provide on-site affordable units

### Special Districts (SD) in the SmartCode

The SmartCode provides for Special Districts (SD) that have unique standards not captured by the T1-T6 transect. These include:

- **SD-Civic**: government buildings, courthouses, libraries, post offices, fire stations (may have unique setback, height, or form requirements)
- **SD-Waterfront**: marina, port, beach, riverwalk (unique frontage types, flood-resistant construction, public access requirements)
- **SD-Campus**: university, hospital, corporate campus (internal circulation, larger building footprints, campus-wide master plan)
- **SD-Transit**: rail station, bus terminal, intermodal facility (high intensity, maximum density, connectivity standards)
- **SD-Industrial**: manufacturing, logistics, innovation district (larger lots, taller buildings, loading and access standards, buffering from residential)

## 6. Incentive Zoning and Density Bonuses

### Concept

Incentive zoning allows developers to exceed base zoning standards (typically height and FAR) in exchange for providing public benefits. This is a voluntary system: the developer may build by-right at the base standards, or choose to provide public benefits to access bonus capacity.

### Common Public Benefits and Bonus Values

| Public Benefit | Typical FAR Bonus | Typical Height Bonus | Notes |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| Affordable housing (20% of units at 80% AMI) | +1.0-2.0 FAR | +1-3 stories | Most common and most valued incentive |
| Public open space (plaza or park accessible to public) | +0.5-1.0 FAR per 0.1 ha of space | +1-2 stories | Space must be maintained by developer |
| LEED Gold or Platinum certification | +0.5-1.0 FAR | +1 story | Or equivalent green building certification |
| Transit infrastructure contribution | +0.5 FAR | +1 story | May include station improvements, bike-share, transit passes |
| Public art (1-2% of construction cost) | +0.25-0.5 FAR | -- | Curated by public art program |
| Daycare facility (licensed, on-site) | +0.5 FAR | +1 story | Minimum 150 sqm, licensed operator |
| Historic preservation (on-site or TDR) | +0.5-1.0 FAR | +1-2 stories | Preservation of designated structure |
| Workforce housing (units at 120% AMI) | +0.5-1.0 FAR | +1 story | Less deep subsidy than affordable |
| Underground parking (no surface lots) | +0.25-0.5 FAR | -- | Incentive to eliminate visible parking |

### Transfer of Development Rights (TDR)

TDR programs allow the unused development capacity from a "sending site" (typically a historic building, open space, or agricultural land) to be transferred to a "receiving site" (typically a higher-intensity urban area). This preserves the sending site by economically compensating the owner through the sale of development rights, while concentrating growth in appropriate locations.

- Sending sites: historic landmarks, farmland, environmentally sensitive areas, affordable housing
- Receiving sites: transit corridors, downtown, designated growth areas
- Transfer ratio: typically 1:1 (one sqm of unused FAR from sending site = one sqm of bonus FAR at receiving site), but may be adjusted to account for land value differentials
- Administration: TDR bank or direct buyer-seller transaction, recorded on both sending and receiving site deeds

## 7. Parking Standards by Zoning System

### Conventional Minimums (Euclidean)

| Use | Typical Minimum | Notes |
|-----|----------------|-------|
| Single-family dwelling | 2 spaces/DU | Enclosed garage typically required |
| Multifamily dwelling | 1.0-1.5 spaces/DU | + guest parking 0.25/DU |
| Retail | 1 space/30 sqm GFA | 1 space/250-300 sq ft |
| Office | 1 space/30-40 sqm GFA | 1 space/300-400 sq ft |
| Restaurant | 1 space/4-6 seats | Or 1/10-15 sqm dining area |
| Hotel | 1 space/room | + employee and event parking |

### Form-Based Code Approach to Parking

Form-based codes typically eliminate or drastically reduce minimum parking requirements, especially in T5 and T6 zones, based on the following principles:

- **T3**: 1-2 spaces per DU on-site, rear-loaded garages or driveways
- **T4**: 0.5-1.5 spaces per DU, rear-loaded, shared lots permitted
- **T5**: no minimum or maximum 0.5/DU, structured parking required above a threshold (e.g., more than 20 spaces), no surface lots between building and street
- **T6**: no minimum, structured or underground only, parking podiums wrapped with active uses on street-facing sides

Additional form-based parking standards:
- Parking structure facades facing streets must be lined with habitable space or designed with architectural treatment
- Parking entrances limited to one per block face, maximum 7.5m wide
- Bicycle parking required: 1 space per 4 DU (residential), 1 per 10 employees (commercial), minimum 4 spaces at any commercial establishment
- Shared parking reductions permitted when uses have complementary peak demand patterns (e.g., office + residential + restaurant)

## 8. Dimensional Standard Calibration Guide

### Climate-Based Adjustments

| Climate | Height Adjustment | Setback Adjustment | Coverage Adjustment | Notes |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| Hot-arid | Taller ground floors (4.5m+) for natural ventilation | Narrower streets (0.5-1.0 H:W ratio) for shade | Higher coverage for continuous shade canopy | Courtyard building types preferred |
| Hot-humid | Taller ground floors (4.5m+) | Wider setbacks for cross-ventilation | Moderate coverage with breezeway requirements | Gallery and arcade frontages essential |
| Temperate | Standard (3.6-4.2m ground floor) | Standard per transect zone | Standard per transect zone | Seasonal variation in solar access important |
| Cold | Standard to tall ground floors | Standard, may increase for snow storage | Standard, consider snow loading on canopies | Enclosed connections between buildings encouraged |
| Tropical | Taller ground floors (4.5m+) | Wider for ventilation, narrower for rain protection | Moderate with covered walkway requirements | Monsoon drainage critical |

### Market-Based Adjustments

| Market Condition | Density Adjustment | Height Adjustment | Parking Adjustment |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Strong urban market | Higher density targets achievable | Taller buildings supportable | Lower parking ratios viable (transit and density support shared mobility) |
| Moderate market | Mid-range density targets | 3-6 stories typical maximum for wood-frame feasibility | 0.75-1.25 spaces/DU needed for absorption |
| Weak/emerging market | Lower initial density, plan for future intensification | 2-4 stories initially, design blocks for future taller buildings | Higher parking initially, design for future conversion |
| Suburban fringe | Lowest density initially, preserve land for future urbanization | 2-3 stories initially | 1.5-2.0 spaces/DU initially |
