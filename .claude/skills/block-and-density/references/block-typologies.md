# Block Typologies Reference

Comprehensive specifications for 12+ urban block typologies used in global practice. Each entry provides
dimensional data, performance metrics, design guidance, and built precedent. Use this reference when
selecting, configuring, or evaluating block designs for any urban context.

---

## 1. Perimeter Block (Closed Corners Variant)

**Description:**
The perimeter block is the foundational building block of European urbanism. Buildings line all four sides
of the block, forming a continuous street wall that encloses a semi-private courtyard. The closed-corner
variant wraps buildings around all corners without gaps, creating a fully enclosed interior space
separated from the public street. This typology produces the strongest street definition and the
highest sense of urban enclosure.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 70-100m (measured property line to property line)
- Length: 70-120m
- Block perimeter: 280-440m
- Building depth: 12-16m (single-aspect) or 14-18m (dual-aspect with central corridor)

**Section Description:**
Buildings rise 4-8 stories from the street edge with minimal or zero front setback. Ground floors are
typically 4.0-4.5m floor-to-floor for commercial uses, with residential floors above at 3.0-3.3m.
Rooflines may include mansard or set-back upper floors. The courtyard is accessed through one or more
archways or passages from the street level.

**Coverage Ratio:** 55-70%

**FAR Range:** 2.0-5.0

**Typical Height:** 4-8 stories (15-28m)

**Building Depth:** 14-18m (most common: 14-16m for residential with double-loaded corridor)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
The courtyard is a semi-private communal space, typically 25-50m across in the larger dimension.
Landscaped with trees, gardens, play areas, seating, and bicycle storage. Vehicular access is restricted
to servicing. The courtyard provides acoustic relief from street noise, a sheltered microclimate, and
daylight to interior-facing apartments. Mature trees can grow to 15-20m within larger courtyards.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Preferred: below-grade parking under the courtyard (1-2 levels)
- Alternative: rear-access parking within the courtyard (screened, limited capacity)
- On-street: parallel parking on surrounding streets supplements resident parking

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Active commercial uses (retail, cafes, services, co-working) on primary street frontages. Secondary
frontages may have residential lobbies, community rooms, or live-work units. Target: minimum 80%
active frontage on primary streets, minimum 50% on secondary streets. Entrance doors every 8-12m
for fine-grained activation (Gehl standard: 15+ doors per 100m for "active" rating).

**Advantages:**
- Strongest street spatial definition of any typology
- Excellent acoustic separation between noisy streets and quiet courtyards
- Highly efficient land use at medium-to-high densities
- Flexible ground-floor commercial space
- Strong sense of community identity within the block
- Proven typology with centuries of precedent

**Disadvantages:**
- North-facing courtyard facades receive limited direct sunlight at high latitudes
- Deep plans (>16m) require careful core and corridor design for daylight
- Closed corners can create dark, poorly ventilated corner apartments
- Below-grade parking is expensive
- Can feel monotonous if architectural variation within the block is insufficient

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Barcelona Eixample** (Ildefons Cerda, 1860): 113m x 113m blocks with chamfered 20m corners; original design at 2-3 stories with large gardens has been built out to 6-7 stories at FAR 3.5-4.0; 133km of continuous street frontage; the global benchmark for perimeter block urbanism
2. **Berlin Mitte / Kreuzberg IBA Blocks** (various architects, 1980s-present): 80-100m blocks, 5-7 stories, FAR 2.5-3.5; IBA program mandated courtyard-facing housing with minimum daylight standards; varied architectural expression block by block
3. **Vienna Grunderzeit Blocks** (19th century): 60-90m blocks, 5-6 stories, FAR 2.5-3.5; ground-floor commercial, upper-floor residential; large interior courtyards with mature trees; model for Viennese housing quality standards
4. **Amsterdam IJburg** (2000s): contemporary perimeter blocks, 60-80m, 4-6 stories, FAR 2.0-3.0; mixed-tenure with social housing; varied facade designs by multiple architects per block
5. **Copenhagen Nordhavn** (2010s-present): 70-90m perimeter blocks, 5-7 stories, FAR 2.5-3.5; waterfront location; ground-floor commercial; courtyard parking below grade

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Block perimeter between 280m and 440m
- [ ] Building depth does not exceed 18m
- [ ] Courtyard minimum dimension at least 21m (preferably 25m+)
- [ ] Ground floor height minimum 4.0m (commercial-ready)
- [ ] Active frontage on at least 80% of primary street facade
- [ ] Entrance doors spaced every 8-12m on primary frontage
- [ ] Height varies by 1-2 stories within the block
- [ ] Corner treatment is architecturally distinguished
- [ ] Mid-block passage provided if any facade exceeds 120m
- [ ] Below-grade or courtyard parking; no surface parking visible from street

## 2. Perimeter Block (Open Corners Variant)

**Description:**
A variation of the perimeter block where one or more corners are left open, creating gaps in the street
wall that allow visual and physical connections between the street and the courtyard. Open corners
improve ventilation and daylight penetration into the courtyard, create opportunities for entrance
courts and lobby spaces, and add spatial variety to the streetscape. The trade-off is weaker street
enclosure at corners.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 60-100m
- Length: 70-120m
- Block perimeter: 260-440m
- Corner opening: 8-15m gap at one or two corners (diagonal cut or removed building wing)
- Building depth: 12-16m

**Section Description:**
Similar to the closed-corner variant but with the building mass interrupted at selected corners.
The opening may be at grade only (pedestrian passage) or extend the full building height (gap in
the building volume). Often one corner is opened for pedestrian access to the courtyard while
the other three remain closed.

**Coverage Ratio:** 45-60%

**FAR Range:** 1.8-4.0

**Typical Height:** 4-7 stories (14-24m)

**Building Depth:** 12-16m

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
More permeable and visually connected to the street than the closed-corner variant. The open corner
creates a threshold space that is neither fully public nor fully private. Courtyard landscape can
be more generous due to slightly lower coverage. Better natural ventilation through the corner opening.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade preferred
- Open corner can serve as vehicular entry to below-grade or courtyard parking (if carefully designed to not conflict with pedestrian use)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Active commercial on main frontages. The open corner itself can be designed as a small public plaza,
entrance court, or cafe terrace. Residential lobbies can address the corner opening to create a
welcoming arrival sequence.

**Advantages:**
- Better ventilation and daylight than closed-corner variant
- Visual permeability creates more welcoming public realm
- Corner opening provides natural entry point and address for the block
- Slightly lower coverage allows more generous courtyard

**Disadvantages:**
- Weaker street enclosure at the open corner
- Corner opening can create wind funneling if not managed with planting and screens
- Reduced buildable area compared to closed-corner variant
- Acoustic separation between street and courtyard is compromised at the opening

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Berlin Prenzlauer Berg** (post-reunification infill): many blocks feature one open corner with a gate or arch marking the entry to the courtyard
2. **Hammarby Sjostad, Stockholm** (2000s): open-corner perimeter blocks with courtyard gardens visible from the street; 5-7 stories, FAR 2.0-3.0
3. **Vauban, Freiburg** (1990s-2000s): low-rise open-corner blocks, 3-5 stories, FAR 1.5-2.0; car-free courtyards with extensive planting
4. **GWL Terrein, Amsterdam** (1998): car-free perimeter block development with open corners providing pedestrian access to communal gardens

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Corner opening is minimum 8m and maximum 15m wide
- [ ] Opening does not create a wind tunnel (use planting, screens, or partial enclosure)
- [ ] Sightlines from street into courtyard are managed (not fully exposed)
- [ ] Corner opening is well-lit and maintained as a threshold space
- [ ] Maximum two corners open per block (otherwise enclosure is lost)
- [ ] All other rules from closed-corner variant apply

## 3. Courtyard Block (Single Court)

**Description:**
A building organized around a single central courtyard, smaller in scale than the perimeter block and
with the courtyard as the primary organizing element. The courtyard is the heart of the block, providing
communal open space, daylight, and a shared address for all residents. Building wings surround the
courtyard on three or four sides, creating an intimate, inward-focused community.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 50-70m
- Length: 50-80m
- Block perimeter: 200-300m
- Courtyard: 20-35m x 25-45m (single court)
- Building depth: 12-15m

**Section Description:**
3-6 stories of residential above active or residential ground floors. Building wings are typically
uniform in height, creating a consistent cornice line around the courtyard. The courtyard floor is
at ground level or slightly raised (0.5-1.0m) above street level for privacy.

**Coverage Ratio:** 45-55%

**FAR Range:** 1.5-3.0

**Typical Height:** 3-6 stories (10-21m)

**Building Depth:** 12-15m

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
The courtyard is a true communal garden: soft landscape with trees, planting beds, lawn areas,
children's play, benches, and communal gathering space. Bicycle parking and waste bins are screened
within the courtyard or in dedicated enclosures. The courtyard is typically car-free. It functions
as an extension of the dwelling for daily life: children playing, neighbors socializing, urban gardening.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade under courtyard (preferred)
- On-street parking on surrounding streets
- Small courtyard parking areas only for disabled access and loading (screened)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Can be fully residential (especially on quieter streets) or mixed-use with small shops and services
on primary frontages. Ground-floor units should have defensible private space (front gardens, raised
ground floors, planting buffers) to manage the transition between public street and private dwelling.

**Advantages:**
- Strong sense of community with shared courtyard
- Excellent daylight to all units (courtyard-facing and street-facing)
- Human scale (typically 3-6 stories)
- Good acoustic environment in the courtyard
- Efficient building footprint at moderate densities

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower FAR than perimeter block (smaller coverage, fewer floors)
- Courtyard management requires residents' cooperation or professional management
- Limited commercial space (fewer active frontage meters)
- Less urban street definition than perimeter block at lower heights

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Amsterdam Java Island** (Sjoerd Soeters et al., 2000s): courtyard blocks 60-70m, 4-6 stories, communal gardens, multiple architects per block, FAR 2.0-2.5
2. **Copenhagen Kartoffelraekkerne** (1870s): historic courtyard row houses, 2-3 stories, shared gardens, FAR 1.0-1.5, a beloved residential neighborhood
3. **Freiburg Vauban** (1990s-2000s): 50-60m courtyard blocks, 3-5 stories, car-free courtyards, solar-oriented, FAR 1.5-2.0
4. **Bo01, Malmo** (2001): courtyard blocks around Turning Torso, 3-6 stories, varied courtyard sizes, FAR 1.5-2.5, sustainable design showcase
5. **Borneo Sporenburg, Amsterdam** (West 8, 2000s): narrow courtyard blocks with individual house-like units, 3-4 stories, FAR 1.2-1.8

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Courtyard minimum 21m in narrowest dimension
- [ ] Courtyard floor receives minimum 2 hours direct sun at equinox (where latitude allows)
- [ ] Building depth does not exceed 15m for courtyard block (daylight-critical)
- [ ] Ground-floor courtyard-facing units have private terrace or garden (minimum 6 m2)
- [ ] One vehicular access point for servicing; otherwise courtyard is car-free
- [ ] Communal facilities (bike store, waste, mailboxes) integrated at ground floor

## 4. Courtyard Block (Multiple Courts)

**Description:**
A larger building complex organized around two or more courtyards, connected by building wings or
passages. Multiple courtyards allow different functions (e.g., one quiet garden, one children's play,
one more public entrance court) and create a richer spatial sequence within the block. Common in
larger urban blocks where a single courtyard would be too vast or where the block depth requires
interior subdivision.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 60-100m
- Length: 80-150m
- Block perimeter: 280-500m
- Courtyards: 2-4 courts, each 18-30m x 20-40m
- Building depth: 12-16m

**Section Description:**
4-7 stories with building wings dividing the block interior into distinct courtyard spaces. Wings
may be lower (2-3 stories) to allow daylight to pass over them into adjacent courts. The section
reveals a varied roofline with taller street-facing wings and lower courtyard-dividing wings.

**Coverage Ratio:** 50-65%

**FAR Range:** 2.0-4.0

**Typical Height:** 4-7 stories (14-24m)

**Building Depth:** 12-16m

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Each courtyard can have a distinct character: an arrival court (more public, paved, formal), a
garden court (soft landscape, play, relaxation), a service court (waste, deliveries, bike parking).
Passages between courts create a processional sequence from public street to private garden.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade under multiple courts (continuous basement slab)
- Service court can accommodate limited surface parking (screened)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Active commercial on primary street frontages. Lobbies and residential entrances on secondary streets
and facing courtyards. Service court facades can be less activated but should still have windows and
passive surveillance.

**Advantages:**
- Higher density than single-court block while maintaining courtyard amenity
- Differentiated outdoor spaces serve multiple user groups
- Rich spatial sequence through the block
- Efficient land use for medium-to-large blocks

**Disadvantages:**
- Internal building wings can create narrow passages with limited daylight
- More complex building management (multiple courtyards, passages)
- Higher construction cost (more external wall area, below-grade parking over larger area)
- Requires careful design to avoid dark, underused spaces between courts

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Vienna Karl-Marx-Hof** (Karl Ehn, 1930): monumental multiple-courtyard social housing, 1,382 apartments, 1.1km long, FAR 1.5, multiple landscaped courts
2. **Berlin IBA blocks, Kreuzberg** (1980s): multiple-courtyard infill blocks, 5-6 stories, mixed architects, FAR 2.5-3.0
3. **Amsterdam Eastern Docklands** (2000s): multiple-court blocks on artificial islands, 4-6 stories, FAR 2.0-3.0
4. **London Peabody Estates** (19th-20th century): philanthropic housing with multiple courts, 4-6 stories, dense but well-lit courtyard layouts

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Each individual courtyard meets minimum 18m dimension (21m preferred)
- [ ] Connecting passages between courts are minimum 4m wide and well-lit
- [ ] No unit is more than 30m from a courtyard space
- [ ] Internal wings do not exceed 4 stories if they would block daylight to adjacent courts
- [ ] Clear hierarchy of courts from public to private
- [ ] At least one court is car-free and dedicated to communal garden use

## 5. Superblock (Tower Arrangement Strategies)

**Description:**
The superblock contains multiple freestanding buildings (typically towers or slabs) arranged within a
large block defined by arterial or collector roads. The buildings sit within a shared landscape of
parks, pathways, playgrounds, and community facilities. Internal roads are local access only or
entirely pedestrianized. The superblock originated from modernist planning (Le Corbusier's Ville
Radieuse) and has been extensively refined in Singapore, Hong Kong, and Latin American contexts.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 150-400m
- Length: 150-400m
- Block perimeter: 600-1,600m
- Internal building spacing: 24-40m (tower-to-tower)
- Building footprint per tower: 400-1,200 m2

**Section Description:**
Towers of 8-40 stories rise from a landscaped ground plane. Podium levels (1-3 stories) may connect
towers at ground level, containing parking, retail, community facilities, and childcare. The section
shows towers spaced for daylight with generous landscape between them.

**Coverage Ratio:** 20-35% (tower footprints only, excluding landscape)

**FAR Range:** 2.0-8.0

**Typical Height:** 8-30+ stories (25-100m+)

**Building Depth:** 18-25m (tower plates are larger than courtyard block depths; point towers 18-20m, slab towers 14-16m deep x 40-80m long)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Open landscape rather than enclosed courtyards. Shared ground-level gardens, playgrounds, fitness
equipment, community gardens, pathways, and gathering spaces. The challenge is to make these spaces
feel welcoming and defined despite the lack of spatial enclosure. Strategies include: topography,
dense tree planting, pergolas, hedges, and low walls to create sub-spaces within the large landscape.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Multi-story parking structures within the superblock (often at the periphery or center)
- Below-grade parking under podium or landscape
- Surface parking lots (least preferred; only for very low-density superblocks)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
- Tower bases: lobbies, mailrooms, community rooms, bike storage
- Podium ground floors: small retail, convenience stores, cafes, medical clinics, childcare centers
- Important: superblocks often suffer from inactive ground floors; conscious effort required to program activity at the base of towers and along internal pathways

**Advantages:**
- Very high FAR achievable (up to 8.0+) with adequate open space
- Generous landscape and recreation area at ground level
- Good natural ventilation around freestanding towers (important in tropical climates)
- Can accommodate large populations efficiently (5,000-20,000 residents per superblock)
- Internal car-free zones possible (Barcelona Superilles model)

**Disadvantages:**
- Weak street enclosure; buildings read as objects rather than defining spaces
- Ground-level open spaces can feel windswept, undefined, and unsafe after dark
- Long walking distances within the superblock
- Often produces monotonous, repetitive environments unless deliberately varied
- Social isolation in tower living (Christopher Alexander's critique: above 4 stories, connection to ground is lost)
- Maintenance of large shared landscapes requires professional management

**Tower Arrangement Strategies:**
- **Parallel rows (north-south oriented):** maximizes east-west solar access between rows; creates corridors that can be windswept; common in early modernist housing (e.g., Pruitt-Igoe, Robin Hood Gardens)
- **Staggered / offset:** towers offset from each other to break wind corridors, create varied spaces, and eliminate direct overlooking; better spatial variety than parallel rows
- **Cluster:** groups of 3-5 towers around a shared courtyard-like space defined by the tower footprints and connecting podiums; creates micro-neighborhoods within the superblock; most socially successful arrangement
- **Pinwheel:** towers rotated at angles to each other, creating dynamic views and varied spatial relationships; avoids overlooking; used in Singapore and Hong Kong

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Singapore HDB Towns** (1960s-present): Toa Payoh, Ang Mo Kio, Punggol; 250-400m superblocks, 12-40 stories, FAR 2.5-5.0; highly refined model with void decks, sky gardens, and precinct pavilions
2. **Barcelona Superilles** (2016-present): repurposing existing Cerda grid superblocks (3x3 blocks = 400m) by restricting through-traffic and converting streets to public space; not tower-based but a superblock model for existing cities
3. **Brasilia Superquadras** (Lucio Costa, 1960): 280m x 280m blocks, 6-story pilotis buildings, FAR 1.0-1.5; open landscape with mature trees; a livable modernist model despite low density
4. **Barbican Estate, London** (Chamberlin, Powell and Bon, 1969-1976): towers up to 42 stories in a podium-landscape complex; FAR 3.5; brutalist architecture; excellent community facilities
5. **Punggol Waterway, Singapore** (2010s): new-generation HDB superblocks with waterfront, varied tower heights, mixed-income, integrated retail and transit; FAR 3.0-4.5

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Tower spacing minimum 24m (point towers) or 1.0H (whichever is greater)
- [ ] No more than 40% of ground level is hardscape (excluding building footprints)
- [ ] Internal pathways connect all towers to public transit stops within 400m
- [ ] Ground-floor uses activated: every tower base has at least one community or commercial function
- [ ] Tower arrangement avoids parallel rows (use staggered, cluster, or pinwheel)
- [ ] Wind impact assessment completed for tower arrangements above 15 stories
- [ ] Landscape design creates defined sub-spaces (not undifferentiated open lawn)
- [ ] Night-time lighting strategy for safety in ground-level open spaces

## 6. Row / Terrace House Block

**Description:**
A block composed of attached houses arranged in continuous rows, each with its own front door
on the street and a private rear garden. The row house is one of the most efficient low-rise
housing types, achieving moderate densities while providing every household with private outdoor
space and a direct relationship with the street. The typology produces strong, consistent street
frontages and characterful residential neighborhoods.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block width: 40-60m (two rows of houses back-to-back with rear gardens between)
- Block length: 100-200m
- Block perimeter: 280-520m
- Individual unit width: 4.5-7.5m (narrow terraces: 4.5-5.5m; wide terraces: 6.0-7.5m)
- Unit depth: 8-14m (ground floor); upper floors may be shorter

**Section Description:**
2-4 stories with a pitched or flat roof. Ground floor: living/dining or living/kitchen opening onto
a rear garden at grade. Upper floors: bedrooms and bathrooms. Basement: storage or parking in some
markets. Rear garden: 5-12m deep. The section shows a street-facing facade, through the dwelling,
to a rear garden, then to the rear garden of the adjacent row on the parallel street.

**Coverage Ratio:** 50-65%

**FAR Range:** 0.8-2.0

**Typical Height:** 2-4 stories (7-14m)

**Building Depth:** 8-14m (typically 10-12m for efficient residential plans)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
No communal courtyard; instead, every unit has a private rear garden (25-80 m2 typically). The
collective pattern of rear gardens creates a green heart within the block visible from upper-floor
windows. Front gardens or small setbacks (1-2m) provide a transition zone between the public
sidewalk and private dwelling.

**Parking Strategy:**
- On-street parallel parking (most common and space-efficient)
- Integral garages within the unit (ground-floor front, increases unit width requirement to 6m+)
- Rear mews lanes with garages (traditional London/Dublin model)
- Communal basement parking (for higher-end developments)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Residential front doors directly on the street, spaced at 4.5-7.5m intervals. This produces the
finest-grained, most activated street frontage of any typology. Bay windows, stoops, porches, and
front gardens create a rich threshold between public and private.

**Advantages:**
- Every unit has private outdoor space (rear garden)
- Finest-grained street frontage: maximum "eyes on the street" and entrance frequency
- Low construction cost (party walls, no elevators, no corridors)
- Flexible over time: can be subdivided (flats), extended (rear additions), or converted (live-work)
- Highly marketable and popular housing type across cultures
- Achieves moderate density without feeling dense (FAR 1.0-1.5)

**Disadvantages:**
- Limited density ceiling (FAR rarely exceeds 2.0)
- Rear gardens reduce privacy between back-to-back rows (upper-floor overlooking)
- Narrow units (under 5m) produce dark plans requiring lightwells
- Not suitable for high-density urban centers
- Car-dependent if not located near transit

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **London Georgian Terraces** (18th-19th century): Bath, Bloomsbury, Islington; 5-6m wide, 3-4 stories, FAR 1.5-2.0; the global benchmark for terraced housing
2. **Amsterdam Grachtengordel** (17th century): 5-7m wide canal houses, 3-5 stories, FAR 1.5-2.5; narrow and deep with rear gardens; a UNESCO World Heritage Site
3. **Brooklyn Brownstones** (19th century): 5-6m wide, 3-4 stories, FAR 1.2-1.8; stoops, bay windows, tree-lined streets; one of the most desirable housing typologies in the US
4. **Borneo Sporenburg, Amsterdam** (West 8, 2000s): contemporary row houses, 5-7m wide, 3 stories, FAR 1.2-1.5; innovative individual designs within a strict envelope
5. **Accordia, Cambridge** (Feilden Clegg Bradley, 2006-2008): Stirling Prize-winning terrace houses, 3 stories, varied widths, communal gardens, FAR 1.0-1.5

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Minimum unit width 4.5m (5.5m+ preferred for comfortable plans)
- [ ] Maximum building depth 14m (deeper requires internal lightwells)
- [ ] Rear garden minimum depth 6m (9m+ preferred)
- [ ] Front-to-front distance across street minimum 15m (including road and sidewalks)
- [ ] Back-to-back distance between rear facades minimum 21m
- [ ] Every unit has its own front door on the street
- [ ] Continuous terrace length does not exceed 120m without a break (passage, setback, or street)
- [ ] Variation in facade treatment every 3-5 units to avoid monotony

## 7. Tower-Podium (Hong Kong Model)

**Description:**
The Hong Kong tower-podium model places one or more residential or office towers on a large commercial
podium that covers most of the site. The podium typically contains a shopping mall, car park, and
community facilities. The tower(s) rise from the podium roof, set back from the podium edges.
This model maximizes both commercial GFA (podium) and residential/office GFA (tower) while
maintaining a continuous street-level commercial environment.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block: 60-100m x 60-100m
- Podium: covers 80-100% of the site
- Tower footprint: 600-1,500 m2 per tower (15-30% of site area)
- Tower setback from podium edge: 5-10m minimum

**Section Description:**
3-5 story podium (12-20m) with a tower rising 20-60 stories (60-200m) above. The podium provides
a landscaped deck (sky garden, swimming pool, recreational facilities) at podium roof level. The
tower emerges from this deck. Ground-floor podium is retail with covered pedestrian circulation.

**Coverage Ratio:** 80-100% (podium), 15-30% (tower above podium)

**FAR Range:** 6.0-12.0

**Typical Height:** Podium 3-5 stories + Tower 20-60 stories

**Building Depth:** Podium: 40-80m (deep commercial floor plates); Tower: 18-25m (residential) or 25-40m (office)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Podium roof deck functions as the primary open space: landscaped gardens, swimming pool, children's
playground, barbecue areas. This elevated open space is private to tower residents (not public).
Ground-level public open space is minimal due to high podium coverage. Some jurisdictions (e.g.,
Hong Kong) require public open space provision at ground level or on podium accessible to the public.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Integrated within the podium (typically 2-3 levels of parking above retail or below grade)
- Highly efficient: parking is hidden within the commercial podium structure
- Some sites include automated parking systems within the podium

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Retail mall entrances, shop frontages, restaurant terraces, transit connections. The best examples
maintain active, fine-grained retail at street level despite the large podium footprint. The worst
examples present blank walls, service entrances, and parking ramps to the street.

**Advantages:**
- Extremely high FAR (8-12) with proven livability
- Efficient integration of parking, retail, and residential in a single structure
- Podium deck provides generous private recreational space
- Strong retail environment at street level (when well-designed)
- Wind protection at podium level (tower wind effects are above the podium deck)

**Disadvantages:**
- Podium covers most of the site, eliminating ground-level open space
- Podium rooftop open space is private, not public (contributes nothing to the city)
- Deep podium creates large, often blank facades on side and rear streets
- Extremely expensive to construct (podium structure must support towers above)
- Can create oppressive street environment if podium height exceeds 5 stories
- Social stratification: podium users (shoppers) and tower residents (private deck) occupy different realms

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Hong Kong typical** (1980s-present): the ubiquitous development model; podium 100% coverage, 3-4 floors retail/parking, towers 30-50 stories, FAR 8-12; examples include Pacific Place, Taikoo Shing, Laguna Verde
2. **Singapore ION Orchard / CapitaGreen** (2000s-2010s): refined tower-podium with extensive public space integration and landscaped podium decks; URA guidelines require tower setbacks and podium height limits
3. **Melbourne CBD** (2000s-present): podium 3-5 stories matching heritage street wall, tower above set back 10m from podium edge; Melbourne Planning Scheme Clause 22.01 controls podium-tower relationship

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Podium height does not exceed 5 stories (20m) to maintain human-scale street
- [ ] Tower setback from podium edge minimum 5m (10m preferred for daylight and wind)
- [ ] Ground-floor frontage is at least 60% active retail/commercial (no blank walls, parking ramps hidden)
- [ ] Podium deck includes minimum 20% of site area as open-air landscaped space
- [ ] Tower floor plate does not exceed 1,500 m2 (larger plates block daylight and views)
- [ ] Wind impact assessment for tower above podium completed
- [ ] Public open space contribution at ground level or accessible podium level as required

## 8. Tower-Podium (Vancouver Model)

**Description:**
The Vancouver model refines the Hong Kong tower-podium by emphasizing urban livability, view corridors,
and a stronger relationship between tower and street. The podium is lower (2-4 stories), more finely
grained, and designed to match the scale of the surrounding neighborhood. Towers are slender (maximum
floor plate often limited to 600 m2), well-separated, and oriented to protect views of mountains
and water. This model has been widely influential and is considered the gold standard for tower
livability in North America.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block: 60-100m x 60-100m (typically one city block)
- Podium: 3-4 stories, covering 60-80% of the site
- Tower footprint: 500-750 m2 (slender point towers)
- Tower spacing: minimum 24m face-to-face between towers on the same block

**Section Description:**
2-4 story podium (8-15m) containing townhouses, live-work units, or commercial uses facing the
street. Tower(s) of 20-40 stories rise from the podium, setback from all podium edges. The podium
roof often contains communal amenity space. Townhouse units within the podium have their own front
doors on the street, maintaining fine-grained activation at grade.

**Coverage Ratio:** 60-80% (podium), 10-20% (tower above podium)

**FAR Range:** 4.0-7.0

**Typical Height:** Podium 2-4 stories + Tower 20-40 stories

**Building Depth:** Podium: 14-20m (townhouses/live-work); Tower: 18-22m (slender residential point tower)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Podium courtyard or podium roof deck provides private amenity space. Publicly accessible plazas or
parkettes often provided at ground level at the base of the tower or at block corners. The emphasis
is on public benefit: view corridors, public open space, and street-level livability.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade parking (2-3 levels) under the podium and tower
- Parking access from rear lane or secondary street (not primary frontage)
- Parking ratios increasingly reduced in transit-rich areas (0.5-1.0 per unit)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Podium townhouses with individual front doors create a residential street character. Commercial uses
at corners and on main streets. The tower base (lobby) is recessed or set back to create a public
forecourt. Emphasis on fine-grained, human-scale ground-floor treatment.

**Advantages:**
- Slender towers preserve views and daylight between buildings
- Podium townhouses create excellent street-level urbanity
- Lower podium height (2-4 stories) maintains neighborhood scale
- Public open space contributions at ground level
- Widely considered the most livable high-density typology in North America

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower FAR than Hong Kong model (slender towers yield less floor area)
- Podium townhouses are expensive to build and maintain
- Requires large sites to achieve meaningful tower separation (minimum 24m)
- View corridor requirements can constrain tower placement and orientation
- Below-grade parking is expensive in high water table areas

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Vancouver Concord Pacific Place** (1990s-2010s): the original Vancouver model; 40 towers on former Expo lands; podium townhouses, slender towers, public waterfront park; FAR 4.5-6.0
2. **Vancouver Coal Harbour** (2000s): refined model with lower podium heights, more public open space, strict view corridor protection
3. **Toronto CityPlace** (2000s-2010s): application of Vancouver model in Toronto; mixed results due to larger tower floor plates and less refined podium design
4. **Portland Pearl District** (2000s): lower-scale version; 5-7 story podiums with occasional towers; FAR 4.0-6.0; excellent street-level urbanism

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Tower floor plate maximum 600-750 m2 (slender tower)
- [ ] Tower setback from podium edge minimum 3m (5m preferred)
- [ ] Tower-to-tower spacing minimum 24m (face-to-face)
- [ ] Podium height does not exceed 4 stories
- [ ] Podium contains townhouses or live-work units with front doors on street
- [ ] Publicly accessible open space minimum 10% of site area at ground level
- [ ] View corridor analysis completed; tower placement does not block protected views
- [ ] Parking access from lane or secondary street only

## 9. Villa / Garden Block

**Description:**
A low-density residential block with detached or semi-detached houses set within generous private
gardens. Buildings are set back from all property boundaries, surrounded by vegetation, and accessed
from tree-lined streets. This typology creates the leafy, suburban character associated with Garden
Cities and affluent residential neighborhoods. It achieves the lowest FAR of any typology but provides
the most private open space per dwelling.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block width: 50-80m
- Block length: 80-120m
- Block perimeter: 260-400m
- Individual lot: 250-800 m2
- Building footprint: 80-250 m2 per dwelling

**Section Description:**
1-3 stories with pitched or flat roof. Front setback 3-10m, side setbacks 2-5m, rear setback 6-15m.
Gardens on all sides. The section shows the house as a freestanding object within a green setting,
separated from neighbors by vegetation and distance.

**Coverage Ratio:** 25-35%

**FAR Range:** 0.3-0.8

**Typical Height:** 1-3 stories (4-10m)

**Building Depth:** 10-15m

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Private gardens on all four sides of each dwelling. Mature trees, hedges, lawns, flower beds, and
outdoor living spaces. The collective effect of multiple gardens creates a green, park-like
neighborhood character visible from the street through front gardens and from upper floors.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Private garages or carports on-lot (1-3 spaces per dwelling)
- Driveways from the street or rear lane
- On-street parking for visitors

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Residential front doors, porches, and front gardens. No commercial uses (typically prohibited by
zoning). The street interface is defined by garden walls, hedges, or fences at 0.5-1.5m height.

**Advantages:**
- Maximum private outdoor space per dwelling
- Quiet, green, and private living environment
- High market value and broad market appeal
- Simple construction (no shared structure)
- Maximum flexibility for additions and modifications over time

**Disadvantages:**
- Very low density (FAR 0.3-0.8); inefficient use of urban land
- Car-dependent (too low density to support frequent transit)
- High infrastructure cost per dwelling (long utility runs, wide streets)
- Contributes to urban sprawl if not bounded by growth limits
- Lacks the vitality and convenience of mixed-use urban neighborhoods

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Letchworth Garden City** (Barry Parker and Raymond Unwin, 1903): the original Garden City; detached and semi-detached houses, generous gardens, tree-lined streets, FAR 0.3-0.5
2. **Hampstead Garden Suburb** (Raymond Unwin, 1907): refined Garden City principles with Arts and Crafts architecture; FAR 0.3-0.6; hedged front gardens, communal greens
3. **Chandigarh Sectors** (Le Corbusier, 1950s): modernist villa plots within 800m x 1,200m sectors; 1-2 stories, FAR 0.3-0.5; generous lots with fruit trees and gardens
4. **Singapore Good Class Bungalow Areas** (GCBAs): preserved low-density zones within high-density Singapore; lots minimum 1,393 m2, 2 stories max, FAR 0.35-0.40; the most valuable residential land in Asia

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Minimum lot area 250 m2 (detached), 150 m2 (semi-detached)
- [ ] Building coverage does not exceed 35% of lot area
- [ ] Front setback minimum 3m with landscaping
- [ ] Side setback minimum 2m (detached), 0m on party wall side (semi-detached)
- [ ] Rear garden minimum depth 6m
- [ ] Street trees planted at 8-10m spacing
- [ ] Building height does not exceed 3 stories or 10m
- [ ] Garage or carport does not dominate the street facade (set back or side-accessed)

## 10. Mansion Block

**Description:**
A large, dignified residential apartment building occupying a significant portion of a city block.
Mansion blocks are typically 5-8 stories, with generous unit sizes, shared lobbies and staircases,
and a strong architectural presence on the street. They differ from perimeter blocks in being
conceived as a single building (or small number of buildings) rather than a continuous ring of
attached buildings. Common in late 19th and early 20th century European cities.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 40-60m
- Length: 60-80m
- Block perimeter: 200-280m
- Building footprint: 1,200-3,000 m2

**Section Description:**
5-8 stories with a prominent ground floor (often rusticated or with a colonnade), a piano nobile
(first floor with higher ceilings), and a mansard or set-back upper floor. Generous floor-to-floor
heights (3.3-3.8m) throughout, reflecting a pre-modern standard of domestic space.

**Coverage Ratio:** 50-65%

**FAR Range:** 2.0-4.0

**Typical Height:** 5-8 stories (18-28m)

**Building Depth:** 14-20m (often with lightwells for interior rooms)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Communal rear garden, typically landscaped with mature trees and formal planting. The garden is
private to residents and accessed from the ground-floor lobby or directly from garden-level
apartments. Lightwells within the building provide ventilation and daylight to interior rooms.

**Parking Strategy:**
- On-street parking (typical for historic mansion blocks)
- Below-grade parking (for new-build mansion blocks)
- Rear mews parking (if site includes mews/lane access)

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
The ground floor is typically residential (often the most desirable units with garden access) or
contains a concierge lobby, mail room, and communal facilities. Some mansion blocks have
commercial uses at ground level on busier streets. The architectural treatment of the ground
floor establishes the building's character: entrance porticos, bay windows, balconies, and
decorative stonework.

**Advantages:**
- Generous, high-quality residential space
- Strong architectural identity and street presence
- Communal garden provides shared amenity without the complexity of a full courtyard block
- Adaptable: units can be subdivided or combined to serve different household sizes over time
- Proven typology with strong market appeal

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower density than perimeter block due to smaller footprint relative to block area
- Deep plans (>16m) require lightwells, which reduce efficiency
- Generous floor-to-floor heights reduce the number of floors within a given building height
- Can be difficult to provide adequate parking without below-grade construction
- Not suitable for very high densities (FAR ceiling approximately 4.0)

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Paris Haussmann Apartments** (1850s-1920s): the archetype; 6-7 stories, stone facades, iron balconies, mansard roofs, piano nobile, FAR 3.0-4.0; continuous street walls define Paris's character
2. **London Mansion Blocks** (1880s-1930s): Kensington, Maida Vale, Battersea; 5-7 stories, red brick, communal gardens, generous units (100-200 m2), FAR 2.5-3.5
3. **Madrid Ensanche** (19th century): Salamanca district; 6-8 stories, stone and brick, interior patios, commercial ground floors on main streets, FAR 3.0-4.0
4. **Buenos Aires Palermo** (early 20th century): 5-7 stories, French-inspired facades, generous balconies, interior lightwells, FAR 2.5-3.5
5. **New York Pre-War Apartment Buildings** (1900s-1940s): Upper West Side, Park Avenue; 8-15 stories (larger than European mansion blocks), doorman lobbies, generous units, FAR 4.0-8.0

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Ground-floor ceiling height minimum 3.5m (4.0m preferred for flexibility)
- [ ] Upper-floor ceiling height minimum 2.8m (3.0m+ for mansion block character)
- [ ] Building depth with lightwells does not produce any room deeper than 8m from a window
- [ ] Communal garden minimum 200 m2 (preferably 400 m2+)
- [ ] Main entrance architecturally expressed (portico, canopy, set-back, or double-height lobby)
- [ ] Facade divided into base, middle, and top (classical composition)
- [ ] Balconies or bay windows on at least 50% of street-facing units
- [ ] Roof treated as a design element (mansard, cornice, set-back penthouse)

## 11. Campus Block

**Description:**
A large block containing multiple buildings arranged in a landscaped setting, organized around
quadrangles, courts, or open lawns. Primarily used for institutional purposes (universities,
hospitals, corporate campuses, research parks) but increasingly adapted for mixed-use urban
neighborhoods. Buildings are spaced for daylight, views, and pedestrian circulation through the
campus. The character is more spacious and park-like than an urban block.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Width: 100-200m
- Length: 100-200m
- Block perimeter: 400-800m
- Individual building footprints: 500-5,000 m2
- Building spacing: 15-40m

**Section Description:**
1-6 stories with varied building heights reflecting functional hierarchy (e.g., a library or
assembly hall taller than classroom buildings). Buildings may be connected by covered walkways,
bridges, or underground passages. The ground plane flows between buildings as a continuous landscape.

**Coverage Ratio:** 25-40%

**FAR Range:** 0.5-2.0

**Typical Height:** 1-6 stories (4-24m)

**Building Depth:** 14-25m (varies by function: narrow for classrooms and offices, deep for labs, libraries, and auditoriums)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Formal quadrangles, lawns, groves of trees, water features, sculpture gardens, and informal
gathering spaces. The landscape is the primary organizing element, not the building perimeter.
Pathways create desire-line connections between buildings and to transit stops, parking areas,
and surrounding streets.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Peripheral surface lots (traditional but wasteful of land)
- Structured parking at campus edges (preferred for denser campuses)
- Below-grade parking under buildings or open spaces (most urban solution)
- Strong bicycle and transit access to reduce parking demand

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Entrances, lobbies, cafes, common rooms, and publicly accessible functions (galleries, bookshops,
welcome centers) at ground level. Transparent ground-floor facades to activate pathways and create
a sense of invitation. Blank service facades relegated to interior service courts.

**Advantages:**
- Generous daylight and landscape for every building
- Flexible layout can accommodate diverse building types and functions
- Adaptable over time: new buildings can be added within the campus framework
- Strong sense of place and institutional identity
- Pedestrian-friendly internal circulation

**Disadvantages:**
- Low density (FAR rarely exceeds 2.0 with campus typology)
- Large land requirement
- Weak relationship with surrounding urban fabric (campus as island)
- Peripheral parking creates dead edges facing surrounding streets
- Internal circulation can be complex and illegible without clear wayfinding

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Oxford University Colleges** (13th century-present): quadrangle plan; enclosed courts create a cloistered, contemplative environment; FAR 0.5-1.5
2. **MIT Campus, Cambridge MA** (various, 20th century): connected buildings with interior corridors; the "infinite corridor" creates a continuous indoor spine; FAR 1.0-2.0
3. **Apple Park, Cupertino** (Foster + Partners, 2017): 461m ring building in 71-hectare landscape; FAR 0.3; an extreme campus model
4. **Novartis Campus, Basel** (multiple architects, 2000s-present): urban campus with buildings by Gehry, SANAA, Moneo, and others; FAR 1.0-1.5; open to the public
5. **One North, Singapore** (Zaha Hadid Architects masterplan, 2000s): research and innovation campus; 2-6 stories, FAR 1.5-2.5; mixed-use with retail and residential components

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Clear campus framework plan with defined building plots, landscape zones, and circulation routes
- [ ] Primary pedestrian routes connect all buildings to transit and parking within 400m
- [ ] Building entrances face primary pathways (not parking lots)
- [ ] Parking structures wrapped with active uses or screened with landscape
- [ ] At least one publicly accessible ground-floor function per building
- [ ] Landscape plan includes formal and informal spaces, canopy trees, and stormwater management
- [ ] Wayfinding system with maps, signage, and distinctive landmarks at decision points
- [ ] Edge condition: campus buildings address surrounding streets, not just internal spaces

## 12. L-Shaped Block

**Description:**
An L-shaped building wraps around two sides of a block, leaving the remaining area as open
space, courtyard, or garden. The L-shape creates a defined outdoor room on one corner while
maintaining a strong street frontage on two sides. This typology is common in urban infill
situations where a full perimeter block is not possible, or where the site geometry creates
an L-shaped building footprint naturally.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block width: 50-80m
- Block length: 60-100m
- L-shape wing lengths: 40-80m x 40-60m
- Building depth: 12-16m

**Section Description:**
3-7 stories along both wings of the L, with the corner element often 1-2 stories taller as a
landmark. The open side of the L faces south (northern hemisphere) where possible to maximize
solar access to the courtyard and garden. A wall, hedge, or low building may close the open
sides of the L to create partial enclosure.

**Coverage Ratio:** 35-50%

**FAR Range:** 1.5-3.5

**Typical Height:** 3-7 stories (10-24m)

**Building Depth:** 12-16m

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
The L-shape creates a sheltered outdoor space on the open side. This space can be a communal garden,
playground, or semi-public plaza depending on the urban context. The concave angle of the L provides
wind shelter and a sense of enclosure without fully closing the block.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade (preferred)
- On the open side of the L (screened and limited)
- On-street

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Active commercial on the street-facing sides of the L. Residential lobbies or community rooms
at the corner junction of the two wings. The open side of the L can have ground-floor units with
private gardens addressing the courtyard space.

**Advantages:**
- Creates defined outdoor space without requiring a full perimeter block
- Adaptable to irregular sites and corner lots
- Strong street frontage on two sides with "quiet" garden side
- Good solar access to courtyard when oriented correctly
- Can be combined with adjacent L-blocks to form U-shapes or full perimeters

**Disadvantages:**
- Less efficient than a full perimeter block (lower coverage)
- Weaker street definition on the open sides of the L
- Corner junction of the L can create awkward apartment plans
- Open sides of the courtyard lack enclosure; wind and noise penetration

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Berlin Kreuzberg infill** (IBA, 1980s): many L-shaped blocks filling gaps in war-damaged urban fabric; 5-6 stories, FAR 2.0-3.0
2. **Vienna Seestadt Aspern** (2010s-present): L-shaped and U-shaped blocks creating courtyards and public spaces; 5-8 stories, FAR 2.0-3.5
3. **Copenhagen Orestad** (2000s): L-shaped apartment buildings with south-facing gardens; 4-7 stories, FAR 2.0-3.0

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Corner of the L receives special architectural treatment (taller, articulated, commercial)
- [ ] Open side of the L faces south or southeast (northern hemisphere) for solar access
- [ ] Courtyard space within the L is minimum 20m in narrowest dimension
- [ ] Low wall, hedge, or fence (1.2-1.5m) along open sides of the L for partial enclosure
- [ ] Active ground-floor uses on all street-facing facades
- [ ] Junction of the L-wings designed as a distinctive element (lobby, cafe, community space)

## 13. U-Shaped Block

**Description:**
A U-shaped building wraps around three sides of a block, leaving one side open. The open side
typically faces a street, park, or waterfront, creating a semi-enclosed courtyard that opens
to the public realm. The U-shape provides strong street definition on three sides while offering
a generous courtyard with a south-facing opening (when correctly oriented). It is one of the most
versatile and commonly used block typologies in European urban design.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block width: 50-80m
- Block length: 70-100m
- U-shape wings: 60-90m (long wings) x 40-60m (short wing)
- Courtyard: 25-45m wide x 30-60m deep
- Building depth: 12-16m

**Section Description:**
4-7 stories with the rear wing (connecting the two long wings) typically at the same height or
slightly lower. The courtyard is open to the street on one side, creating a visual and physical
connection between the public realm and the semi-private courtyard space.

**Coverage Ratio:** 40-55%

**FAR Range:** 1.8-3.5

**Typical Height:** 4-7 stories (14-24m)

**Building Depth:** 12-16m

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
The courtyard is the defining feature. It is semi-enclosed on three sides, creating a sheltered,
sunny outdoor room when the opening faces south. The courtyard can be communal garden, formal
entrance court, or semi-public plaza accessible from the street. The depth and width of the
courtyard determine its character: narrow and deep = intimate; wide and shallow = more public.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade under courtyard (preferred; preserves ground-level garden)
- Courtyard-level parking only if courtyard is raised as a podium deck
- On-street parking on surrounding streets

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Active commercial on the street-facing facades of all three wings. The courtyard-facing ground
floor may have residential units with private gardens, community rooms, or additional commercial
space (particularly on the ground floor of the connecting wing, which faces into the courtyard
and can be a cafe or gallery).

**Advantages:**
- Generous, well-proportioned courtyard space
- Strong street definition on three sides
- Good solar access to courtyard when opening faces south
- Flexible and adaptable typology for a wide range of contexts
- Can be assembled in pairs to form full perimeter blocks (two U-shapes back-to-back)

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower density than full perimeter block (one side open)
- Open side provides less street definition than a closed block
- Wind can channel through the courtyard opening
- Privacy concern if the courtyard is visible from the street

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Copenhagen Norrebro** (various, 19th-20th century): classic U-shaped apartment blocks, 5-6 stories, communal courtyards with mature trees, FAR 2.0-3.0
2. **Amsterdam Java Island** (2000s): U-shaped blocks opening to canals, 4-6 stories, varied architects, FAR 2.0-2.5
3. **Stockholm Hammarby Sjostad** (2000s): U-shaped blocks opening to waterfront, 5-7 stories, green courtyards, FAR 2.0-3.0
4. **Vienna Sonnwendviertel** (2010s): U-shaped blocks around public parks, 5-8 stories, mixed-use, FAR 2.5-3.5

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Courtyard opening faces south (northern hemisphere) or toward the best view/amenity
- [ ] Courtyard minimum 25m wide at the opening and 25m deep
- [ ] Connecting wing (back of the U) does not exceed the height of the side wings
- [ ] Ground floor of the connecting wing is activated (community, commercial, or elevated lobby)
- [ ] Courtyard is at least partially visible from the street (inviting, safe, surveilled)
- [ ] Wind protection at the courtyard opening (trees, screens, partial wall)
- [ ] Active frontages on all three street-facing facades

## 14. Linear / Slab Block

**Description:**
A single elongated building with a narrow plan (12-16m deep) and a long facade (40-150m+).
Linear blocks are typically double-loaded corridor buildings (apartments on both sides of a central
corridor) and produce a strong, repetitive street wall. They are common in modernist social housing
and in contemporary urban developments where long street frontages need to be defined efficiently.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Block width: 12-16m (building depth only, plus setbacks)
- Block length: 40-150m+
- Building depth: 12-16m (double-loaded corridor)

**Section Description:**
4-12 stories of apartments flanking a central corridor. The corridor is typically internal
(windowless, artificially lit) or semi-open (skip-stop with gallery access every 2-3 floors).
End walls of the slab may be blank or minimally fenestrated. The long facades face the street
and the rear open space.

**Coverage Ratio:** 30-50% (of the wider block including setback areas)

**FAR Range:** 1.5-4.0

**Typical Height:** 4-12 stories (14-40m)

**Building Depth:** 12-16m (rarely exceeds 16m for daylight reasons)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
No courtyard in the traditional sense. Open space exists in the gaps between parallel slabs
(20-40m spacing) or in front/rear setback areas. This space can be landscaped but often lacks
enclosure and definition. The space between parallel slabs is the most problematic aspect of
this typology.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Below-grade (preferred)
- Surface parking between slabs (common in social housing; produces dead space)
- On-street parallel parking along the long facades

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Entrance lobbies at regular intervals (every staircore, typically 20-30m). Commercial uses
possible at ground level if the block faces an active street. Back-of-slab ground floors often
have secondary entrances or service functions.

**Advantages:**
- Highly efficient building plan (double-loaded corridor maximizes units per core)
- Every apartment has dual aspect (front and back windows)
- Strong, continuous street frontage
- Simple, repetitive structure reduces construction cost
- Good cross-ventilation in tropical climates

**Disadvantages:**
- Monotonous repetition over long facades
- Blank end walls create dead frontages at block ends
- Space between parallel slabs often lacks definition and feels residual
- Central corridors can be dark, poorly ventilated, and insecure
- Limited adaptability: the plan is optimized for one function (apartments)

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Berlin Hansaviertel** (various architects, 1957): slab blocks by Aalto, Niemeyer, Gropius, and others; 4-10 stories in landscape; Interbau exhibition
2. **Robin Hood Gardens, London** (Alison and Peter Smithson, 1972): twin slab blocks with "streets in the sky" (gallery access corridors); 7-10 stories; demolished 2017-2024
3. **Siemensstadt, Berlin** (Scharoun, Gropius, et al., 1930): early modernist slab housing, 4-5 stories, generous landscape between blocks; UNESCO World Heritage
4. **Singapore SkyVille @ Dawson** (WOHA, 2015): contemporary slab typology with sky gardens, community terraces, and cross-ventilation; 47 stories; reimagining the slab block for the tropics

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] Building length does not exceed 80m without a break, setback, or change in direction
- [ ] End walls are treated architecturally (windows, art, planting) not left blank
- [ ] Spacing between parallel slabs is minimum 1.5x building height
- [ ] Central corridor has natural light at each end and ideally at intermediate points
- [ ] Ground-floor entrances every 20-30m for fine-grained access
- [ ] Facade varies in rhythm, material, or color to break up repetition
- [ ] Landscape between slabs is designed as defined spaces (not leftover gaps)

## 15. Hybrid / Mixed-Typology Block

**Description:**
A block that combines two or more building typologies within a single block framework to achieve
density targets, create spatial variety, and respond to different site conditions on different
edges of the block. For example, townhouses on the south side, a mid-rise apartment building on
the north side, and a point tower at the corner. Hybrid blocks are increasingly common in
contemporary urban design as practitioners seek to combine the best qualities of multiple
typologies while avoiding the monotony of single-typology blocks.

**Dimensional Plan:**
- Variable; depends on the combination of typologies
- Typically 60-120m x 60-150m
- Block perimeter: 240-540m

**Section Description:**
The section reveals a varied profile: low-rise elements (2-3 stories) transitioning to mid-rise
(4-8 stories) and potentially a point tower (15-30 stories). Each typological element has its own
section logic (row houses with gardens, courtyard apartments with communal space, tower with
podium amenity). The challenge is to compose these elements into a coherent whole.

**Coverage Ratio:** Variable (30-65%, depending on the mix)

**FAR Range:** Variable (1.5-6.0, depending on the mix)

**Typical Height:** Mixed: 2-30 stories depending on typologies combined

**Building Depth:** Variable by typology segment: 10-12m (row houses), 14-16m (apartments), 18-22m (tower)

**Courtyard / Open Space Character:**
Multiple open space types within a single block: private gardens (townhouses), communal courtyard
(apartments), podium deck (tower), and shared block-level open space. The variety of open space
types is one of the key attractions of the hybrid block.

**Parking Strategy:**
- Shared below-grade parking spanning the entire block (most efficient)
- Townhouse garages for low-rise elements
- Podium parking for tower elements
- On-street for supplementary visitor parking

**Ground Floor Treatment:**
Varies by street condition: townhouse front doors on residential streets, commercial ground
floors on busy streets, lobby and community uses at the tower base. The variety of ground-floor
treatments creates a richer, more diverse street experience than a single-typology block.

**Advantages:**
- Maximum spatial variety and architectural interest within a single block
- Can optimize each edge of the block for its specific urban condition
- Achieves higher density than single-typology mid-rise while maintaining human scale
- Multiple housing types within one block supports social diversity
- Flexible framework can accommodate different architects and development phases

**Disadvantages:**
- Complex to design, manage, and construct (multiple structural systems, access strategies)
- Risk of incoherence if the typological mix is not carefully composed
- Below-grade parking spanning mixed typologies is structurally challenging
- Ownership and management boundaries between typologies must be clearly defined
- Higher design and coordination costs

**Precedent Projects:**
1. **Borneo Sporenburg, Amsterdam** (West 8, Adriaan Geuze, 2000s): the benchmark hybrid block; individual row houses, collective apartment buildings, and point towers all within a single urban plan; FAR 1.2-2.5; strict envelope rules with individual design freedom within
2. **Hammarby Sjostad, Stockholm** (2000s): blocks combine courtyard apartments, point towers, and townhouses; 4-8 stories mixed heights; FAR 1.5-2.5; integrated sustainable infrastructure
3. **HafenCity, Hamburg** (2000s-present): hybrid blocks with commercial podiums, mid-rise apartments, and point towers; 6-12 stories; FAR 2.5-4.0; multiple architects per block mandated
4. **Kings Cross, London** (Allies and Morrison et al., 2010s-present): hybrid blocks combining historic warehouse conversions, new-build apartments, townhouses, and commercial buildings; varied heights 3-20 stories; FAR 2.0-4.0
5. **Barangaroo, Sydney** (Rogers Stirk Harbour et al., 2010s-present): hybrid waterfront blocks combining towers, mid-rise, and low-rise elements; FAR 3.0-6.0; extensive public space framework

**Design Rules Checklist:**
- [ ] A clear block framework plan establishes the footprint, height, and use of each typological element
- [ ] Transitions between typologies are handled through stepped heights, setbacks, or linking elements (not abrupt jumps)
- [ ] Each typological element meets the design rules for its own type (e.g., townhouses meet row house standards, towers meet tower standards)
- [ ] Shared below-grade parking is structurally coordinated across all elements
- [ ] A consistent landscape and public realm strategy unifies the diverse building types
- [ ] Management and ownership boundaries are clearly defined in the block plan
- [ ] Multiple architects or distinct facade treatments are used to express the different typologies
- [ ] The mix of housing types includes a range of unit sizes and tenures for social diversity
