# Precedent Study Methodology Reference

This document details the full methodology for conducting rigorous urban design precedent studies: data collection, site visit protocols, documentation standards, interview frameworks, comparison matrices, and transferability assessment rubrics.

---

## 1. Data Collection Methods

### 1.1 Desktop Research Protocol

Before any site visit, compile all available published information:

**Primary Sources (highest reliability):**
- Official masterplan documents and design codes
- Planning application documents and environmental impact assessments
- Developer/client project reports and annual reviews
- Municipal planning authority reports and monitoring data
- Government statistical data (census, transport surveys, economic indicators)
- Post-occupancy evaluation reports
- Academic research papers (peer-reviewed journals)

**Secondary Sources (moderate reliability):**
- Architectural and urban design journal articles (Architectural Review, Domus, Topos, Urban Design Journal)
- Industry publications (Urban Land Institute case studies, RIBA Journal)
- Award submissions and jury citations (Aga Khan Award, Mies van der Rohe Award, ULI Global Awards)
- Conference papers and presentations
- Design studio reports from universities
- Guidebooks (Density Atlas, Spacematrix, Responsive Environments)

**Tertiary Sources (use with caution):**
- News articles and media coverage
- Wikipedia and online encyclopedias (use for leads only; verify all facts)
- Project websites and marketing materials (inherently biased toward positive narrative)
- Social media posts and reviews (useful for user experience insights, not metrics)
- Blog posts and informal writing

**Data Verification Rule:** Every quantitative metric cited in a precedent study should be sourced from at least one primary or secondary source. If a metric can only be found in tertiary sources, note this explicitly and flag uncertainty.

### 1.2 Quantitative Data Collection Checklist

Collect the following metrics for every precedent study:

**Site and Density:**
- [ ] Site area (hectares) - gross and net developable
- [ ] Total GFA (m2) - by use category
- [ ] FAR (Floor Area Ratio) - gross and net
- [ ] GSI (Ground Space Index / Plot Coverage Ratio)
- [ ] OSR (Open Space Ratio)
- [ ] Dwelling count and dwelling density (DU/ha, gross and net)
- [ ] Population (residents) and population density
- [ ] Worker population and jobs density
- [ ] Building heights (range, average, maximum)

**Land Use Mix:**
- [ ] Residential GFA and percentage of total
- [ ] Commercial/office GFA and percentage
- [ ] Retail GFA and percentage
- [ ] Institutional/community GFA and percentage
- [ ] Industrial/productive GFA and percentage
- [ ] Parking GFA and percentage (if structured)

**Housing:**
- [ ] Unit type mix (studio, 1-bed, 2-bed, 3-bed, 4-bed+, as percentages)
- [ ] Tenure mix (market sale, market rent, affordable rent, social rent, shared ownership, co-op)
- [ ] Affordability percentage (and definition used: percentage of median income, absolute rent level, etc.)
- [ ] Average unit size (m2, by type)
- [ ] Building types (apartment block, townhouse, maisonette, live-work, co-housing, etc.)

**Public Space and Green Infrastructure:**
- [ ] Total public open space (hectares and m2 per person)
- [ ] Green space (hectares and m2 per person)
- [ ] Public space hierarchy (pocket parks, neighborhood parks, district parks, regional parks - count and area of each)
- [ ] Tree canopy coverage (percentage, at maturity)
- [ ] Biodiversity metrics (species count, biodiversity net gain percentage, if available)
- [ ] Water features (type, area)

**Mobility:**
- [ ] Modal split (walk / cycle / transit / car - as percentages)
- [ ] Car ownership rate (cars per household or per 1,000 residents)
- [ ] Parking ratio (spaces per dwelling)
- [ ] Transit type, distance, and frequency
- [ ] Cycling infrastructure (km of separated lanes, bike parking spaces)
- [ ] Street hierarchy (primary, secondary, local, pedestrian-only - km of each)
- [ ] Block perimeter (average, range)
- [ ] Intersection density (per km2)

**Environmental Performance:**
- [ ] Energy demand (kWh/m2/year, by building type)
- [ ] Carbon emissions (kg CO2/m2/year or tonnes CO2/person/year)
- [ ] Renewable energy (kWp installed, percentage of demand met)
- [ ] Water consumption (liters per person per day)
- [ ] Stormwater management approach and permeable surface percentage
- [ ] Waste diversion rate (percentage recycled/composted vs. landfill)
- [ ] Green Space Factor or Biotope Area Factor (if applied)
- [ ] Certification achieved (LEED-ND, BREEAM, etc.) and level

**Economic:**
- [ ] Total development cost (local currency and USD equivalent)
- [ ] Cost per m2 of GFA
- [ ] Public investment (infrastructure, transit, public space)
- [ ] Private investment catalyzed
- [ ] Property values (per m2, comparison with city average)
- [ ] Commercial vacancy rate
- [ ] Tax revenue generated (if published)

### 1.3 Spatial Data Collection

**Map-Based Analysis:**
- [ ] Figure-ground plan (built vs. open)
- [ ] Land use plan (color-coded by use category)
- [ ] Street network and hierarchy
- [ ] Public space and green space network
- [ ] Transit network and stop locations with 400m/800m walkshed
- [ ] Building heights plan
- [ ] Block structure and perimeter analysis
- [ ] Active frontage mapping

**Cross-Section Analysis:**
- [ ] At least 3 representative street cross-sections (primary, secondary, local)
- [ ] At least 2 public space cross-sections (showing building height, space width, H:W ratio)
- [ ] Typical block cross-section (showing building depth, courtyard, setbacks)

## 2. Site Visit Protocol

When a site visit is possible (for the user or their research team), follow this structured protocol:

### 2.1 Pre-Visit Preparation
- Review all desktop research and identify knowledge gaps
- Prepare a site visit checklist targeting specific questions
- Identify key locations to visit (main public spaces, typical streets, edges, transit nodes, problem areas)
- Plan timing: visit at least twice - once on a weekday daytime and once on a weekend or evening - to observe different activity patterns
- Arrange any meetings or interviews (see Section 4)
- Prepare equipment: camera, notebook, measuring tape (or laser measure), sound level meter (smartphone app is acceptable), lux meter (smartphone app acceptable)

### 2.2 Structured Walk-Through

Follow a systematic route covering:

**Arrival Experience (15 minutes):**
- [ ] Approach from main transit stop: first impression, wayfinding clarity, transition from city to project
- [ ] Approach by foot from adjacent neighborhood: edge condition, permeability, welcoming or hostile?
- [ ] Arrival by car (if relevant): parking location, walk from parking to destination, transition

**Street Network (30 minutes):**
- [ ] Walk the primary streets: measure widths, count pedestrians, note active frontage, assess comfort
- [ ] Walk secondary and local streets: character change, quietness, residential quality
- [ ] Find and test mid-block connections: do they exist? Are they used?
- [ ] Identify dead-ends: how many? Impact on permeability?
- [ ] Assess street trees: spacing, species, health, canopy coverage

**Public Spaces (30 minutes per major space):**
- [ ] Sit in the space for at least 10 minutes and observe: who is using it? How? How many?
- [ ] Assess Gehl's 12 criteria for each major space (use the rubric in the design-evaluation skill)
- [ ] Measure the space dimensions and H:W ratios (or estimate from building heights)
- [ ] Note seating type, quantity, orientation (sun/shade)
- [ ] Note microclimate: wind, sun, shade, temperature relative to surrounding streets
- [ ] Note maintenance quality: cleanliness, repair state, graffiti, vandalism

**Building Edges (20 minutes):**
- [ ] Walk a 200m stretch of primary frontage and count: doors, windows, active uses, blank walls
- [ ] Calculate doors per 100m and active frontage percentage
- [ ] Note ground floor uses: retail types, lobby entries, residential front doors, service access, blank walls
- [ ] Note building articulation at ground level: canopies, setbacks, display windows, signage
- [ ] Take a representative facade photo every 50m

**Residential Quality (20 minutes):**
- [ ] Walk through residential areas: private space quality, boundary treatment, defensible space
- [ ] Note children playing: are they present? Where? Supervised or independent?
- [ ] Note personalization: window boxes, front garden variety, signage, cultural expression
- [ ] Note noise environment: traffic noise, neighbor noise, industrial noise

**Edges and Transitions (15 minutes):**
- [ ] Walk the boundary of the project: how does it meet the existing city?
- [ ] Note barriers, changes in character, permeability issues
- [ ] Assess whether the project feels integrated or isolated

### 2.3 Quantitative Site Measurements

Take the following measurements on-site (or estimate where measurement is impractical):

- [ ] Street widths (at least 5 representative locations: footpath, carriageway, total)
- [ ] Building heights (count floors; estimate total height)
- [ ] H:W ratios at representative cross-sections
- [ ] Pedestrian counts (5-minute counts at 3 locations: primary street, secondary street, public space)
- [ ] Ambient noise levels (dB, using smartphone app, at 3 locations)
- [ ] Light levels at night (lux, using smartphone app, at 3 locations) - if evening visit
- [ ] Ground floor door count per 100m (at 2 locations: primary and secondary street)
- [ ] Seating count in main public space (formal and informal)
- [ ] Tree count and spacing on a representative 200m street segment
- [ ] Cycling count (5-minute count at a key cycling route intersection)

## 3. Photography Documentation Protocol

Photographs should be systematic, not just attractive. Capture:

### Required Photo Categories

**Overview Photos (5-10 shots):**
- Aerial or elevated view of overall project (if possible; otherwise use satellite imagery from desktop)
- Key vistas along primary axes
- Project in its city context (from surrounding high point or bridge)

**Street Photos (10-15 shots):**
- Each primary street: long view and close-up
- Each secondary/local street type: representative view
- Street cross-section view (stand in the center and shoot perpendicular to the street to show full width)
- Ground floor close-ups showing door frequency, active uses, transparency
- Street trees and furniture close-ups

**Public Space Photos (5-10 per space):**
- Overall view of each major public space
- Close-up of seating, materials, edges, planting
- People using the space (document diversity of users and activities)
- Microclimate documentation (shade patterns, sunny areas, sheltered spots)

**Building Typology Photos (5-10 shots):**
- Representative facade of each building type
- Ground floor detail (entry, display window, transition from public to private)
- Roof treatment (green roof, solar panels, terraces - if visible)
- Scale reference (include a person in the photo for scale)

**Edge and Transition Photos (3-5 shots):**
- Where project meets existing city
- Boundary conditions (walls, fences, open transitions)
- Arrival sequence from key approach directions

**Problem Documentation (3-5 shots):**
- Maintenance issues
- Design failures (unused spaces, desire lines showing missed paths, barriers, hostile architecture)
- Conflict points (pedestrian-vehicle, cyclist-pedestrian)

### Photo Documentation Standards

- Shoot in landscape orientation for street views, portrait for buildings
- Include a person for scale reference where possible
- Note the location, direction, date, and time for each photo
- Shoot in consistent light conditions (overcast is best for even exposure)
- Avoid shooting into the sun
- Take bracketed exposures for high-contrast scenes
- Include context: do not just zoom into details; show the element in its setting

## 4. Interview and Stakeholder Insight Framework

If access to local professionals or residents is available, conduct semi-structured interviews:

### 4.1 Interview with Project Architect/Urban Designer (30-45 minutes)

**Design Intent:**
- What were the 3 most important design principles for this project?
- How did the site conditions and context shape the design response?
- What were the biggest constraints, and how did you respond to them?
- How did you balance density with livability?
- What is your parking and mobility strategy, and how was it negotiated?

**Process:**
- How long was the design process from concept to construction start?
- What was the level of community participation? How did it influence the design?
- How did you work with the planning authority? What were the key planning negotiations?
- How was the design code/guidelines developed and enforced?

**Reflection:**
- What would you do differently if you started again today?
- What has surprised you about how the project is used?
- What is the single most successful aspect of the project?
- What is the single biggest disappointment or failure?

### 4.2 Interview with Developer/Client (20-30 minutes)

**Economics:**
- What was the development model (phased sales, build-to-rent, public investment, PPP)?
- How did the project perform financially relative to projections?
- What was the relationship between public realm investment and private value?
- How were affordable housing obligations negotiated and delivered?

**Management:**
- How is the ongoing management of public spaces and common areas funded?
- What are the ongoing maintenance costs (per m2 of public space, if available)?
- How is the community governance structure organized?

### 4.3 Interview with Residents/Users (10-15 minutes per person, 5-10 people)

**Daily Life:**
- How do you typically travel to work/school/shops? (walking, cycling, transit, car)
- Do you own a car? If not, do you miss having one?
- Where do you go for daily shopping? How far is it?
- Where do your children play? Do they play independently?

**Quality of Life:**
- What do you like most about living/working here?
- What would you most like to change?
- Do you feel safe here at night?
- Do you know your neighbors? Is there a sense of community?

**Comparison:**
- How does this compare to where you lived before?
- Would you recommend this place to a friend? Why or why not?

## 6. Transferability Assessment Rubric

Use this rubric to systematically assess how transferable each precedent's lessons are to the user's project.

### 6.1 Transferability Dimensions

Rate each dimension 1-5 (1 = very different, 5 = very similar):

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Climate compatibility** | | Same climate zone = 5; opposite climate = 1 |
| **Scale match** | | Same order of magnitude in area and population = 5 |
| **Typological match** | | Same project type = 5; unrelated type = 1 |
| **Economic context** | | Similar GDP/capita and development economics = 5 |
| **Governance model** | | Same planning system and development process = 5 |
| **Cultural context** | | Similar urban culture, housing expectations, mobility norms = 5 |
| **Regulatory framework** | | Similar planning and building regulations = 5 |
| **Market conditions** | | Similar real estate market dynamics = 5 |
| **Infrastructure context** | | Similar existing infrastructure (transit, utilities, roads) = 5 |
| **Temporal relevance** | | Built within last 10 years = 5; historic = 1-2 (context-dependent) |

**Composite Transferability Score:**
- 40-50: **High transferability** - Lessons can be applied with minimal adaptation
- 25-39: **Medium transferability** - Lessons are relevant but require significant contextual adaptation
- 10-24: **Low transferability** - Only the most universal design principles transfer; specific solutions do not apply

### 6.2 Transferability Narrative Template

For each precedent, write a brief transferability assessment:

```
### Transferability: [Project Name] → [User's Project]

**Overall Rating:** [High / Medium / Low] (Score: [X/50])

**What transfers directly:**
- [Principle 1 that can be applied as-is]
- [Principle 2]

**What transfers with adaptation:**
- [Principle 3 that needs modification] - Adaptation needed: [specific adaptation]
- [Principle 4] - Adaptation needed: [specific adaptation]

**What does NOT transfer:**
- [Feature that is context-specific and should not be copied]
- [Feature that depends on conditions absent in user's context]

**Key risk of uncritical transfer:**
[Identify the single biggest risk of copying this precedent without understanding its context]
```

## 7. Report Quality Standards

Every precedent study report should meet these quality standards:

1. **Accuracy:** All metrics sourced from primary or secondary sources; no unverified claims presented as fact
2. **Completeness:** All five dimensions addressed (Context, Program, Design Principles, Performance, Transferability)
3. **Honesty:** Failures and shortcomings documented alongside successes; no hagiography
4. **Quantification:** Qualitative descriptions supported by quantitative metrics wherever possible
5. **Visual Clarity:** If images are unavailable, spatial qualities described in sufficient detail for a designer to understand the project's character
6. **Actionability:** Lessons are specific and actionable, not vague platitudes ("create good public spaces" is useless; "allocate minimum 9m2 green space per person within 300m of all dwellings" is actionable)
7. **Critical Distance:** Precedent is analyzed, not celebrated; every project has flaws worth discussing
8. **Comparability:** Metrics are presented in consistent units to enable cross-project comparison
9. **Attribution:** Sources are cited so the user can pursue deeper research
10. **Contextual Awareness:** Lessons are always framed relative to the user's specific project context
