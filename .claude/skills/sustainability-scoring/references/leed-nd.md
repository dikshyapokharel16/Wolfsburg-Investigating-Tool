# LEED-ND v4.1 Complete Credit Reference

Every LEED for Neighborhood Development v4.1 credit with intent, requirements summary, point value, design implications, typical evidence needed, achievability rating, and synergies with other credits. This document provides the detailed technical reference needed for credit-by-credit assessment and design optimization.

---

## Smart Location and Linkage (SLL): 27 Points

### SLL Prerequisite: Smart Location

**Intent**: Encourage development in locations with existing infrastructure, transit, and services, reducing vehicle miles traveled and preserving greenfield land.

**Requirements Summary**: The project site must meet at least one of the following:
- Located on an infill site (75%+ of perimeter bordered by previously developed land)
- Located on a site served by existing water and wastewater infrastructure
- Located within a transit-served area (bus stop within 400m with 60+ weekday trips, OR rail/BRT station within 800m with 72+ weekday trips)
- Located on a previously developed site
- Located within 400m of at least 4 diverse uses (retail, service, civic, employment)

AND the site must NOT be:
- Prime farmland (as defined by USDA NRCS or equivalent)
- Within 30m (100 ft) of a water body (with exceptions for previously developed sites)
- Within a 100-year floodplain (unless previously developed and meeting FEMA requirements)
- Habitat for species listed as threatened or endangered (unless habitat conservation plan is approved)
- On public parkland (unless replaced with equivalent land of equal or greater value)

**Evidence**: Site survey, FEMA flood maps, USDA soil survey, state/federal endangered species lists, transit schedules, aerial photographs showing surrounding development context.

---

### SLL Prerequisite: Imperiled Species and Ecological Communities

**Intent**: Protect imperiled species and ecological communities from development impacts.

**Requirements**: Conduct a biological assessment of the site. If imperiled species or ecological communities are present, prepare and implement a habitat conservation plan approved by relevant wildlife agencies. The plan must demonstrate no net loss of habitat value.

**Evidence**: Biological assessment report, habitat conservation plan (if applicable), agency correspondence.

---

### SLL Prerequisite: Wetland and Water Body Conservation

**Intent**: Protect water quality, natural hydrology, habitat, and biodiversity by conserving wetlands and water bodies.

**Requirements**: No development within wetlands or water bodies. Maintain a 15m (50 ft) buffer from water bodies and wetlands. Maintain a 30m (100 ft) buffer where slopes exceed 15% within the buffer area. Buffers must be vegetated with native species and maintained in perpetuity.

**Evidence**: Wetland delineation, site plan showing buffers, landscape plan for buffer areas, conservation easement or deed restriction.

---

### SLL Prerequisite: Agricultural Land Conservation

**Intent**: Preserve prime and unique farmland for food production and avoid conversion to development.

**Requirements**: Do not develop on land classified as prime farmland, unique farmland, or farmland of statewide importance (USDA NRCS classification or equivalent). If such land is present on the site, preserve it through conservation easement or transfer to a land trust. Exception: previously developed land that is classified as farmland due to historical agricultural use may be developed.

**Evidence**: USDA NRCS soil survey, farmland classification maps, conservation easement (if applicable).

---

### SLL Prerequisite: Floodplain Avoidance

**Intent**: Protect life, property, and natural floodplain functions by avoiding development in flood-prone areas.

**Requirements**: No development within the 100-year (1% annual chance) floodplain. If floodplain is present on the site, all development must be located outside the floodplain, or the floodplain must be mitigated per FEMA requirements (LOMR or CLOMR). Critical infrastructure (hospitals, emergency services, power plants) must be located outside the 500-year floodplain.

**Evidence**: FEMA Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM), floodplain study, LOMR/CLOMR documentation (if applicable).

### SLL Credit 1: Preferred Locations (10 Points)

**Intent**: Encourage development in preferred locations to reduce environmental impacts and promote efficient land use.

**Requirements and Points**:
- Previously developed site (brownfield, greyfield, or redevelopment): 1 point
- Infill site (75%+ of perimeter bordered by developed parcels or public ROW): 2 points
- Proximity to transit (tiered by transit frequency and distance):
  - Bus with 60+ weekday trips within 400m: 1 point
  - Bus with 100+ weekday trips within 400m: 2 points
  - Bus with 144+ weekday trips within 400m: 3 points
  - Rail/BRT with 72+ weekday trips within 800m: 2 points
  - Rail/BRT with 144+ weekday trips within 800m: 4 points
  - Multiple transit types combining above: up to 5 points
- Proximity to existing bicycle network within 400m of project boundary: 1 point

Maximum 10 points total for this credit.

**Design Implications**: This credit is primarily determined by site selection. During site evaluation, prioritize locations that are infill, previously developed, and transit-served. These locations also tend to reduce infrastructure costs and accelerate absorption rates.

**Achievability**: Moderate. Depends entirely on site location -- cannot be designed into the project after site selection.

**Synergies**: SLL-4 (Access to Quality Transit), NPD-1 (Walkable Streets), NPD-2 (Compact Development), NPD-7 (Transit Facilities).

### SLL Credit 2: Brownfield Remediation (2 Points)

**Intent**: Encourage the remediation and redevelopment of contaminated sites, reducing pressure on greenfield land.

**Requirements**: The site must be classified as a brownfield (contaminated by hazardous substances or petroleum). The project must include a remediation plan approved by the relevant environmental authority (EPA, state DEP, or equivalent). Remediation must be completed to residential or commercial standards appropriate for the proposed use.

**Design Implications**: Remediation costs can be significant but may be offset by tax incentives, grants, and lower land acquisition costs. Remediation approach affects building foundation design, landscape design (capped vs. cleaned sites), and stormwater management.

**Evidence**: Phase I and Phase II Environmental Site Assessments, remediation plan, regulatory approval, completion report.

**Achievability**: Easy if the site is a brownfield. N/A for greenfield sites.

**Synergies**: SLL-1 (Preferred Locations -- previously developed site), GIB-5 (Building Reuse).

---

### SLL Credit 3: Housing and Jobs Proximity (3 Points)

**Intent**: Encourage balanced communities with jobs near housing, reducing commute distances and vehicle miles traveled.

**Requirements** (tiered):
- Option 1 (Residential project): demonstrate that 50%+ of dwelling units are within 800m walking distance of a number of full-time-equivalent jobs:
  - 50% of DU within 800m of existing or planned jobs (ratio jobs/DU): 1-3 pts based on ratio
- Option 2 (Mixed-use or employment project): demonstrate that the project includes a jobs-housing ratio within 800m that supports balance

**Design Implications**: For residential projects, site selection near employment centers is critical. For mixed-use projects, programming sufficient commercial and office space to create a jobs-housing balance earns this credit.

**Achievability**: Moderate. Context-dependent -- easier in urban infill locations with existing employment base.

**Synergies**: NPD-3 (Mixed-Use Neighborhoods), SLL-1 (Preferred Locations).

---

### SLL Credit 4: Access to Quality Transit (5 Points)

**Intent**: Promote development in locations served by quality transit, reducing automobile dependence.

**Requirements** (tiered by transit service level):
- Weekend transit service (40+ trips/day Saturday and Sunday) within 400m of 50%+ of DU: 1 point
- Weekday transit service: 72+ trips/day within 400m: 1 point; 144+ trips/day: 2 points; 360+ trips/day: 3 points
- Multiple transit modes (bus + rail): up to 5 points total
- Walking distance measured along pedestrian network (not straight-line distance)
- Future transit counts only if funded, designed, and under construction or contractually committed

**Design Implications**: Cannot be designed into the project unless the project includes or commits to transit infrastructure. Site selection is the primary lever. For projects that include a transit investment (BRT station, streetcar stop), coordinate transit planning with the transit agency early in the design process.

**Evidence**: Transit schedules, transit agency correspondence, pedestrian network mapping, walking distance calculations.

**Achievability**: Moderate to Difficult. Requires existing or firmly committed transit service at high frequency.

**Synergies**: SLL-1 (Preferred Locations), NPD-7 (Transit Facilities), NPD-8 (Transportation Demand Management).

---

### SLL Credit 5: Bicycle Facilities (2 Points)

**Intent**: Promote cycling as a practical transportation mode by ensuring connectivity to a bicycle network and providing bicycle parking.

**Requirements**:
- Bicycle network: the project is connected to an existing or planned bicycle network that provides access to 10+ diverse uses within 4.8 km (3 mi) bicycling distance: 1 point
- Bicycle storage: secure, covered bicycle parking for 30%+ of planned occupants (residential) and 10%+ of peak users (commercial); short-term visitor bicycle parking (1 per 10 DU, 1 per 5,000 sqf commercial): 1 point

**Design Implications**: Include protected bike lanes or multi-use paths connecting to the municipal bicycle network. Provide secure bicycle storage in or adjacent to residential buildings (ground-floor bike rooms, covered racks). Include bicycle corrals on commercial streets.

**Evidence**: Bicycle network map showing connectivity to uses, bicycle parking plan with count and type, photographs or drawings of bicycle storage facilities.

**Achievability**: Easy if the municipal bicycle network exists or is planned within the project vicinity.

**Synergies**: NPD-1 (Walkable Streets), NPD-8 (Transportation Demand Management), GIB-13 (Infrastructure Energy Efficiency -- reduced need for parking infrastructure).

---

### SLL Credit 6: Steep Slope Protection (1 Point)

**Intent**: Minimize erosion, protect natural drainage patterns, and preserve hillside visual character by limiting development on steep slopes.

**Requirements**: Avoid development on slopes exceeding 15%. No grading, clearing, or disturbance on slopes exceeding 25% (unless for restoration). Where development occurs on slopes between 10-15%, implement erosion and sediment control measures exceeding minimum requirements.

**Design Implications**: Slope analysis during site planning. Cluster development on flatter areas. Use slopes for green space, stormwater management, and trail corridors.

**Achievability**: Easy for flat or gently sloping sites. May be N/A for sites without significant slopes.

**Synergies**: SLL-7 (Habitat Conservation), GIB-7 (Minimized Site Disturbance).

---

### SLL Credit 7: Site Design for Habitat or Wetland Conservation (1 Point)

**Intent**: Conserve existing habitat and wetlands to protect biodiversity and ecosystem services.

**Requirements**: Conserve 100% of significant habitat (as identified in the biological assessment) within the project boundary. Maintain 30m (100 ft) buffers from all water bodies, wetlands, and significant habitat areas. Habitat areas must be protected by conservation easement or deed restriction in perpetuity.

**Evidence**: Biological assessment, conservation easement, site plan showing conserved areas and buffers.

**Achievability**: Easy if habitat is present and the design preserves it. N/A if no significant habitat on site.

**Synergies**: SLL-6 (Steep Slope Protection), SLL-8 (Restoration of Habitat), SLL-9 (Long-Term Conservation Management), GIB-7 (Minimized Site Disturbance).

---

### SLL Credit 8: Restoration of Habitat or Wetlands (1 Point)

**Intent**: Restore degraded natural habitat to improve biodiversity, ecosystem function, and environmental quality.

**Requirements**: Restore native habitat on at least 10% of the total site area (including previously developed areas) using native plant species appropriate to the local ecoregion. Restored areas must include a 5-year maintenance and monitoring plan. Restored areas may include: riparian corridors, wetland reconstruction, meadow restoration, forest replanting, green corridors connecting habitat patches.

**Design Implications**: Integrate habitat restoration into the open space system. Use restored areas for stormwater management (dual function). Select native species with wildlife value (food, shelter, nesting).

**Achievability**: Moderate. Requires ecological expertise and long-term management commitment.

**Synergies**: SLL-7 (Habitat Conservation), SLL-9 (Long-Term Conservation Management), GIB-7 (Minimized Site Disturbance), GIB-8 (Rainwater Management -- natural areas contribute to stormwater management).

---

### SLL Credit 9: Long-Term Conservation Management (2 Points)

**Intent**: Ensure the long-term protection and management of conserved and restored habitat areas.

**Requirements**:
- 10-year conservation management plan prepared by a qualified ecologist: 1 point
- Dedicated funding mechanism for ongoing management (endowment, HOA assessment, conservation easement with funded stewardship): 1 point

The plan must include: species monitoring, invasive species management, fire management (where applicable), access management (limiting human disturbance), and adaptive management provisions.

**Evidence**: Conservation management plan, funding mechanism documentation, ecologist qualifications.

**Achievability**: Moderate. Requires long-term financial commitment and professional ecological management.

**Synergies**: SLL-7, SLL-8 (Habitat Conservation and Restoration).

## Neighborhood Pattern and Design (NPD): 44 Points

### NPD Credit 1: Walkable Streets (9 Points, up to 12 with Exemplary Performance)

**Intent**: Promote transportation efficiency and reduce vehicle distance traveled by creating safe, comfortable, and interesting pedestrian environments.

**Requirements** (cumulative -- each item below earns specified points, up to 9 total):

Building Facade and Entrance Standards (up to 3 points):
- 80%+ of mixed-use and retail building facades within 7.5m (25 ft) of the front property line or sidewalk: 1 point
- 50%+ of mixed-use and retail building facades with functional entries directly accessible from the sidewalk at average intervals of 23m (75 ft) or less: 1 point
- 50%+ of ground-floor commercial frontage with clear, non-reflective glass (minimum 60% transparency between 0.9m and 2.4m above grade): 1 point

Sidewalk and Streetscape Standards (up to 3 points):
- Continuous sidewalks on both sides of 90%+ of new and reconstructed streets: 1 point (prerequisite achievement may count)
- Street trees on both sides of 60%+ of new and reconstructed streets, planted at maximum 12m (40 ft) on-center: 1 point
- Ground-floor retail or other pedestrian-activating uses (galleries, lobbies, display windows) along 60%+ of designated retail street frontage: 1 point

Street Design Standards (up to 3 points):
- Design speed of 40 km/h (25 mph) or less on 70%+ of new streets: 1 point
- No more than one vehicle entrance (driveway) per block face on 90%+ of retail and mixed-use street frontages: 1 point
- Continuous tree canopy coverage providing shade on 40%+ of sidewalk length (at 10-year canopy maturity): 1 point

**Exemplary Performance**: Achieve all 9 base points plus 3 additional points for exceptional performance: total of 12 points (3 exemplary points count toward Innovation credits).

**Design Implications**: This is one of the highest-value credits in LEED-ND. Achieving full points requires intentional urban design: buildings at the sidewalk edge, frequent entries, ground-floor transparency, continuous sidewalks with street trees, and traffic calming. These are all hallmarks of good urbanism.

**Achievability**: Moderate. Requires consistent application across the entire project. Easy for urban infill with form-based code; more challenging for suburban contexts.

**Synergies**: NPD-2 (Compact Development), NPD-3 (Mixed-Use), NPD-14 (Tree-Lined Streets), GIB-9 (Heat Island Reduction -- street trees contribute to both).

---

### NPD Credit 2: Compact Development (6 Points)

**Intent**: Conserve land, promote walkability, support transit viability, and reduce vehicle miles traveled through compact development patterns.

**Requirements** (tiered by density -- residential DU/ha and/or non-residential FAR):

| Density (DU/ha) | Non-Residential FAR | Points |
|-----------------|---------------------|--------|
| 17-31 (7-12.5 DU/acre) | 0.50-0.80 | 1 |
| 31-54 (12.5-22 DU/acre) | 0.80-1.25 | 2 |
| 54-100 (22-40 DU/acre) | 1.25-1.75 | 3 |
| 100-155 (40-63 DU/acre) | 1.75-2.25 | 4 |
| 155-230 (63-93 DU/acre) | 2.25-3.00 | 5 |
| 230+ (93+ DU/acre) | 3.00+ | 6 |

For mixed-use projects, calculate residential density and non-residential FAR separately; the lower of the two point values determines the credit achievement.

**Design Implications**: Density is the single most impactful design parameter for sustainability. Higher density reduces per-capita land consumption, supports transit, enables walkable access to services, and reduces infrastructure costs per unit. Achieving 4-6 points requires mid-rise to high-rise typologies.

**Evidence**: Site plan, unit count, lot area calculations, floor area calculations, density calculations.

**Achievability**: Easy to Moderate. Density is a fundamental design choice that also improves project economics. The primary constraint is market absorption and community acceptance.

**Synergies**: NPD-1 (Walkable Streets), NPD-3 (Mixed-Use), SLL-1 (Preferred Locations), SLL-4 (Transit Access).

---

### NPD Credit 3: Mixed-Use Neighborhoods (4 Points)

**Intent**: Reduce vehicle distance traveled by providing a variety of uses within walking distance of residences, creating complete neighborhoods where daily needs can be met on foot.

**Requirements** (tiered by number of diverse uses within 400m / 0.25 mi walking distance of the geographic center of the project, or within 400m of 50%+ of dwelling units):

| Diverse Uses within 400m | Points |
|--------------------------|--------|
| 4-6 uses | 1 |
| 7-11 uses | 2 |
| 12-18 uses | 3 |
| 19+ uses | 4 |

Qualifying diverse uses (each counts once): grocery/supermarket, restaurant/cafe, retail (clothing, books, electronics, home goods), convenience store/corner store, bank/credit union, pharmacy, hair salon/barber, laundry/dry cleaner, gym/fitness center, hardware store, medical/dental office, community/civic center (library, post office, community center), religious institution, daycare/preschool, entertainment (theater, gallery, museum), park or recreation facility, school (K-12), senior center, social services office.

**Design Implications**: Program the neighborhood with a diverse mix of uses concentrated in the walkable center (T5 zone). Ground-floor retail on main streets naturally supports this credit. Ensure the programming includes daily-needs uses (grocery, pharmacy, daycare) not just specialty retail.

**Evidence**: Land use plan, tenant directory or programming schedule, walking distance analysis from project center to each use.

**Achievability**: Easy to Moderate. Requires intentional programming but most good masterplans include diverse uses. Easier for larger projects (more land area to accommodate diverse tenants) and for infill sites near existing services.

**Synergies**: NPD-1 (Walkable Streets), NPD-2 (Compact Development), SLL-3 (Housing and Jobs Proximity), NPD-13 (Local Food Production -- farmers market counts as a use).

---

### NPD Credit 4: Housing Types and Affordability (7 Points)

**Intent**: Enable residents from a wide range of economic levels, household sizes, and age groups to live within the neighborhood by providing diverse housing types and affordable options.

**Requirements**:

Housing Type Diversity (1-3 points):
- Simpson Diversity Index (SDI) of housing types: SDI 0.5+ = 1 pt, SDI 0.6+ = 2 pts, SDI 0.7+ = 3 pts
- Housing types include: detached single-family, attached single-family (townhouse/rowhouse), duplex/triplex, small multifamily (4-19 units), large multifamily (20+ units), live-work, accessory dwelling unit, senior housing
- Minimum 3 distinct housing types for any points

Affordable Housing (1-3 points):
- 10% of total DU at 80% Area Median Income (AMI) or below: 1 point
- 15% of total DU at 80% AMI or below: 2 points
- 20% of total DU at 80% AMI or below: 3 points
- Affordable units must be distributed throughout the project (not concentrated in one area)
- Affordability must be maintained for minimum 15 years through deed restriction, covenant, or affordable housing agreement

Senior Housing (1 point):
- Include housing designed for seniors (age-restricted, universal design, assisted living) within the project

**Design Implications**: Design multiple building types to serve different household sizes and income levels. Integrate affordable units throughout the project rather than concentrating them. Universal design features (zero-step entries, wide doorways, reinforced bathroom walls for grab bars) benefit all residents.

**Achievability**: Moderate for diversity (3 pts), Moderate to Difficult for affordability (requires subsidy or cross-subsidy mechanisms), Easy for senior housing component.

**Synergies**: NPD-11 (Visitability and Universal Design), NPD-12 (Community Outreach).

---

### NPD Credit 5: Reduced Parking Footprint (1 Point)

**Intent**: Minimize the environmental and aesthetic impacts of surface parking by reducing parking supply and using structured or underground parking.

**Requirements**: Meet ALL of the following:
- Total parking spaces do not exceed the minimum required by local zoning (or, if no minimum, do not exceed national average for the project's use mix)
- No more than 20% of the total development footprint is dedicated to surface parking
- Any parking structure has at least 2 levels (to reduce surface area)
- No surface parking lots adjacent to or visible from the primary street frontage
- Provide carpool/vanpool preferred parking (5%+ of total parking spaces)

**Design Implications**: Eliminate surface parking from street-facing areas. Structure or underground parking. Reduce parking ratios below conventional suburban standards. Include carshare, bikeshare, and transit pass programs to reduce parking demand.

**Achievability**: Easy in urban contexts with structured parking. More challenging in suburban contexts where surface parking is market-expected.

**Synergies**: NPD-2 (Compact Development -- less parking = more density), NPD-8 (Transportation Demand Management), GIB-9 (Heat Island Reduction -- less impervious surface).

---

### NPD Credit 6: Connected and Open Community (2 Points)

**Intent**: Promote walking, cycling, and transit use by providing a connected, permeable street network with no gated or restricted access.

**Requirements** (beyond the prerequisite, which requires 54 intersections/sq km):
- Connectivity index of 77+ intersections per sq km (200/sq mi): 1 point
- Connectivity index of 100+ intersections per sq km (260/sq mi): 2 points
- No gated streets or restricted-access enclaves
- Through-connections to adjacent development at minimum every 240m (800 ft) along each project boundary

**Design Implications**: Use a fine-grained grid or modified grid street network. Short blocks (100-150m face length). Mid-block pedestrian passages where blocks exceed 150m. Multiple connections to surrounding street network. No cul-de-sacs (except where topography absolutely requires).

**Achievability**: Moderate. Requires intentional street network design. Easier with a grid; harder with irregular topography or large land parcels with limited edge connections.

**Synergies**: NPD-1 (Walkable Streets), SLL-4 (Transit Access -- connected network enables walking to transit), NPD-2 (Compact Development).

## Innovation and Design Process (ID): 6 Points

| Credit | Points | Requirements |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| ID-1 Innovation | 5 (max) | 1 point per innovation. Options: (a) Exemplary performance in existing credits (exceeding top threshold by significant margin), (b) Pilot credits from USGBC pilot credit library, (c) Novel sustainability strategies not addressed by LEED-ND credits. Each innovation must be documented with narrative, metrics, and evidence. |
| ID-2 LEED Accredited Professional | 1 | At least one principal participant on the project team is a LEED AP with Neighborhood Development (ND) specialty. |

**Common Innovation Credit Strategies**:
- Exemplary performance in NPD-1 Walkable Streets (12 points vs 9 base)
- Exemplary performance in NPD-2 Compact Development (exceeding 230 DU/ha)
- Net-zero energy community (all energy from on-site renewables)
- Living Building Challenge pilot credit
- Bird-safe building design
- Biophilic design framework at community scale
- Resilience planning (climate adaptation, emergency preparedness)
- Social equity audit and action plan
- Circular economy infrastructure (material reuse facilities, repair cafes)

## LEED-ND Certification Process Timeline

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Pre-application (optional) | 1-2 months | Preliminary review of site eligibility; USGBC provides conditional approval of prerequisites |
| Stage 1: Conditional Approval of Plan | 3-6 months | Submit site plan, regulating plan, and evidence for SLL and NPD prerequisites and credits; USGBC reviews and issues conditional approval |
| Stage 2: Pre-Certified Plan | 6-12 months | Submit updated plans reflecting any conditions from Stage 1; receive "Pre-Certified" designation (useful for marketing and financing) |
| Stage 3: Certified Neighborhood Development | 12-36 months (after construction) | Submit evidence of built conditions including GIB credits, as-built documentation, and verification of commitments made in Stages 1-2; USGBC reviews and issues final certification |

**Key Timing Considerations**:
- Stage 1 can be submitted early in design (after masterplan completion)
- Stage 2 provides market value during sales/leasing (pre-certified designation)
- Stage 3 requires as-built verification (after construction is substantially complete)
- LEED-ND allows phased certification (phases of a masterplan can be certified separately)
