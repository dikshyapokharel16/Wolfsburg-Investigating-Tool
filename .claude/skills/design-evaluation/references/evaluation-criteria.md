# Comprehensive Evaluation Criteria Reference

This document contains the full evaluation criteria from all major urban design quality frameworks, adapted for structured assessment. Each framework includes its complete criteria set, a scoring rubric (1-5), and guidance on observable evidence.

---

## 1. Jan Gehl - 12 Quality Criteria for Public Space

Source: Jan Gehl, "Cities for People" (2010); Gehl Architects Public Space Public Life methodology.

Gehl's 12 criteria are organized into three categories of needs: Protection, Comfort, and Enjoyment. They apply primarily to streets, squares, parks, and other public spaces.

### PROTECTION (Criteria 1-3)

**Criterion 1: Protection Against Traffic and Accidents**
Assesses whether pedestrians feel safe from vehicular traffic.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No separation between pedestrians and fast-moving traffic; high accident risk; pedestrians feel endangered |
| 2 | Minimal separation; narrow footpaths alongside busy roads; infrequent crossings; uncomfortable proximity to traffic |
| 3 | Basic separation present; footpaths of adequate width; signalized crossings at main intersections; moderate traffic speeds |
| 4 | Good separation; wide footpaths; frequent safe crossings; traffic calmed to 30 km/h; clear pedestrian priority at key points |
| 5 | Complete pedestrian priority or car-free areas; shared space design where appropriate; Vision Zero design; children can move independently |

**Criterion 2: Protection Against Crime and Violence (Perceived Safety)**
Assesses whether people feel safe using the space at all hours.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Deserted space; no natural surveillance; dark and hidden areas; known safety concerns |
| 2 | Limited surveillance; inconsistent lighting; some blind spots; low activity levels |
| 3 | Moderate surveillance from buildings; adequate lighting; some activity into evening; reasonable sense of safety |
| 4 | Good natural surveillance; consistent bright lighting; active edges; people present across most hours; clear sightlines |
| 5 | Excellent natural surveillance; 24-hour activity; bright, warm lighting; overlapping eyes on street; diverse users at all hours; maintained and cared-for |

**Criterion 3: Protection Against Unpleasant Sensory Experiences**
Assesses protection from wind, rain, sun, noise, and pollution.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Fully exposed to weather; wind tunnel effects; no shade or shelter; high noise/pollution levels |
| 2 | Minimal protection; some shelter at building entries only; significant wind issues; noticeable pollution |
| 3 | Moderate protection; some covered areas; partial wind mitigation; acceptable noise levels |
| 4 | Good weather protection; sheltered walking routes; effective wind mitigation; shade in summer; sun in winter; low pollution |
| 5 | Comprehensive microclimate design; sheltered routes between key destinations; wind-comfortable; summer shade and winter sun; low noise and clean air |

### COMFORT (Criteria 4-9)

**Criterion 4: Opportunities to Walk**
Assesses the quality and attractiveness of the walking environment.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Walking impossible or extremely unpleasant; no footpaths; barriers to movement |
| 2 | Walking possible but uncomfortable; narrow, obstructed, or poorly surfaced paths; indirect routes |
| 3 | Adequate walking conditions; reasonable paths and surfaces; functional but not inviting |
| 4 | Good walking environment; wide smooth paths; direct routes; interesting walk; accessible gradients |
| 5 | Excellent walking experience; generous paths; varied and interesting routes; high-quality surfaces; full accessibility; walking as pleasure |

**Criterion 5: Opportunities to Stand and Linger**
Assesses whether the space supports staying activities and informal gathering.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No reason or opportunity to stop; hostile environment; nothing to lean against or pause at |
| 2 | Few opportunities; limited edge effects; no leaning surfaces; nowhere comfortable to pause |
| 3 | Some opportunities at building edges and nodes; a few leaning or pause points |
| 4 | Good opportunities; attractive edge zones; leaning points; things to watch; comfortable standing areas |
| 5 | Abundant staying opportunities; rich edge effects; bollards and ledges for leaning; facade niches; window displays; busker spots; natural gathering nodes |

**Criterion 6: Opportunities to Sit**
Assesses the provision and quality of seating.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No seating at all; no secondary seating (ledges, steps) |
| 2 | Minimal seating; poorly located; no choice of sun/shade; uncomfortable design |
| 3 | Adequate seating at regular intervals; basic comfort; some choice of location |
| 4 | Good seating variety; benches, chairs, ledges; choice of sun and shade; good views from seats; backs and armrests |
| 5 | Excellent seating; abundant variety (formal and informal); choice of sun/shade/shelter; sociable and private options; movable chairs; well-maintained |

**Criterion 7: Opportunities to See**
Assesses sight lines, views, and visual interest.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No views; enclosed by blank walls; nothing to observe |
| 2 | Limited views; restricted sightlines; minimal visual interest |
| 3 | Adequate views; reasonable sightlines; some visual interest from activity or landscape |
| 4 | Good views; clear sightlines; interesting activity to observe; view to landmarks or nature |
| 5 | Excellent views at multiple scales; panoramic views and intimate details; rich people-watching; visual connections to landmarks, nature, and sky |

**Criterion 8: Opportunities to Talk and Listen**
Assesses the acoustic environment and social interaction potential.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Impossible to converse; traffic noise dominant; acoustic hostility |
| 2 | Difficult to converse; must raise voice; noise interference |
| 3 | Conversation possible at close range; moderate background noise; some quiet spots |
| 4 | Easy conversation; low background noise; seating arranged for social interaction; acoustic comfort |
| 5 | Excellent acoustic environment; natural conversation easy; seating arrangements promote interaction; quiet enough to hear birds and water; intimate spaces |

**Criterion 9: Opportunities for Play and Exercise**
Assesses provision for physical activity, play, and recreation.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No opportunities for physical activity; no play provision; sedentary environment |
| 2 | Minimal provision; a single play area or fitness station; limited to one age group |
| 3 | Adequate provision; playground plus some sports/exercise facilities; serves several age groups |
| 4 | Good provision; multiple activity types; all-age play and exercise; informal and formal opportunities |
| 5 | Excellent provision; diverse activities for all ages and abilities; integrated playful design; informal sport spaces; nature play; seasonal variation |

### ENJOYMENT (Criteria 10-12)

**Criterion 10: Scale**
Assesses whether the space relates to human proportions and senses.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Vastly out of scale; overwhelming or alienating proportions; designed for vehicles not people |
| 2 | Uncomfortable scale; too large or too compressed; poor relationship between building heights and space width |
| 3 | Acceptable scale; spaces are functional but not particularly comfortable |
| 4 | Good human scale; comfortable proportions; spaces feel defined but not cramped; 5-story datum; fine grain detail |
| 5 | Exquisite human scale; intimate spaces within larger settings; rich detail at eye level; spaces feel like outdoor rooms; masterly proportional design |

**Criterion 11: Opportunities to Enjoy the Positive Aspects of Climate**
Assesses whether the design exploits pleasant weather conditions.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No consideration of climate amenity; spaces unusable in good weather |
| 2 | Minimal; few south-facing spaces (Northern Hemisphere); no wind-sheltered sun spots |
| 3 | Some climate enjoyment; south-facing seating available; partial shelter |
| 4 | Good climate design; sun-catching spaces; sheltered from prevailing winds; shade available in hot weather |
| 5 | Excellent; sun-traps; wind-sheltered terraces; water play in summer; thermal delight; connection to natural elements |

**Criterion 12: Positive Sensory Experience (Aesthetic Quality and Design)**
Assesses the overall aesthetic quality and sensory richness.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Ugly, neglected, hostile; poor quality materials and design; no positive sensory experience |
| 2 | Mediocre; unremarkable design; basic materials; limited positive sensory input |
| 3 | Acceptable design quality; reasonable materials; some planting; neutral sensory experience |
| 4 | Good design quality; quality materials and planting; water features; art; positive sensory richness |
| 5 | Outstanding design; beautiful materials and detailing; rich planting with seasonal interest; water; art; varied textures, colors, and scents; delightful |

## 2. Ian Bentley - 7 Qualities of Responsive Environments

Source: Ian Bentley, Alan Alcock, Paul Murrain, Sue McGlynn, Graham Smith, "Responsive Environments: A Manual for Designers" (1985).

**Quality 1: Permeability**
The degree to which an environment allows people to choose between alternative routes.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Map analysis - route choice count | Single route; walled compound; no through-movement |
| 2 | | 2-3 route options; significant barriers and dead-ends |
| 3 | | Moderate route choice; some permeability; functional network |
| 4 | | Good route choice; few barriers; connected network |
| 5 | | Maximum route choice; fine grain; no dead-ends; mid-block connections |

**Quality 2: Variety**
The range of uses and activities available.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Land use survey | Single use; monoculture; no activity variety |
| 2 | | 2-3 uses; limited variety; single-period activity |
| 3 | | 4-6 uses; moderate variety; basic daily needs met |
| 4 | | 7-10 uses; good variety; day and evening activity |
| 5 | | >10 uses; rich variety; full 18-hour activity; fine-grained mix |

**Quality 3: Legibility**
The degree to which the environment can be understood and mentally mapped.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Lynch-style mental mapping | Disorienting; no identifiable structure; impossible to navigate |
| 2 | | Weak structure; confusing; few reference points |
| 3 | | Basic structure readable; functional navigation with signage help |
| 4 | | Clear structure; identifiable paths, edges, nodes, landmarks; intuitive navigation |
| 5 | | Highly legible; vivid mental map; memorable landmarks; effortless wayfinding |

**Quality 4: Robustness**
The degree to which spaces can be used for different purposes over time.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Flexibility audit | Completely inflexible; single-purpose; cannot be adapted |
| 2 | | Limited flexibility; specialized spaces; adaptation would require major structural change |
| 3 | | Moderate robustness; some spaces can be repurposed; building envelopes adaptable |
| 4 | | Good robustness; multipurpose spaces; buildings designed for use change; adaptable infrastructure |
| 5 | | Maximum robustness; all spaces support multiple uses; generous floor-to-ceiling heights; convertible buildings; flexible public spaces |

**Quality 5: Visual Appropriateness**
The degree to which the appearance of the environment makes people aware of its qualities.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Design review | Completely out of context; misleading visual cues; buildings do not express their use or relate to setting |
| 2 | | Weak contextual relationship; confusing visual language; insensitive to neighbors |
| 3 | | Acceptable appearance; functional but unremarkable; basic contextual fit |
| 4 | | Thoughtful visual response; buildings express their use; sensitive to context while being contemporary |
| 5 | | Visually rich and appropriate; confident architectural language; enhances context; uses and activities legible from appearance |

**Quality 6: Richness**
The degree to which the environment provides a rich sensory experience.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Sensory audit | Sterile; monotonous; single material; no sensory stimulation |
| 2 | | Limited richness; 1-2 materials; minimal variety; bland experience |
| 3 | | Moderate richness; some material variety; basic planting; functional |
| 4 | | Rich experience; diverse materials; interesting planting; spatial variety; surprise |
| 5 | | Extraordinary richness; multiple sensory layers; texture, color, scent, sound; art; water; seasonal change; delight |

**Quality 7: Personalization**
The degree to which people can put their own mark on the environment.

| Score | Assessment Method | Evidence |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| 1 | Personalization audit | No personalization possible; rigid control; sterile uniformity |
| 2 | | Minimal scope; highly controlled; limited individual expression |
| 3 | | Some personalization; front gardens, window boxes, signage within guidelines |
| 4 | | Good personalization; facade expression; garden variety; community murals; market stalls |
| 5 | | Rich personalization; diverse individual expression; community-shaped spaces; cultural layering; living environment that evolves |

## 3. Project for Public Spaces (PPS) - 4 Placemaking Qualities

Source: Project for Public Spaces, "What Makes a Successful Place?" (The Place Diagram).

### Quality 1: Access and Linkages
How well-connected the place is to its surroundings; ease of getting to and through it.

| Score | Indicators to Assess |
|-------|---------------------|
| 1 | Isolated; no connections; inaccessible by public transport; no pedestrian links; hard to find |
| 2 | Poorly connected; 1-2 access points; limited transit; confusing entry sequence |
| 3 | Adequately connected; reasonable transit access; visible from a distance; basic wayfinding |
| 4 | Well-connected; multiple access points; good transit; visible and inviting; clear wayfinding; ADA compliant |
| 5 | Superbly connected; seamless integration with surroundings; excellent transit; universally accessible; intuitive arrival sequence; porous edges |

**Indicators:** Proximity to transit, pedestrian accessibility, visibility from a distance, ease of walking to, connections to adjacent uses, ADA compliance, wayfinding quality.

### Quality 2: Comfort and Image
Whether the place is comfortable, safe, clean, and attractive.

| Score | Indicators to Assess |
|-------|---------------------|
| 1 | Unsafe, dirty, uncomfortable; negative image; people avoid it |
| 2 | Basic safety but uncomfortable; poorly maintained; unremarkable image |
| 3 | Reasonably comfortable and maintained; neutral image; functional |
| 4 | Comfortable; well-maintained; positive image; seating and shade available; clean; people choose to linger |
| 5 | Highly comfortable; beautifully maintained; strong positive image; excellent seating; optimal microclimate; people love being there and recommend it |

**Indicators:** Safety perception, cleanliness, seating availability, attractiveness, walkability, parking convenience, transit convenience, historic quality.

### Quality 3: Uses and Activities
The range and quality of activities taking place.

| Score | Indicators to Assess |
|-------|---------------------|
| 1 | No activities; no reason to visit; empty and purposeless |
| 2 | 1-2 activities; limited to single user group; no programming |
| 3 | Several activities; moderate visitor numbers; some programming; seasonal events |
| 4 | Diverse activities; multiple user groups; regular programming; high footfall; events calendar |
| 5 | Rich and varied activities; all ages and backgrounds; active programming; celebration of local culture; place is the "heart" of community; high day and evening use |

**Indicators:** Activity diversity, user group diversity, vitality/footfall, programming quality, local business presence, event calendar, unique/memorable activities, land use mix, retail diversity.

### Quality 4: Sociability
Whether the place fosters social interaction, community building, and a sense of belonging.

| Score | Indicators to Assess |
|-------|---------------------|
| 1 | Anti-social design; no interaction; people pass through without engaging; hostile architecture |
| 2 | Limited sociability; few gathering spots; minimal interaction; no community ownership |
| 3 | Some sociability; people greet each other; informal gathering occurs; moderate community connection |
| 4 | Good sociability; people meet friends; children play; diverse groups mix; community events; neighborhood pride |
| 5 | Exceptional sociability; vibrant community hub; intergenerational interaction; cultural diversity celebrated; volunteering and stewardship; strong sense of belonging; people identify with the place |

**Indicators:** Social interaction frequency, community group presence, volunteerism, neighborly behavior, evening use, street life quality, diverse user demographics, sense of pride and belonging.

## 4. Transport for London (TfL) - Healthy Streets 10 Indicators

Source: Transport for London, "Guide to the Healthy Streets Indicators" (2017), developed by Lucy Saunders.

**Indicator 1: Pedestrians from All Walks of Life**
Everyone feels welcome and can use the street regardless of age, disability, ethnicity, or income.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Street is hostile to vulnerable users; no accessibility; excludes most non-drivers |
| 2 | Limited accessibility; narrow paths; steps without alternatives; few user types observed |
| 3 | Basic accessibility; wheelchair-passable but not comfortable; moderate user diversity |
| 4 | Good accessibility; smooth wide paths; dropped kerbs; tactile paving; diverse users observed |
| 5 | Fully inclusive; universally accessible; users of all ages, abilities, and backgrounds observed; wayfinding for visually impaired; seating for elderly; stroller-friendly |

**Indicator 2: Easy to Cross**
People can cross streets easily, directly, and safely at points they wish to.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Major barrier to crossing; multi-lane road; no formal crossings; high traffic speed |
| 2 | Difficult to cross; infrequent crossings; long waits; indirect routes; guardrails directing to distant crossings |
| 3 | Crossings available at main junctions; moderate wait times; some desire lines served |
| 4 | Frequent crossings; short waits; pedestrian-phase priority; desire lines respected; refuge islands |
| 5 | Crossing easy everywhere; pedestrian-priority design; continuous footpaths across side streets; no guardrails; at-grade crossings on desire lines |

**Indicator 3: Shade and Shelter**
Protection from sun, rain, and wind is available along the route.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No shade or shelter at all; fully exposed |
| 2 | Minimal cover; a few awnings near shops; no tree shade |
| 3 | Some shade from buildings and trees; bus shelter available; partial coverage |
| 4 | Good shade and shelter; tree canopy on most blocks; awnings; covered waiting areas |
| 5 | Comprehensive coverage; continuous tree canopy; covered walkways; wind-sheltered areas; comfortable in all weather |

**Indicator 4: Places to Stop and Rest**
Benches and informal resting spots are available at regular intervals.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No resting opportunities; no benches; no ledges; nothing to lean on |
| 2 | Very few seats; spacing >200m; no variety; uncomfortable design |
| 3 | Seats available at 100-200m intervals; basic design; some variety |
| 4 | Good seating at <100m intervals; comfortable design with backs and arms; informal resting options too |
| 5 | Abundant resting opportunities at <50m intervals; varied seating types; choice of sun/shade; informal leaning and perching; accessible for all |

**Indicator 5: Not Too Noisy**
Noise levels allow comfortable conversation and do not cause stress.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | >75 dB; impossible to converse; traffic roar; stressful noise levels |
| 2 | 70-75 dB; must shout to converse; persistent traffic noise |
| 3 | 65-70 dB; conversation possible at close range; traffic noise present but tolerable |
| 4 | 55-65 dB; comfortable conversation; low traffic noise; pleasant ambient sounds |
| 5 | <55 dB; easy quiet conversation; natural sounds audible (birds, water); acoustic delight |

**Indicator 6: People Choose to Walk, Cycle, and Use Public Transport**
The street environment encourages active and sustainable travel choices.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Car-dominated; walking and cycling feel dangerous; no transit; no one walks by choice |
| 2 | Walking possible but unpleasant; cycling dangerous; transit distant or infrequent |
| 3 | Walking adequate; cycling possible on quieter streets; transit accessible; moderate active travel mode share |
| 4 | Walking pleasant and attractive; safe cycling infrastructure; good transit; high active travel share (>40%) |
| 5 | Walking and cycling are the natural choice; dedicated infrastructure; excellent transit; >60% active/sustainable travel; car use exceptional |

**Indicator 7: People Feel Safe**
Users feel safe from crime and antisocial behavior at all times.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | People feel unsafe; avoid the area; evidence of crime or antisocial behavior; dark, hidden spaces |
| 2 | Some safety concerns; uneven lighting; quiet/empty periods; some antisocial behavior |
| 3 | Generally feels safe during daytime; some evening concerns; adequate lighting |
| 4 | Feels safe at most times; good lighting; activity into evening; well-maintained |
| 5 | Feels very safe at all hours; excellent lighting; continuous activity and surveillance; well-maintained; trusted by all user groups including women walking alone at night |

**Indicator 8: Things to See and Do**
The street provides visual interest, activity, and delight.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Nothing to see or do; blank walls; empty lots; monotonous; boring walk |
| 2 | Little interest; sparse activity; few shop windows; minimal visual stimulation |
| 3 | Some interest; occasional shops and active uses; some planting; moderate visual quality |
| 4 | Interesting walk; varied frontages; active uses; good planting; display windows; public art |
| 5 | Highly engaging; rich visual variety; street life; markets; performers; art; varied architecture; water features; seasonal interest; sense of discovery |

**Indicator 9: People Feel Relaxed**
The overall environment allows people to feel calm and relaxed rather than stressed.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Stressful environment; aggressive traffic; pollution; noise; conflict between users |
| 2 | Somewhat stressful; traffic feels close; air quality concerns; rushed feeling |
| 3 | Neutral; functional but not particularly calming; manageable stress levels |
| 4 | Relaxed atmosphere; traffic at distance; clean air; natural elements; calm environment |
| 5 | Very relaxed; green and leafy; sounds of nature; slow pace; people dawdle and linger; stress-reducing environment |

**Indicator 10: Clean Air**
Air quality is good and people are not exposed to harmful pollution.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Visibly polluted; on major traffic corridor; >40 ug/m3 NO2; above all WHO limits |
| 2 | Noticeable pollution; near busy roads; 30-40 ug/m3 NO2; above some limits |
| 3 | Moderate air quality; some traffic influence; 20-30 ug/m3 NO2; meets most standards |
| 4 | Good air quality; low traffic; 10-20 ug/m3 NO2; meets WHO guidelines; planting helps filter |
| 5 | Excellent air quality; minimal motorized traffic; <10 ug/m3 NO2; well below all limits; green infrastructure for air filtration |

## 5. CPTED - 4 Principles Assessment

Source: International CPTED Association; C. Ray Jeffery (1971); Oscar Newman (1972).

### Principle 1: Natural Surveillance
The ability to see and be seen; the "eyes on the street" concept.

| Score | Assessment Areas | Evidence |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | Building orientation, window placement, sightlines, activity levels | Buildings turn away from public space; no windows overlooking streets/parks; blind corners; entrapment spots; nobody watching |
| 2 | | Few windows facing public space; long stretches without observation; limited activity; some blind spots |
| 3 | | Moderate window coverage; most public areas visible from some buildings; activity during daytime hours |
| 4 | | Good window coverage on most streets; active uses at ground level; clear sightlines; good lighting for evening visibility |
| 5 | | Excellent surveillance from windows, balconies, and active ground uses; overlapping sight lines; no hidden areas; 18-hour activity; well-lit throughout |

**Design features to check:** Window-to-wall ratio on street-facing facades, balcony provision, ground floor use types, landscape that maintains visibility (no dense planting above 0.6m or below 2.0m), lighting uniformity ratio (<4:1), CCTV as supplement not substitute.

### Principle 2: Natural Access Control
The use of design to guide movement, define entry points, and create a sense of controlled access without fortress-like barriers.

| Score | Assessment Areas | Evidence |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | Entry definition, pathway design, spatial transitions | No defined entries; confused public/private boundaries; easy undetected access to all areas; no sense of arrival |
| 2 | | Weak entry definition; unclear spatial hierarchy; some confusion about public vs. private |
| 3 | | Adequate entry definition; basic public/private distinction; functional wayfinding |
| 4 | | Clear entry points; well-defined spatial transitions from public to semi-public to private; natural wayfinding through design |
| 5 | | Masterful spatial hierarchy; clear welcome at entries; intuitive transition zones; access control through design (not barriers); attractive and legible |

**Design features to check:** Gateway features, change in surface material at boundaries, landscape as boundary definition, porch/stoop/threshold design, pathway hierarchy (primary, secondary, private), signage and wayfinding.

### Principle 3: Territorial Reinforcement
Design that promotes a sense of ownership and encourages legitimate users to feel a sense of belonging.

| Score | Assessment Areas | Evidence |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | Ownership cues, maintenance, personalization, activity | No sense of ownership; nobody's territory; abandoned feeling; no maintenance; vandalism |
| 2 | | Weak ownership signals; limited maintenance; low community engagement; some neglect |
| 3 | | Moderate territorial signals; basic maintenance; some community use; functional spaces |
| 4 | | Good territorial signals; well-maintained; community gardens and personalization; clear semi-private spaces; pride of place |
| 5 | | Strong sense of ownership; well-loved and maintained; extensive personalization; community stewardship; front gardens tended; neighborhood pride; zero tolerance of vandalism through social control |

**Design features to check:** Semi-private front gardens, defensible space gradient, community gardens, boundary treatment quality, maintenance regime, evidence of personalization, community notice boards, art and cultural expression.

### Principle 4: Maintenance and Management
The quality of ongoing maintenance and management that signals the space is cared for ("Broken Windows" theory prevention).

| Score | Assessment Areas | Evidence |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | Cleanliness, repair, graffiti, vandalism, landscape maintenance | Severely neglected; broken fixtures; graffiti; litter; overgrown; abandoned feel |
| 2 | | Poor maintenance; delayed repairs; some graffiti/litter; patchy landscape |
| 3 | | Adequate maintenance; reasonably clean; minor issues addressed periodically |
| 4 | | Well-maintained; clean; rapid repair response; well-tended landscape; no vandalism |
| 5 | | Immaculately maintained; rapid response systems; community involvement in upkeep; landscape in excellent condition; zero tolerance for deterioration |

**Design features to check:** Material durability, ease of maintenance (can surfaces be cleaned/repaired easily?), landscape management plan, lighting maintenance, vandal-resistant fixtures, management organization presence.

## 6. Universal Design - 7 Principles Adapted for Urban Scale

Source: Centre for Universal Design, North Carolina State University (1997). Adapted for urban design and public space application.

### Principle 1: Equitable Use
The design is useful and appealing to people with diverse abilities.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Excludes many users; stairs without ramps; no accessible routes; hostile to wheelchair users, elderly, or visually impaired |
| 2 | Minimal compliance; ramp exists but is hidden or stigmatizing; accessible route is the "back door" experience |
| 3 | Meets minimum accessibility code; accessible routes provided; basic compliance achieved |
| 4 | Inclusive design; accessible routes are the primary routes; equivalent experience for all users; dignity maintained |
| 5 | Fully equitable; all paths and spaces accessible; no "special" routes needed; design is inherently inclusive; appeals equally to all users |

### Principle 2: Flexibility in Use
The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Single way to use each space; no choice; rigid design |
| 2 | Limited flexibility; spaces serve one primary function; minimal adaptability |
| 3 | Moderate flexibility; some spaces serve multiple purposes; basic choice available |
| 4 | Good flexibility; spaces accommodate different use patterns; left and right-hand access; varying pace; time of day |
| 5 | Maximum flexibility; all spaces support multiple uses; adaptable furniture; adjustable features; user can customize experience |

### Principle 3: Simple and Intuitive Use
The design is easy to understand regardless of experience, knowledge, language, or cognitive ability.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Confusing layout; complex wayfinding; signage in single language only; disorienting |
| 2 | Somewhat confusing; excessive signage needed; navigation requires familiarity |
| 3 | Understandable with some effort; functional wayfinding; multilingual signage at key points |
| 4 | Intuitive layout; clear wayfinding; pictographic and multilingual signage; logical spatial sequence |
| 5 | Immediately understandable by anyone; intuitive navigation without signage; universal symbols; logical sequence; no instruction needed |

### Principle 4: Perceptible Information
The design communicates necessary information effectively regardless of ambient conditions or user's sensory abilities.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | No information provision; no tactile cues; no auditory signals; visually impaired users cannot navigate |
| 2 | Minimal information; visual-only signage; no redundant cues |
| 3 | Basic information provision; some tactile paving; standard signage; moderate contrast |
| 4 | Good multi-modal information; tactile paving at crossings; audible signals; high-contrast wayfinding; clear maps |
| 5 | Excellent multi-modal information; tactile, auditory, and visual information at all decision points; talking signs; Braille; high contrast throughout; digital wayfinding integration |

### Principle 5: Tolerance for Error
The design minimizes hazards and adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Dangerous design; unprotected edges; trip hazards; no fall protection; disorienting layout that leads to hazards |
| 2 | Some hazards present; inconsistent edge protection; some trip risks; unclear danger zones |
| 3 | Basic safety; main hazards addressed; standard railings and edge protection |
| 4 | Good safety design; clear hazard marking; protective barriers; forgiving surfaces; fail-safe elements |
| 5 | Maximally forgiving design; no unprotected edges; slip-resistant surfaces; clear hazard communication; layout naturally prevents dangerous situations; child-safe |

### Principle 6: Low Physical Effort
The design can be used efficiently and comfortably with minimal fatigue.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | High physical effort required; steep gradients; long distances without rest; heavy doors; exhausting environment |
| 2 | Significant effort; some steep sections; limited rest opportunities; tiring routes |
| 3 | Moderate effort; gradients within code limits; rest opportunities every 100-200m |
| 4 | Low effort; gentle gradients (<5%); regular rest stops every 50-100m; smooth surfaces; automatic doors where appropriate |
| 5 | Minimal effort; level or very gentle routes; frequent rest opportunities; moving walkways where appropriate; effortless navigation; fatigue-free experience |

### Principle 7: Size and Space for Approach and Use
Appropriate size and space is provided for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of user's body size, posture, or mobility.

| Score | Urban Application |
|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Inadequate space; narrow passages (<1.2m); no wheelchair turning space; cramped; impassable for mobility devices |
| 2 | Tight spaces; minimum widths frequently not met; difficult for two wheelchairs to pass; restricted turning |
| 3 | Minimum standards met; 1.5m path widths; turning space at key points; basic compliance |
| 4 | Generous space; >2.0m paths; ample turning space; sight lines for seated users; accessible counters and interfaces |
| 5 | Very generous space; >2.5m paths; abundant turning and passing room; all interfaces accessible from seated and standing positions; clear floor areas at all features |

## 7. Biophilic Design - 14 Patterns Adapted for Outdoor Urban Spaces

Source: Terrapin Bright Green, "14 Patterns of Biophilic Design" (2014); Stephen R. Kellert, "Biophilic Design" (2008). Adapted for urban outdoor application.

### Nature in the Space Patterns (Patterns 1-7)

**Pattern 1: Visual Connection with Nature**
Views to natural elements (plants, water, animals, landscapes) from occupied spaces.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No nature visible; fully hardscaped; no planting; no water; no views to natural landscape |
| 2 | Minimal nature; sparse ornamental planting; no water feature; limited green views |
| 3 | Moderate nature presence; street trees; planted areas; some green views available |
| 4 | Good nature presence; abundant planting; water features; views to parks or landscape from most points |
| 5 | Rich visual nature connection; layered planting; water features; distant landscape views; biodiversity visible; nature as organizing design element |

**Pattern 2: Non-Visual Connection with Nature**
Sounds, scents, textures, and tactile engagement with nature.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No non-visual nature contact; sealed, smooth, artificial surfaces only; traffic sounds mask all nature |
| 2 | Minimal; occasional bird sounds; no tactile nature contact |
| 3 | Some non-visual connection; birdsong audible; natural stone or timber surfaces present |
| 4 | Good multi-sensory nature; water sounds; fragrant planting; natural material textures; birdsong prominent |
| 5 | Rich multi-sensory nature experience; water sounds; fragrant gardens; barefoot paths; tactile natural surfaces; wind through leaves; seasonal scents |

**Pattern 3: Non-Rhythmic Sensory Stimuli**
Naturally occurring, unpredictable sensory moments that draw attention briefly.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No natural variability; static, sterile environment; fully controlled |
| 2 | Minimal; some tree movement in wind; little else |
| 3 | Moderate; tree canopy movement; occasional wildlife; some dappled light |
| 4 | Good variety; dancing water; moving shadows; butterflies; birdsong variety; cloud reflections |
| 5 | Rich unpredictable stimuli; water play; diverse wildlife; wind effects; dappled sunlight through varied canopy; rustling grasses; sensory surprise |

**Pattern 4: Thermal and Airflow Variability**
Variations in temperature and air movement that connect to natural conditions.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Uniform microclimate; no variability; fully exposed or fully enclosed |
| 2 | Limited variability; basic sun/shade difference only |
| 3 | Some variability; sun and shade zones; occasional breeze channels |
| 4 | Good thermal variety; cool shaded areas and warm sunny spots; natural ventilation corridors; seasonal variation |
| 5 | Rich thermal landscape; sun-traps, shade groves, mist features, breezy corridors, sheltered pockets; seasonal and daily variation designed in |

**Pattern 5: Presence of Water**
Seeing, hearing, or touching water.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No water presence whatsoever |
| 2 | Hidden infrastructure; water present but not visible or accessible |
| 3 | Basic water feature present; fountain or small pool; limited interaction |
| 4 | Good water presence; interactive fountain; stream; reflecting pool; rain gardens visible |
| 5 | Rich water presence; multiple water types (flowing, still, mist); interactive play; rainwater celebrated; natural water body connection; water as design generator |

**Pattern 6: Dynamic and Diffuse Light**
Variations in light intensity, shadow, and color that change over time.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Harsh, uniform lighting or deep permanent shadow; no light variation |
| 2 | Limited light quality; monotonous; minimal shadow play |
| 3 | Some light variation; dappled shade from trees; basic lighting design |
| 4 | Good light quality; varied shade patterns; warm evening lighting; seasonal light change appreciated |
| 5 | Exceptional light quality; dappled canopy light; dramatic shadow patterns; warm layered evening lighting; dawn/dusk effects; light as design material |

**Pattern 7: Connection with Natural Systems**
Awareness of ecological processes such as seasons, weather, animal behavior, and growth.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No connection to natural systems; fully artificial; seasons indistinguishable |
| 2 | Minimal; some deciduous trees indicating seasons |
| 3 | Moderate; seasonal planting; weather visible; some ecological awareness |
| 4 | Good; clear seasonal cycle (blossom, fruit, autumn color); wildlife habitat visible; weather patterns engaged |
| 5 | Deep connection; food growing; composting visible; pollinator gardens; seasonal celebrations; ecological succession legible; climate patterns engaged with design |

### Natural Analogues Patterns (Patterns 8-10)

**Pattern 8: Biomorphic Forms and Patterns**
Use of organic shapes, forms, and patterns in design.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | All rectilinear, industrial forms; no organic reference |
| 2 | Minimal organic forms; standard orthogonal design throughout |
| 3 | Some organic forms in furniture, paving patterns, or planting beds |
| 4 | Good integration of organic forms; curved paths; natural paving patterns; biomorphic furniture; fractal-inspired layouts |
| 5 | Rich biomorphic design language; fractal geometry; organic curves throughout; nature-inspired structures; biomimetic details |

**Pattern 9: Material Connection with Nature**
Use of natural materials that reflect local ecology and geology.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Only synthetic, industrial materials; concrete, plastic, steel; no natural materials |
| 2 | Minimal natural materials; occasional timber bench; largely synthetic |
| 3 | Some natural materials; local stone or timber used in key features; moderate natural palette |
| 4 | Good natural material use; local stone paving; timber structures; natural palette dominant; materials age beautifully |
| 5 | Rich natural material palette; locally sourced stone, timber, planting; materials tell geological story; patina celebrated; cradle-to-cradle material choices |

**Pattern 10: Complexity and Order**
Rich sensory information adhering to a spatial hierarchy, similar to natural environments.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Either chaotic (overwhelming complexity) or sterile (no complexity); no underlying order |
| 2 | Unbalanced; either too monotonous or too chaotic; weak spatial hierarchy |
| 3 | Basic complexity with underlying order; functional spatial hierarchy; moderate detail |
| 4 | Good balance; rich detail within clear spatial structure; fractal-like nested scales; legible hierarchy |
| 5 | Masterful complexity and order; rich detail at every scale; coherent spatial hierarchy; nature-like balance of variety and pattern; deeply satisfying spatial experience |

### Nature of the Space Patterns (Patterns 11-14)

**Pattern 11: Prospect**
Long-distance views; ability to survey the surroundings; open views.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No prospect; enclosed by walls; no views out; claustrophobic |
| 2 | Limited prospect; restricted views; enclosed feeling dominant |
| 3 | Moderate prospect; some views available; occasional opening |
| 4 | Good prospect; clear views across public spaces; vista to landmarks or landscape; elevated viewing points |
| 5 | Excellent prospect; panoramic views available; elevated vantage points; views to horizon, sky, and landscape; sense of freedom and overview |

**Pattern 12: Refuge**
Enclosed, protected spaces for withdrawal and retreat.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No refuge; fully exposed; nowhere to retreat; no protected spaces |
| 2 | Minimal refuge; limited sheltered spots; few intimate spaces |
| 3 | Some refuge opportunities; bus shelters; covered walkways; a few alcoves |
| 4 | Good refuge options; covered seating areas; intimate garden rooms; sheltered courtyards; under-canopy spaces |
| 5 | Rich refuge opportunities; varied enclosure degrees; alcoves, arbors, covered terraces, walled gardens; choose your level of exposure or protection |

**Pattern 13: Mystery**
The promise of more information around a corner or beyond a threshold; sense of discovery.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Everything visible at once; no spatial surprise; no anticipation; no discovery |
| 2 | Minimal mystery; predictable sequence; little reward for exploration |
| 3 | Some mystery; a few turns and reveals; moderate spatial sequence |
| 4 | Good mystery; interesting spatial sequence; reveals and surprises; compelling exploration paths |
| 5 | Rich mystery; irresistible spatial sequences; partial views drawing exploration; hidden gardens; threshold moments; sense of discovery and delight throughout |

**Pattern 14: Risk/Peril**
Identifiable threat paired with a reliable safeguard; controlled excitement.

| Score | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Either genuinely dangerous (uncontrolled risk) or completely sanitized (no stimulation); neither is biophilic |
| 2 | Minimal controlled risk; entirely predictable; no excitement |
| 3 | Some controlled risk elements; height variation; water edge with safe barrier; adventure play |
| 4 | Good controlled risk; lookout points with barriers; challenging play equipment; water interaction; height experiences |
| 5 | Excellent controlled risk/thrill; elevated walkways; boulder scrambles; water play with depth; tall climbing structures; zip lines; calculated adventure - all with reliable safeguards |
