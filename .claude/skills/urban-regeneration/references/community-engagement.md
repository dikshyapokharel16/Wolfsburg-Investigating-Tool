# Community Engagement in Urban Regeneration Reference

## Stakeholder Mapping

### Stakeholder Categories

| Category | Typical Stakeholders | Interest | Influence |
|----------|---------------------|----------|-----------|
| **Residents** | Existing tenants, homeowners, leaseholders | Very High | Medium (but politically powerful) |
| **Local businesses** | Shop owners, market traders, service providers | High | Medium |
| **Landowners** | Freeholders, institutional owners, absent landlords | High | High |
| **Developers** | Potential investors, house builders, commercial developers | High | High |
| **Local government** | Planning department, housing, transport, parks, education | Very High | Very High |
| **Elected officials** | Councilors, mayor, MPs/representatives | High | Very High |
| **Community organizations** | Residents' associations, faith groups, charities, sports clubs | High | Medium-High |
| **Statutory bodies** | Heritage agencies, environment agencies, transport authorities | Medium | High |
| **Infrastructure providers** | Utilities, transit operators, telecom | Medium | Medium-High |
| **Education / health** | Schools, universities, hospitals, GP practices | Medium | Medium |
| **Adjacent communities** | Neighboring residents, businesses affected by change | Medium | Medium |
| **Future residents** | People who will live/work in the regenerated area | High | None (unidentifiable) |

### Influence-Interest Matrix

```
         HIGH INFLUENCE
              |
   MANAGE     |    ENGAGE
   CLOSELY    |    CLOSELY
              |
  ------------|------------
              |
   MONITOR    |    KEEP
   (minimal)  |    INFORMED
              |
         LOW INFLUENCE

       LOW INTEREST    HIGH INTEREST
```

**Engagement level by quadrant:**
- **Engage closely** (high interest + high influence): Residents, local government, key landowners. Co-design, regular bilateral meetings.
- **Manage closely** (low interest + high influence): Utilities, statutory bodies. Technical working groups, formal consultation.
- **Keep informed** (high interest + low influence): Community groups, local businesses. Newsletters, public exhibitions, workshops.
- **Monitor** (low interest + low influence): General public, media. Website, press releases.

### Design Charrette Protocol

The charrette is the gold standard for community-engaged design:

**Pre-Charrette (4-6 weeks before):**
1. Stakeholder mapping and individual briefings
2. Technical base information assembled (site surveys, traffic data, market analysis)
3. Community survey (online + door-to-door) to identify priorities
4. Logistics (venue, catering, materials, childcare, translation, accessibility)
5. Publicity (flyers, social media, community radio, faith networks)

**Charrette (typically 4-5 days):**

| Day | Activity | Output |
|-----|----------|--------|
| Day 1 (PM) | Opening presentation + public workshop: issues, priorities, vision | Community priorities list; base constraints map |
| Day 2 (all day) | Design team works on options; lunchtime pin-up for feedback; focus group sessions (youth, elderly, businesses) | 3-4 concept options |
| Day 3 (all day) | Refine to 2 options based on Day 2 feedback; technical testing (density, transport, cost); midday presentation + feedback | 2 refined options |
| Day 4 (all day) | Merge into preferred option; detail key areas; prepare presentation | Draft preferred plan |
| Day 5 (PM) | Public presentation of preferred plan; Q&A; written comments collected | Endorsed preferred plan |

**Post-Charrette (2-4 weeks after):**
1. Compile all feedback and comments
2. Refine plan based on written submissions
3. Publish charrette report (full record of process and decisions)
4. Present to local authority for formal response
5. Establish ongoing community liaison group

### Community Survey Design

**Core questions for regeneration projects:**

1. How long have you lived/worked in this area? (establishes rootedness)
2. What do you like most about this area? (identifies assets to protect)
3. What are the biggest problems in this area? (identifies priorities)
4. What 3 things would most improve this area? (guides investment)
5. Do you feel safe in this area? When / where don't you? (CPTED input)
6. Where do you go for daily shopping, school, healthcare, recreation? (catchment mapping)
7. How do you usually travel around the area? (transport baseline)
8. Would you support new housing in this area? What type? (housing demand)
9. Would you support new businesses / jobs in this area? What type? (economic vision)
10. Are you concerned about any of these: rising costs, displacement, crime, traffic, loss of character? (risk perception)
11. How would you like to be involved in future plans? (engagement preference)

**Survey standards:**
- Minimum response rate: 20% of households for statistical validity
- Offer multiple channels: paper, online, telephone, in-person
- Translate into community languages (top 3 at minimum)
- Include large-print and easy-read versions
- Provide incentive (prize draw) for completion
- Process results within 4 weeks; publish summary within 6 weeks

## Gentrification Monitoring Framework

### Early Warning Indicators

Monitor these metrics quarterly once regeneration begins:

| Indicator | Data Source | Warning Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Median rent (residential) | Letting agent data | > 5% annual increase above city average |
| Median sale price | Land registry / transaction data | > 10% annual increase above city average |
| Investor purchases (% of transactions) | Land registry | > 30% of sales to non-resident buyers |
| Eviction notices | Court records / tenant advice services | Any increase from baseline |
| Long-term resident turnover | Council tax / utility records | > 15% annual turnover |
| Local business closure rate | Business rates register | > 10% of businesses closing per year |
| Chain store ratio | Frontage survey | Chains replacing independents |
| Demographic shift (income) | Census / survey | Median income rising faster than employment growth |
| Demographic shift (ethnicity) | Census / survey | Significant change from baseline composition |
| Planning applications (type) | Planning register | Shift from family housing to luxury apartments |
| Cultural venue change | Business register | Independent cultural venues closing or relocating |

### Response Measures

When warning thresholds are triggered:

| Trigger Level | Response |
|---|---|
| **1 indicator** | Note and monitor; no action yet |
| **2-3 indicators** | Convene community advisory group; review affordable housing pipeline |
| **4-5 indicators** | Activate anti-displacement tools (Section 5.4 in SKILL.md); increase affordable housing requirement |
| **6+ indicators** | Emergency response: rent stabilization, community right of first refusal, restrict buy-to-let, dedicated displacement prevention fund |

## Community Benefit Agreement (CBA) Template

### Standard CBA Provisions

| Provision | Typical Commitment | Enforcement |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Local hiring** | 30-50% of construction jobs to local residents | Monthly reporting, financial penalty |
| **Apprenticeships** | 1 apprentice per $2-5M construction value | Quarterly reporting |
| **Living wage** | All on-site workers paid living wage or above | Payroll audit |
| **Affordable housing** | 20-40% of units at below-market rates | Planning condition, covenant |
| **Affordable commercial** | 10-20% of commercial space at below-market rent | Lease condition, 10-15 year term |
| **Community facility** | Specified facility (community center, park, playground) | Planning condition, delivery trigger |
| **Local procurement** | 20-30% of construction materials/services from local suppliers | Procurement reporting |
| **Environmental standards** | Minimum LEED Gold or equivalent | Planning condition |
| **Community fund** | $X per unit or per m2 into community-managed fund | Financial contribution, phased |
| **Ongoing liaison** | Quarterly community meetings during construction | CBA condition |
| **Monitoring** | Annual report on all CBA provisions for 5-10 years | Independent monitor |

### CBA Negotiation Process

1. **Community organizes** — Form a coalition representing diverse community interests
2. **Developer engages** — Acknowledge community as negotiating partner (not just consultee)
3. **Impact assessment** — Independent analysis of development impacts (positive and negative)
4. **Wish list** — Community identifies top priorities from CBA provisions
5. **Negotiation** — Bilateral negotiation with independent mediator if needed
6. **Drafting** — Legal agreement signed by developer and community coalition
7. **Enforcement mechanism** — Specify who monitors, how breaches are addressed, what penalties apply
8. **Review clause** — CBA reviewed at each development phase; adjusted if circumstances change

## Participatory Budgeting

### Process

| Step | Duration | Activity |
|------|----------|----------|
| 1. Budget allocation | 2 weeks | Authority allocates $X to community-directed spending |
| 2. Idea generation | 4-6 weeks | Community submits project ideas (online + in-person) |
| 3. Technical review | 2-4 weeks | Officers assess feasibility, cost, and deliverability |
| 4. Shortlisting | 1-2 weeks | Community panel shortlists viable projects |
| 5. Public vote | 1-2 weeks | All residents vote (online + physical ballot) |
| 6. Results | 1 week | Projects ranked by votes; funded in order until budget exhausted |
| 7. Delivery | 6-18 months | Authority delivers winning projects with community oversight |
| 8. Evaluation | Post-delivery | Community assesses outcomes; feeds into next cycle |

**Budget scale guidance:**
- Neighborhood level: $50K-500K per cycle
- District level: $500K-5M per cycle
- City level: $5M-50M per cycle

**Typical eligible projects:** Park improvements, street lighting, community gardens,
play areas, public art, street furniture, tree planting, event programming, safety measures.

## Social Impact Assessment

### Metrics for Regeneration Social Impact

| Domain | Indicator | Measurement |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Housing** | Affordable units delivered | Count and % of total |
| **Housing** | Existing residents remaining | % still resident after 5, 10 years |
| **Employment** | Local jobs created (construction) | FTE, local hire % |
| **Employment** | Local jobs created (permanent) | FTE, salary levels |
| **Employment** | Training and apprenticeships | Completions |
| **Health** | Access to green space | % within 400m |
| **Health** | Active travel uptake | Walk/cycle mode share |
| **Health** | Air quality improvement | PM2.5/NO2 reduction |
| **Education** | School places delivered | Count vs. demand |
| **Education** | Educational attainment | Local vs. city average |
| **Safety** | Crime rate change | Incidents per 1,000 residents |
| **Safety** | Perceived safety | Survey (% feeling safe) |
| **Community** | Community facility provision | m2 per 1,000 population |
| **Community** | Social cohesion | Neighborliness survey |
| **Culture** | Cultural venue / event provision | Venues + events per year |
| **Wellbeing** | Resident satisfaction | Annual survey score |
| **Environment** | Carbon emissions per capita | tCO2e reduction |
| **Economy** | Business starts vs. closures | Net business growth |
| **Economy** | Property value uplift | % increase vs. city average |
| **Equity** | Gini coefficient change | Income inequality trend |
